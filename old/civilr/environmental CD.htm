Environmental Activism in Seattle’s Central District, 
 1967-1971

by Christy Avery

          Environmentalism became a mass movement in the United States in the decades following World War II. Historians have documented the struggles of white, middle and upper-class Americans to establish wilderness areas, protect national parks from development, and limit the use of toxic chemicals. However, the work of urban African-Americans and other minority groups to improve their environments has received less attention. Residents of Seattle’s racially diverse Central District in the late 1960s and early 1970s did work toward environmental protection – not for the creation of distant wilderness areas, but for clean air, public transit, and parks and recreation areas in the city. Central District community activists clearly believed in a correlation between clean, healthy, and attractive neighborhoods and a stable, prosperous society. Through their environmental activism in two spheres – neighborhood improvement and transportation – Central District residents worked toward realizing a better environment and an ideal community.

     The Central Seattle Community Council (CSCC), an alliance of smaller neighborhood councils in Seattle's Central District, led these urban environmental battles. The CSCC formed in 1967 from the merger of the Jackson Street Community Council and the Central Area Community Council. The Jackson Street council, founded in 1946, had attracted national attention for its efforts to improve public health and the environment in the Asian enclave known as the International District. The Central Area council, formed in 1962, brought together a number of smaller community organizations that represented Seattle’s predominantly African-American neighborhoods. The two councils joined forces after urban blight, formerly confined to the International District, spread into central Seattle. The merger combined the organizational expertise of the Jackson Street group with the broad reach of the Central Area council, and African-Americans made up a significant portion of the membership and leadership of the group.[1]

     The newly formed council stressed community involvement and a self-help approach to a variety of Central District concerns, such as housing, education, and, most importantly for the purposes of this study, land use and the environment. In the mid twentieth-century, African-American communities around the nation were subjected to programs and policies, such as urban renewal and redlining, that negatively affected the social and physical landscape of their neighborhoods. The CSCC espoused “the basic democratic belief in the ability of people to govern themselves,” but they recognized that many neighborhood residents did not understand how to obtain city services or lobby for a better urban environment. Through the council, they hoped to bring disparate elements of the community together “for the mutual consideration of all matters affecting the welfare of the people of this district.”[2] The CSCC hoped to organize and educate community members so that  residents would gain a voice in the land use, planning, and environmental decisions that affected their community. Toward this end, two areas of concern dominated the CSCC’s environmental agenda during the 1960s and 1970s:  neighborhood improvement and anti-freeway campaigns.

Neighborhood Improvement

CSCC members believed that neighborhood improvement, which included aesthetic concerns, public health, and the creation of parks and recreation spaces, could combat not only urban blight, but also the social problems that accompanied it. “To combat blight,” councilmember Lillian Ashworth asserted, “we need aesthetics, a sense of design, or, in a simple word-soul.” The group hoped that through the development of attractive neighborhoods, parks, and recreational facilities, Central District residents would enjoy “the good life,” just as white Seattleites did. One CSCC report quoted famed architect Benjamin Thompson’s 1966 essay, “Visual Squalor and Social Disorder.”  “Environment both affects and teaches,” Thompson wrote. “… It is a formative experience second only to family relationships.” To the Central District community group, an attractive, clean, and healthy physical environment with unpolluted air, parks, and recreational spaces was central to solving the social problems of the neighborhood.[3]

      In October 1970, the CSCC formed a land use committee to deal specifically with its surrounding environment. The committee was initially co-chaired by Flo Ware, a well-known Central District resident who had been active in civil rights causes in Seattle since the early 1950s. Members of the CSCC’s Land Use Committee believed that “Planning is environmental.” Consequently, they incorporated urban planning and design concerns into their environmental agenda. Committee members recognized that “many forces have combined to put neighborhoods in jeopardy,” but they believed that community activism could shape the builtenvironment and foster a higher standard of living. Seeking “an improved quality of life for area residents” through physical change, the committee designated nine categories of concern. The aesthetics and pollution subcommittee worked toward neighborhood beautification (such as the installation of fountains and art), planting street trees, and creating “green strips” – small patches of public space, planted with grass or trees. It pressured developers and city officials to construct new developments, such as a proposed low-income housing project, that were of higher quality and more aesthetically pleasing. The subcommittee also focused on ecology (which was not specifically defined), air, noise, and visual pollution, and garbage and recycling services. In addition, it proposed that the CSCC oppose billboards and lobby the city for underground wiring. The transportation subcommittee similarly sought to improve the neighborhood’s environment by installing street lighting, paving alleys, and building bike paths and safer, wider sidewalks.[4]

     The council also  believed that the quality of the surrounding environment impacted the health and well being of the Central District’s younger residents. As such, the CSCC made the creation of playgrounds and recreational spaces a priority. “Facilities for recreation, both indoors and outdoors, are of vital importance for a community,” stated one CSCC publication. The recreation subcommittee worked to create gardens, parks, playgrounds, pools, and neighborhood centers. The group inventoried all existing recreational facilities in the district and determined what types of recreational programs were available to its residents. It lobbied the Seattle Parks Department for money for local projects and programs and monitored park projects that were located in other, less racially diverse, areas of the city. For instance, when the city proposed turning Fort Lawton, an army post located in the mostly white, affluent neighborhood of Magnolia, into a park, the CSCC urged its members to write the city to find out how much money would be spent on the proposed park and what portion of this would be taken away from Central District programs.[5] 

      The CSCC’s efforts to expand recreational opportunities for young Central District residents inspired the formation of at least one new community organization. Concerned residents in the Harrison neighborhood approached the CSCC with a request for more play areas in their neighborhood. The CSCC helped them organize the Harrison Community Council and then facilitated an alliance between the new group and affluent white residents of the nearby Denny-Blaine neighborhood for the purposes of constructing a playground.[6]

     In the relatively crowded Central District, recreational spaces were a scarce commodity, and activists sometimes proposed unique solutions to this problem. In one example, the CSCC identified a vacant, two block wide area underneath Interstate 5, between Jackson and King Streets and Eighth and Tenth Streets, that it believed should  be developed for “passive and active recreation.” The group proposed lighting, paving, and landscaping the space for a variety of community activities, including a flea market, outdoor concerts and dances, hobby shows, and roller skating. The CSCC plan was challenged by the Yellow Cab Company, which wanted to utilize the area for cab parking. For their part, neighboring businesses favored metered parking in the space. The CSCC contested the cab company’s application, countering that “Our plan would provide some greatly needed beautification and open space for the neighborhood.” It justified its  plan by maintaining that it was for “the maximum benefit of the community.” The city favored the CSCC’s  proposal, but competing visions and a lack of funding hampered the plan’s success, and it is unclear if any public events ever occurred under the freeway.[7] However, the incident does show the creative ways in which the CSCC sought to claim public space. While many city residents would not have found an area below the interstate an appealing spot for community events, the lack of public space in the Central District forced activists to work imaginatively to discover potential gathering locations.

     Central District neighborhood improvement councils that were affiliated with the CSCC also undertook small projects to ensure the area’s attractiveness and improve the surrounding environment. The Leschi Improvement Council, for example, lobbied the city to maintain lots that were undeveloped and overgrown. Central District residents worked hard to create attractive, healthy spaces in their communities through clean-up campaigns as well. Community activists removed six tons of trash during one clean-up day in Leschi. On another occasion, the CSCC organized a “Plant-In” on an empty lot belonging to the highway department. Volunteers weeded the tract and planted vegetables and ornamental plants along busy Empire Way (now called Martin Luther King Jr. Way). Local businesses donated seeds and equipment; once harvested, the vegetables were given to low-income families.[8]

     The CSCC also supported city-wide political initiatives that promised benefits for the Central District. The group endorsed the Forward Thrust bond issue of 1968 in the hopes that the neighborhood would benefit from the community centers, parks, low-income housing, and mass transit proposed by the measure. The levy included money for a number of Central District projects, such as parks, playgrounds, play fields, and construction of a swimming pool at Garfield High School. Not everyone in the community supported the plan, however. The Afro-American Journal urged its readers to vote “no” on the bond issue, believing that black taxpayers would be financing projects designed to benefit white neighborhoods. King County residents voted on the twelve Forward Thrust bond measures separately and many of the issues supported by the CSCC failed. Voters rejected the low-income housing levy as well as the community center proposal. However, to the delight of the CSCC, the parks and neighborhood improvement bonds passed.[9] By 1970, a number of Forward Thrust-funded parks and recreation projects were underway in the Central District.

Anti-Freeway Campaigns

            Anti-freeway campaigns constituted the second category of environmental activism undertaken by the CSCC. In cities across the nation during the 1960s and  1970s, urban residents fought highway projects that threatened to tear through their communities. Largely funded by federal dollars after the passage of the Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956, these projects usually benefited suburban commuters at the expense of in-city and minority neighborhoods.[10] Anti-highway activists often focused on the loss of housing and the spread of blight that resulted from road projects. In the Central District, opposition to the projects focused not only on  displacement of residents, but on aesthetics, human health, and pollution.

     Led by the CSCC, Central District residents fought two large road projects in the late 1960s and early 1970s – the building of a third Lake Washington bridge and the construction of the R.H. Thompson expressway. State highway engineers saw the Central District as a conduit for suburban traffic to the downtown business district, and they planned the proposed highways in order to funnel cars from communities east of Lake Washington to downtown Seattle. Central District residents argued that the roadways would cause noise and air pollution, create an unsightly physical barrier that would divide the neighborhood, and divert resources that could be used for public transportation. Area residents owned fewer cars than the general population (about thirty-nine percent of the neighborhood’s residents did not own an automobile), and in their eyes, city and state officials planned to sacrifice their neighborhood for the convenience of Seattle-area commuters.[11]

     The successful grassroots opposition to the Thompson expressway is a popular topic in Seattle history. The route of the proposed freeway ran from the northern suburbs of Bothell and Lake Forest Park, south through the white, middle-class neighborhoods of Ravenna and Montlake, and culminated in a massive clover leaf interchange at I-90 in the Central District. Many Seattleites recall with pride the way that community activists successfully halted the highway project, which would have negatively impacted the Washington Park Arboretum, the shoreline of Lake Washington, and the quiet, attractive neighborhoods that lay along the route. What is usually lost in this story is the work of Seattle’s African-American community.

     The CSCC’s arguments against the highway focused on the environmental and social consequences of the roadway. According to the council , freeways “seriously affect the character of our neighborhoods they pass through, in ways that make urban living undesirable.” The group protested that freeways brought noise and air pollution, destroyed open space, and imposed artificial divisions within neighborhoods, and it lobbied the city to consider mass transit options. Aesthetic concerns remained primary.  In particular, the council lobbied for an underground rail so that the community would remain free from the sight of rail lines or electric bus wires. The group encouraged Central District residents to attend hearings and write letters to the city and state protesting the proposal.[12]

     White anti-freeway activists viewed African-American residents of the Central District as necessary allies in their struggle. Citizens Against the R.H. Thompson Freeway (CARHT), a group that actively opposed the construction of the highway, drew members largely from the white neighborhoods that lay along the freeway’s proposed path. However, they recognized that by courting Central District activists, they could maximize their lobbying power. CARHT leaders expressly stated that they needed the support of the Black community to fight the project, and they met with CSCC leaders to ask how to involve more African-American residents in the opposition campaign.[13] Largely due to these anti-highway activists, the R.H. Thompson freeway was never built. Mayor Wes Uhlman and the city council rescinded their support for the project in 1972 and a city-wide referendum killed the project later that year.

     While African-American and white Seattleites united to fight the R.H. Thompson expressway, CSCC activists had less support, and less success, in their fight against the expansion of Interstate 90. Highway planners proposed the addition of eight lanes to the interstate, which would widen the roadway to twelve lanes through the Central District. The expansion would also add another bridge across Lake Washington as it facilitated vehicle traffic from Seattle’s eastern suburbs to the downtown shopping and business district. Planners proposed removing eighty-seven blocks of the neighborhood to make room for the roadway, displacing 3,000 residents in the process. 

     The CSCC lobbied the city and state to prevent the expansion, citing the air and noise pollution, the visual impact, and the “possible psychological effect” of building a massive roadway through the Central District. The group also urged the city to undertake “meaningful relocation programs” that would assure displaced low-income African-Americans of decent housing. The CSCC worked to involve community members in the fight against the interstate and held a number of meetings (sometimes co-sponsored by the Urban League) to organize neighborhood opposition to the project.[14]

     Area activists expressed particular concern with the air pollution that would result from the additional traffic. The dangers of leaded gasoline were just beginning to be publicized, and studies showed that children who lived near freeways or busy roads were at risk of brain and nervous system damage. In 1971, CSCC president Roger Leed urged Mayor Wes Uhlman to refuse to consider the highway department’s plan for I-90 until air and noise pollution control studies could be completed. The group argued that the project ran too close to Colman Elementary, where young children would be subjected to harmful pollutants.  The council maintained that the interstate was responsible for seventy-five percent of the neighborhood’s noise and air pollution and that an expansion would disproportionately affect Central District residents. The American dream of auto mobility, they argued, had turned into a “nightmare. The air is filled with poisons.” The CSCC wanted to force transportation planners to consider human health, education, and economic growth for all neighborhoods, rather than just the suburbs and downtown.[15]

     Lobbying for clean, efficient mass transit systems was key to its campaign. The group believed that America’s love affair with the automobile had resulted in polluted environments and prevented the development of cleaner, cheaper forms of urban transportation. It also saw a connection between transportation and racial justice. As one CSCC statement read, “Up with people and down, for a change, with the internal combustion engine ... Cars have forced a segment of the population which cannot afford automobiles to take second best in employment, health services, living facilities and shopping.” The council lobbied the state legislature to repeal the eighteenth amendment of Washington’s constitution, which directed all fees and taxes collected on motor vehicles and gasoline toward highway construction and maintenance. The group believed that the amendment threatened to destroy the social and financial prosperity of central Seattle, since it discouraged “cleaner and cheaper” forms of transportation. The lack of mass transit in the neighborhood, according to the council, relegated African-Americans to substandard jobs, housing, and health care.[16]

     Just as the CSCC supported the Forward Thrust bond measures that promised parks, community centers, and housing, the group urged the passage of the mass transit bond. The centerpiece of the 1968 Forward Thrust bond issue was a $385,000,000 measure to fund a mass transit system that included rapid rail as well as increased express and local bus service. During the planning process, the Forward Thrust transit committee actively solicited involvement from Central District citizens. The CSCC encouraged the Forward Thrust committee to focus on clean, quiet types of mass transit, such as electric buses, which reduced noise and air pollution. Ruth Brandwein, chairwoman of the CSCC’s environment subcommittee, expressed optimism that “well placed and well operated” public transit would help minority job seekers, students, and young workers. She believed it would also prove helpful for Central District residents seeking recreational opportunities, since there was no direct bus route from the neighborhood to the beaches of Lake Washington. Once again, aesthetic concerns took a high priority. “Citizens of the Central Area, as people anywhere, do not want to see, hear, smell or walk in the shadow of ugliness,” Brandwein wrote.[17] However, the mass transit measure was defeated decisively at the polls. While the CSCC and many Central District residents identified a society centered on  the automobile as a problem, other King County voters were not willing to reject their car-based lifestyles. They were, however, willing to pay additional taxes for road and highway construction, and the Forward Thrust highway construction measure did pass at the polls. 

     The aesthetics (or lack thereof) of the interstate expansion concerned the CSCC. The group believed that a third bridge would “seriously damage not only [Lake Washington’s] physical beauty, but its recreational value as well.” The group publicized and protested the highway department’s practice of buying houses in the right-of-way and then either leasing the structures on a no-maintenance basis or demolishing them, leaving in place the ugly ruins. For the CSCC, both practices created “pocket slums” of unsightly, unsafe rental housing or, worse, ugly heaps of splintered wood and metal that remained after home demolition.[18]

     Central Area activists also linked the expansion of freeways with potential social unrest. The interstate expansion threatened to destroy the historic Mt. Baker neighborhood. Ruth Brandwein lamented not only the loss of the attractive neighborhood, but the social costs as well. “The ridge is dotted with lovely homes occupied by businessmen and professionals who happen to be Negro,” she remarked. “[Mt. Baker] is the best example of an integrated middle class in Seattle.” Brandwein asserted that planners designed the project “without adequate consideration for human needs.” The massive interchange, where the interstate connected with the Thompson expressway, would have “irrevocable effects on the character of the entire community.” Brandwein went on to warn that “this psychological as well as physical southern barrier may very well contribute to an already potentially explosive situation” in the district.[19]

     Other area activists also saw the connection between the built environment and a stable society. The Central Area Neighborhood Development Organization (CANDO) believed that the built environment shaped human behavior. “Ugliness breeds ugliness,” one statement read. The group blasted a highway department plan to beautify the interstate with landscaping as inadequate, since state planners were clearly ignoring the “human effects” of running twelve lanes of interstate through a neighborhood. CANDO predicted that the expansion of I-90 would create “a Berlin Wall” in Seattle, one that was meant “to keep Negroes in their place.” To these activists, an unattractive physical barrier through their neighborhood would ruin the surrounding environment and create a setting for antisocial behavior. Many neighborhood residents saw the issue as another example of white oppression of the black community. One African-American speaker at a hearing for the interstate expansion argued there should be “no white freeway through the black community.” The Afro-American Journal was similarly explicit in its opposition toward the I-90 expansion. “If suburbanites want an easier way to work,” the Journal argued, “let them return to the city, pay taxes to the city, and join in trying to save the city rather than hiding in the suburbs, destroying and polluting our city with freeways so that they can hold the best jobs and extract the wealth of the city to the suburbs.”[20]

     The Seattle Post-Intelligencer regarded the racialization of the conflict with contempt. “To make a racial issue of the traffic project takes discussion out of the arena of rational debate,”  one editorial claimed. The editor argued that mitigation measures, such as landscaping projects and the tunnel through Mt. Baker ridge, would render the highway “undisruptive.”[21] Central District residents remained unconvinced that an additional eight lanes of interstate through their neighborhood would not be disruptive.

     Activists managed to stall the I-90 project and also succeeded in convincing the Washington State Department of Transportation to design a tunnel, rather than a slash through the hillside, to accommodate the highway. Ed Banks of the CSCC considered this a partial victory, since the community forced the engineers and government officials to “realize the human values” associated with the project. The third bridge, however, was eventually built. Thanks to new technology, the expanded interstate did not destroy the prosperous Mt. Baker neighborhood, as activists had feared. When construction finally began in 1983, new tunnel boring machines burrowed through Mt. Baker ridge without damaging the homes above. The highway department built play fields over the Mercer Island portion of the interstate to lessen noise pollution and remove the visual impact of the project for the upscale, white suburban community. Similar efforts were not undertaken in Central Seattle, and the expanded interstate still makes a significant visual and environmental impact on the largely minority neighborhood it bisects.[22]

     Urban residents and reformers struggled to better their surrounding environment throughout the twentieth-century. Progressive Era reformers worked for garbage collection, clean water supplies, and playgrounds, while late twentieth-century environmental justice advocates fought against toxic waste sites and for public health initiatives in their communities. Urban environmentalism has largely been left out of the story historians tell about post-war environmentalism, however. Environmental activism in the 1960s and 1970s was not the sole domain of white, middle and upper -class activists who sought to protect wilderness areas. Cities, too, were sites of many environmental battles, and today’s Central District reflects the environmental concerns of  activists in the 1960s and 1970s. These types of struggles continue: despite community opposition, a proposed light rail line is currently planned to run above ground through the Central District, though it will operate underground and out of sight throughout much of the rest of Seattle. More research is needed to understand the extent of urban environmental activism in the mid-twentieth century, but this subject must be given a place in the story of post-war environmentalism.
