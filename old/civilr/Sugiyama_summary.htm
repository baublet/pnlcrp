Alan Sugiyama interview

By Trevor Griffey & Jennifer Speidel


(summary by Jennifer Speidel)

0:00      Alan Sugiyama was born and raised in Seattle, his grandfather came from Japan in 1899 and returned to Japan to marry and then returned to Seattle.  Alan’s mother was born in Seattle and his father was born in Japan and came to Seattle at the age of 11.  The Sugiyama’s have been in Seattle for four generations.

3:00      Relocation to the camps, his parents did not speak of this topic much because they were embarrassed about internment.  Alan explains how his generation was the generation to bring the issue of internment to the forefront.

3:48      Seattle Central Community College experiences, Alan explains how there was a minority history class but it mainly focused on African-Americans and Mexican-Americans, only spending one week on Asian-Americans. 

4:45      Alan created the Oriental Student Union, they used the Black Student Union as an example to theirs.  Their goals were to get Asian-American courses and Asian-American Faculty.

5:35      Alan shares a little about his childhood in Seattle, he explains that they did not realize segregation until they went outside the International District areas.

7:20      Stokely Carmichael came to Seattle in the late 1960’s he visited Garfield High School at the height of the civil rights movement.  Faculty of the school district did not want Stokely to come and speak because of his radical nature.  The students protested to get Stokely to speak.  This was the first time Alan heard the term Asian-American.  Stokely changed the atmosphere at Garfield, people began to try to get along, and this lit the way for African Americans.

10:00    Creation of a rivalry between Garfield High School, a minority school, and Ballard High School, a predominantly white school.  Alan explains about a particular sporting event at Garfield High School where the Ballard students are not allowed to drive and are taken on busses to Garfield.  The Garfield students end up vandalizing the Ballard busses.

11:10    The summer of 1967-1968 a lot of protest was seen, mainly African-Americans taking out anger and frustrations on Asian-American businesses.

12:15    Asian-American movement begins, tensions between African-Americans and Asian-Americans rise.

12:35    Students become more involved in the civil rights movements and become the leader of the movements.  They develop new programs and classes at Seattle Central Community College.

14:20    Involvement with Black Student Unions in college.

14:50    Creation of the Black Panther Party, this was the first type of organization to play a key role with the student unions.

15:25    Asian Coalition for Equality began in 1966-1967, Alan was not involved with this organization.  He remembered the group because it was one of the first to have Asian-Americans speak out in the community.  The group believed that they needed to support African-American movements.

18:52    Alan founded the Oriental Student Union at Seattle Central Community College.  He wanted to get an Asian-American history class taught at the college.  The administration did not want the class.  Asian-Americans petitioned for the class and eventually succeeded.  It was left to Alan to find the professor and come up with the curriculum.  Alan also wanted to integrate all minority history into the main history courses so all races would be educated.

23:32    North Seattle Central Community College and Seattle Central Community College agreed to integrate the classes.

23:52    Alan explains that there were no models for them to model their group after; they made it up on their own.  They did have an advantage because of the African-American civil rights movement going on. 

24:35    The Oriental Student Union changed their name to the Asian Student Coalition.  They created groups at all the high schools, colleges and universities they had 30 different groups.

25:00    Alan explains the choice to be known as Asian-Americans rather than one single race such as Chinese or Japanese; they would make a larger difference having larger numbers.

28:13    The Puyallup fair grounds were used as a holding camp, Camp Harmony.  Alan’s parents were held at this camp.

30:58    Alan tells the story of the sit in he organized at the administration building of Seattle Central Community College.  His group wanted an Asian-American to be hired as an administrator and the administration told them they weren’t qualified enough.  It turned out that most that applied were qualified for the positions.  The president of Seattle Central Community College said, “I’m not going to let you Asian-Americans sneak through the back door and get administrative jobs.” 

35:20   Alan and his supporters shut down the administration building at Seattle Central Community College in February of 1971.  They forced all the administration out of the building.  The demonstration received a lot of media coverage but the school would not change their minds about hiring an Asian-American.

39:15    March three weeks after the first demonstration Alan and his supporters held another in the administrative building.  This time the police were more involved.  Alan wanted a peaceful demonstration so to keep everyone busy he had him or her tape up yellow paper all over the building to get his or her point across.  A lot more publicity came from this demonstration and more people in the Asian-American community began to support the movement.

44:55    The administration began pointing their finger at Alan, calling him a juvenile delinquent, because they thought he had not support from the community.  Community members began to speak up and support him.

45:45    The board of trustees as Seattle Central Community College wanted to make peace with Alan and agreed to hire an Asian-American administrator the following quarter. 

47:10    Seattle Central Community College asked Alan to leave because he had broken the rules.  Alan then transferred to the University of Washington.

48:00    Tensions at Seattle Central Community College between Asian-American and African-American groups grew because Asian-Americans did not want to support the African-American movement.  They were trying to define their own identity and get their movement off the ground.

51:03    The University of Washington was behind in the Asian-American movement.  They were still trying to create an ethnic studies program at UW; Alan used the tactics he learned at Seattle Central Community College to create an ethnic studies program at UW.

52:22    Alan tells how people never had an understanding of what Asian-American history was or even minority history.  People would talk about either Asia or Africa when speaking of it.  The history department at UW only offered European classes because they thought that was were everyone had came from.  Alan didn’t come from Europe and many white students said the same thing.  The teachers at UW were more closed-minded than those at Seattle Central Community College; it was more difficult to establish an ethnic studies program at the UW.

54:55    Alan created the Asian Family Affair, this newspaper focused on creating an identity for Asian-Americans.  They were tired of the medias perspective coming from those not of Asian-American background and wanted to change what people thought of them. 

57:00    Asian Family Affair also wanted to get discriminatory movies, TV series and records off the air. 

1:00:00             Politics with the construction of the Kingdome, the city government decided to place the Kingdome in the International District.  This new construction would create a large housing shortage for low-income families because many of their homes would be torn down to build the dome.  Asian-Americans wanted to fight the dome in the court but they lost.

1:04:08             The ground-breaking celebration for the Kingdome, Alan and a group of his friends decided to go down to the celebration and disrupt it; they wanted the city to know that the community did not agree with the construction of the dome. 

1:09:32             Alan and his supporters organized another demonstration called the March of HUD, because they were the ones holding all the money for low-income housing.   All members of the community came out to support this demonstration.

1:10:35             Community Health Centers for low-income families were being created in other areas in Seattle; the International District felt they needed one.  Alan went to John Spellman, county executive to ask for a health center.  He agreed to fund the building of the first health clinic in the International District.

1:11:58             Model Cities was another organization designed to provide low-income housing but they felt their main responsibility was to provide to the black community.

1:14:00             International District preservation is something Alan is very proud of; he feels the International District is very unique because a lot of it is the original district.  He and his friends were able to maintain a lot of the integrity of the district.

1:16:50             Seattle’s chapter of the Japanese American Citizens League (JACL) took on more leadership for the internment issues; Seattle was at the forefront to create reparations for the internees.  This leadership was created by the student unions that wanted to lean more about their history. 

1:21:45             Alan feels that younger generations today are not familiar or appreciative of what they have today.  Students to not understand or care about what it took to get the history put into the textbooks or what it took to get an Asian-American teacher or administrator hired in the 1970s.  They do not realize the value of their history because they do not have to fight for it today like Asian-American students did in the 1960s and 1970s.
