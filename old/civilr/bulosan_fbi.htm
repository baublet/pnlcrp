About the FBI Files

 

 

 

Introduction by Rick Baldoz

The FBI files on Carlos Bulosan from the 1940s and 1950s shed light on more than just the life of a renowned writer and labor activist.  They also illuminate two interrelated issues: the trajectory of Filipino American political consciousness in the era of anti-colonial upheavals sweeping across the globe, and the ideological machinations of U.S. authorities working to contain the spread of domestic and international radicalism during the Cold War.

 

[read full report/]

The U.S. government’s scrutiny of Filipino political activists during the Cold War is hardly surprising. Filipino immigrants had developed a well-earned reputation for labor militancy in Hawaii, Alaska, and the West Coast dating back to 1920s. That a considerable number of Filipinos (both from the U.S. and the Philippines) had volunteered for the International Brigades during the Spanish Civil War to fight against fascist forces during the late 1930s only added to the perception that they were organically drawn to radical politics. While this political trait won them plaudits from progressives, it triggered a forceful counter-offensive from business and political elites who viewed minority political activism as a serious threat to the American social order.

Consequently, Filipino workers were regular targets of police surveillance and legal harassment in the 1920s and 1930s as their labor organizing efforts gathered momentum. The key distinction between efforts aimed at suppressing radicals during this earlier period and those carried out in the 1950s was the locus of enforcement. Before World War Two, the monitoring of Filipino radicals was often carried out at the local level and were typically handled by “red squads” housed in municipal police departments across the country. The FBI’s investigation of Carlos Bulosan and other left-wing union leaders in the 1940s and 1950s is best viewed as a continuation of a longer-term campaign to discipline and subordinate Filipino labor in the United States.

Early examples of anti-radical campaigns directed at Filipino partisans help to contextualize the Cold War persecution of figures like Carlos Bulosan. This phenomenon first took root in Hawaii in the mid-1920s when organizing efforts led by charismatic labor leader Pablo Manlapit galvanized plantation workers to fight back against exploitative conditions. Filipinos had initially been recruited during the first two decades of the twentieth century to offset the growing solidarity of Japanese workers on the plantations who leveraged their large numerical majority among the plantation workforce to demand concessions from the sugar industry.

The recruitment of Filipinos ultimately backfired as the newcomers quickly embraced oppositional politics, launching a number of crippling island-wide strikes in the 1920s. Filipinos’ enthusiastic embrace of labor militancy prompted a furious backlash from plantation bosses moved quickly to suppress Filipino labor solidarity. The sugar industry worked closely with local law enforcement to develop a network of informants to gather intelligence on union supporters and disrupt strike activity. Popular accounts of labor unrest in the islands depicted Filipino unionists as communist sympathizers and union demands were dismissed as the product of a Bolshevik conspiracy aimed at seizing control of Hawaii’s sugar industry. Plantation officials aided local police and prosecutors to disrupt labor organizing. Many Filipino organizers were arrested on trumped up charges (conspiracy, sedition etc.) aimed at undercutting union solidarity and a number of leaders, including Pablo Manlapit, were expelled from the islands for their “subversive” activities.

A similar pattern unfolded on the West Coast, which witnessed an influx of Filipino immigrants in the late 1920s. Though initially welcomed as a cheap and pliable solution to labor shortages in the region’s burgeoning agricultural sector, they soon gained a reputation for political militancy. Largely ignored by mainstream unions as un-organizable, Filipino workers found allies among the communist-affiliated unions that launched efforts to catalyze the agricultural proletariat as part of their revolutionary program. They also launched a number of independent ethnic labor organizations like the Filipino Labor Union (FLU) and the Filipino Agricultural Laborers Association (FALA) that allowed them to exercise greater autonomy over organizing strategies. Filipino strike activity increased in frequency and sophistication by the mid-1930s, prompting a fierce backlash from growers and political elites who employed legal and extralegal tactics to suppress union activism.

A loose coalition of conservative interest groups coalesced during this period to address the growing political and social intransigence of Filipino workers. Some groups like the American Legion and the Grange worked closely with local law enforcement to intimidate and/or arrest labor organizers (for violating criminal syndicalism laws, anti-picketing ordinances, etc.), while others like the Associated Farmers utilized vigilante methods to violently attack or expel “red agitators” from agricultural communities. Filipino labor actions on the West Coast were frequently depicted in popular media accounts as excessively radical, which in turn justified harsh crackdowns from law enforcement or from vigilantes operating with the tacit approval of the local political establishment. These earlier instances of labor suppression and legal persecution serve as a useful reminder that the persecution of Filipino activists pre-dates the anti-communist hysteria that proliferated during the Cold War.

The federal government took on a more central role in rooting out radicals after World War Two, spurred by the growing influence of the J. Edgar Hoover-led FBI and the outsized political sway of the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) in the U.S. Congress. Conservative leaders were animated by a crusading spirit and mobilized their ample socio-cultural resources to make Americans more aware of the furtive communist conspiracy that had taken root in the nation’s institutions. These political actors derive prestige and power by declaring their values as normative and the source of societal cohesion and depicting alternative viewpoints as a threat to the political and cultural fabric of U.S. society. The federalization of the anti-communist movement provided law enforcement officials with unprecedented manpower and legal powers to pursue those whose political beliefs were deemed suspect.  

By the late 1940s federal officials had a new set of prosecutory tools at their disposal to go after accused subversives. In 1947, President Truman’s issued Executive Order 9835, which created a “loyalty-security program” that barred individuals’ accused of “sympathetic association” with dissident political groups from federal employment. Filipinos activists had close ties to a number progressive labor and civil rights organizations targeted by federal authorities. The Internal Security Act of 1950 (aka the McCarran Act) provided another boost to anti-communist crusaders. It required all members of the Communist Party and/or designated subversive organizations to register with the federal government and acquiesce to regular monitoring from the newly created Subversive Activities Control Board.

This law had significant consequences for Filipino activists since prosecutors could use past or present affiliation with left-wing groups to as grounds for detention and deportation under the pretext of national security. Ernesto Mangaoang and Chris Mensalves and a number of the ILWU members were arrested under the McCarran Act. Mangaoang, who federal authorities named as one of the “86 top alien communists” in the United States, faced deportation in a case that was eventually heard by the U.S. Appeals Court.

FBI Files as Political Texts

Evaluating the FBI files on Filipino radicals is a challenge, since much of the material was filtered through the ideological prism of the McCarthy-era anxieties. Field operatives were tasked with digging up evidence that could be used to prosecute left-wing activists and those who failed to provide actionable intelligence did not last long at the FBI. Agents had at their disposal a formidable state apparatus that was wielded to coerce individuals into cooperating with the government and overzealous investigators often abused this power in their pursuit of activists. The ultra secretive nature of the FBI probes adds another layer of complexity to this story, since we have little knowledge about the context under which testimony was provided or whether informants were compensated for their cooperation. Nor do we know if exculpatory material was suppressed or downplayed if it contradicted the government’s pursuit of suspected subversives. Informants interviewed by the FBI were classified as being of either “known reliability” or “unknown reliability,” the latter trading mostly in rumor and speculation about a target’s political views. The motivations of the various informants referred to in these files certainly raise some immediate questions, since they often identified someone as a “subversive” to escape prosecution themselves and thus were incentivized to “name names” regardless of the veracity of such accusations. Some of the individuals that provided information to the FBI are also suspect since they stood to gain politically from ratting out alleged communists. This would include informants affiliated with rival labor organizations who sought to poach the membership rolls of left-wing unions, officials from the Philippine Consulate who wanted to break up mutual-aid networks between Filipino partisans in the United States and the Philippines, and conservative members of the ILWU who wanted to oust the progressive leadership bloc and seize control of the union.   
            The investigation of Raymundo Cabanilla followed the classic pattern of McCarthy era probe. An unidentified informant named Mr. Cabanilla as a communist setting into motion an investigation into his political background. By the time he came under scrutiny from the FBI in their early 1950s he was working as a low level file clerk who had recently been employed by federal government at the U.S. Department of Treasury and the Federal Security Agency. Cabanilla’s previous work as a labor organizer for the FTA and later for the United Public Workers of America (UPW) in the 1940s raised red flags for authorities. Both organizations were among the 11 unions expelled by the CIO in 1949 and 1950 for failing to purge their ranks of alleged communist members. Cabanilla, like many others facing criminal charges cooperated with authorities hoping to save himself from jail time and the loss of his job. He denied that he held subversive beliefs and gave investigators the names of other labor militants who he believed were actual communist party supporters, including fellow organizers Mamerto Ventura, Ernesto Mangoang and Nick Manzano. One of the key pieces of evidence cited by investigators interrogating Mr. Cabanilla was his relationship with Carlos Bulosan. The two has worked together on a FTA strike campaign in California and FBI agents noted that Cabanilla owned a signed copy of Bulosan’s book America is in the Heart. We see this sort of flimsy evidence was used repeatedly in the FBI files, which often employed a circular, guilt-by-association reasoning: labeling individual A as a subversive because he or she was an associate of individual B. Likewise individual B came under suspicion because of their association with individual A underscoring the self-fulfilling logic of anti-communist crusaders.

Reading Bulosan’s File
The FBI files on Carlos Bulosan affirm much of what we already knew about his life in the United States and adds a few details that help to paint a fuller picture of his political career. He was active in a number of popular front groups during the 1930s including the California Labor School, Committee for the Protection of the Foreign-Born, The League of American Writers, and the Civil Rights Congress. Bulosan wrote for a number of leftist periodicals during the 1930s and 1940s such as the Daily Worker, New Masses, and Daily People’s World. He was also was a established figure in the “proletarian” literary scene in California intermingling with prominent figures like Carey McWilliams, John Fante, and Louis Adamic. His affiliations with a handful of left-wing trade unions on the West Coast drew significant attention from authorities who maintained close surveillance of these groups. Two of these unions, the FTA and the ILWU, were expelled from the CIO in 1949 as part of an effort to purge alleged communist-friendly unions from its ranks.
            The files on Bulosan’s political activities from the 1930s to the 1950s paint a picture of someone who was quite active in popular front circles, though concrete evidence of communist party membership was circumstantial. His strident condemnation of U.S. military interventions in Asia was a key focus of federal officials since it raised the possibility that he might be detained as a national security threat. Such criticisms were often viewed as evidence of “disloyalty” that could be used as grounds for deportation. Moreover, Bulosan’s relationship with the Chris Mensalves and Ernesto Mangoang and the other Seattle labor figures guaranteed that he would remain in the crosshairs of authorities since the ILWU took a vocal anti-imperialist line on U.S. foreign policy during the Cold War. Informants provided contradictory information about his political affiliations. A number of witnesses from the Filipino community suggested that Bulosan was a communist, but offered little corroboration to back up these claims simply citing his work for progressive labor unions and his criticism of U.S. foreign policy as proof of his subversive beliefs. The shakiness of the government’s case is illustrated in an anecdote recounted by an informant who described how Bulosan had signaled his commitment to the worldwide communist movement when he used a pencil to draw a “large C” on the tablecloth during a discussion about his political aspirations. According to numerous witnesses he had frequently described himself as the “number 1 man” among Filipino radicals on the West Coast and had been appointed as the Commission Secretary of the Committee for Filipino Rights, a short-lived CP front group established in Los Angeles in the 1940s.
While Bulosan’s association with various front groups in the 1930s and 1940s was well established, agents struggled to build an open and shut legal case against him. Informants affiliated with the Communist Party on the West Coast cast doubt on allegations that he had been a member of the party, telling investigators that he was unknown to them and that they had never seen him at CP meetings. Prosecutions based on front group activity alone were difficult to win and government attorneys’ approached such cases cautiously after suffering a series of embarrassing legal defeats in their campaign to deport Harry Bridges another partisan of the ILWU targeted by the FBI.
Bulosan’s relationship with the Huks and progressive union leaders in the Philippines attracted significant attention from federal authorities. Intelligence officials in the United States and the Philippines maintained a close working relationship after World War Two and shared a common goal of preventing the spread of the left-wing political forces in Southeast Asia. Bulosan’s support of the Huks was no secret and Philippine intelligence officials had discovered correspondence he had written to Luis Taruc and Amado Hernandez, which was subsequently turned over the U.S. authorities. In letters to the two well-known activists he expressed his admiration for their revolutionary ideals and assured them that he was working to marshal support for Philippine insurgents among American progressives. In one fascinating passage, Bulosan tells Taruc that he is working on a screenplay about the Huks with the hope that it would be made into a Hollywood film. Bulosan’s communication with Philippine radicals combined with his public criticism of U.S. imperialism in Asia clearly piqued the interest of American authorities, yet evidence of material support for the insurgents was clearly lacking.
In typical fashion, the FBI attempted to dig up salacious details about his personal life that might be used to impugn his character and undermine his standing in the community. Despite their best efforts, investigators did not turn up much juicy gossip about his lifestyle. Agents characterized Bulosan as a chronic alcoholic and numerous witnesses, including one of his doctors, claimed that he drank a pint of whiskey per day and other informants told the FBI that he was unable to write without the assistance of liquor. It is difficult to verify the claims of alcoholism, but his drinking took a serious toll on his health and contributed to his being admitted to the Firland Sanitarium in Seattle in 1950 for a relapse of tuberculosis. His disintegrating physical condition was registered in his admitting documents, which noted that Bulsoan’s weight had dropped to 87 pounds. Whether true or not this information was of little value, since news that prominent literary figure was also a heavy drinker was hardly remarkable.
Bulosan’s romantic life was another subject of inquiry for the FBI with a particular focus on his relationships with white women. Investigators took a special interest in his marriage to Marjorie Chubb, a working-class white woman from Los Angeles. The FBI went so far as to interview Chubb’s mother to see if they could find out information about the couple’s social networks. Agents also looked into rumors that Bulosan had numerous affairs with white women during his activist period including with a mysterious schoolteacher known only as “Miss Cunningham” who they suspected might be affiliated with the Communist Party in Los Angeles. Authorities had long exploited anxieties about interracial sex and marriage to dismiss the political grievances of the Filipino community, suggesting that their civil rights demands were a smokescreen for their supposedly real agenda of unimpeded access to white women.
Bulosan’s broader social life also came under scrutiny and his name frequently appears on inventories of individuals who attended fundraisers and political meetings in Seattle during the early 1950s including a number of events organized to support Filipino ILWU activists facing deportation during this period. The FBI files make it clear that they had infiltrators among Bulosan’s political circles in Seattle. They provide a chilling amount of detail recounting conversations and gossip overheard at private events along with comprehensive lists of individuals who attended meetings or social gatherings.
Despite their multi-year investigation into Bulosan’s life, the FBI’s case remained largely circumstantial. Federal agents actually interviewed Bulosan in 1952 in Seattle hoping to trick or coerce him into admitting that he was a subversive. It is appears that he was prepared for the interrogation, offering evasive and vague answers that allowed for plausible deniability regarding his past and present political activities. As one would expect, he denied that had ever been a member of the communist party, knowing that an affirmative answer would likely lead to his deportation. When questioned about his support for the Huks and left-wing unions in the Philippines, Bulosan offered an equivocal response claiming that he supported the Huks for their guerilla efforts against the Japanese occupation during World War Two and that he knew little of their radical political program after the war. While this statement was clearly untrue, agents were unable to pin down the exact nature of his relationship with various left-wing organizations in the Philippines. In one telling anecdote, investigators asked Bulosan why he so often took the “communist line” on contemporaneous political issues like civil rights, labor advocacy, and U.S. foreign policy, and he simply shrugged, offering no concrete answer.
Agents ultimately closed the file on Carlos Bulosan without bringing charges against him deciding that beyond intermittent front-group activity they did not compile clear proof of membership in the communist party or material support of Philippine insurgent organizations. This determination of course does not really answer the question of whether or not Bulosan was in fact a communist. The FBI files on Bulosan affirm what we already knew-- that he was a “fellow traveler” who aligned himself with left-wing political organizations in the United States and the Philippines. Moreover, the investigation into Filipino partisans during the Cold War serves as a reminder of the high price that these immigrant activists paid for challenging the status quo.
