Special Section: Seattle's Asian American Movement

March on HUD; International District Preservation

[Asian Coalition for Equality (ACE)]  [Oriental Student Union Sit-In]  [Kingdome Protest]  [ID Preservation]

After disrupting the groundbreaking of Seattle's Kingdome on November 2, 1972, Asian American activists channeled the media attention and political momentum they generated from that event into a campaign to preserve Seattle's International District. A November 14, 1972 march from Hing Hay Park to the Seattle Office of the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) announced this campaign to the public. It also connected the campaign to specific demands for government funds to rehabilitate the neighborhood.

For the rest of the 1970s, Seattle's Asian American movement would elaborate upon those demands, which centered upon expanding the International District's affordable housing and providing a wide range of culturally appropriate services to the neighborhood's residents, particularly its elderly. Seattle’s movement activity set itself apart from others on the West Coast for two reasons: it was an explicitly pan-Asian movement, and it focused on the needs of the neighborhood’s residents over the desires of tourists.

The march itself was meant to demosntrate community support for an "8 point program" to preserve the ID. It was led by Asian American elderly, organized largely by Asian youth activists from the University of Washington, members of the Union of Democratic Filipinos (KDP) and included a number of students from the International District Youth Center (IDYC) as well as others marching in solidarity.
