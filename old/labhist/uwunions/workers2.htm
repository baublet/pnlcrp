The University of Washington employs more than 20,000 people in hundreds of different capacities. The list of job titles runs for 23 single spaced pages, and includes more than 4,000 faculty, 3,000 graduate student employees, 12,000 classified staff, and 4,000 professional staff. The Human Resources Department keeps track of employment issues and its website is a starting place for learning about UW's workers. 

The other pages of this web project describe the unions and professional organizations that represent many of the campus workers. Here we want to profile in some detail a few of the people who make UW work. We start with four reports written by students in HSTAA 450 "Class and Labor in American History," hoping to add other materials soon.

Yasmine Tarhouni investigates the work experiences of food service workers in one of the campus facilities, showing the new pattern of exploitation that has resulted as management attempts to circumvent the state law limiting the use of temporary workers. (This paper received the Harry Bridges Center for Labor Studies prize for the best undergraduate research paper of 2002).

 Anni Mackin interviews three custodians, learning about the frustrations and tensions that have become part of that job. She explores among other issues the implementation of the “team” system.

Jonathan Gay describes the activities and attitudes of office staff in one of the science departments, asking about their future plans and their relations with their union.

Skilled Craft Workers:

There are more than three hundred skilled craft workers at UW who keep the buildings in good repair. Until recently they were represented by the Seattle-King County Building Trades Council. But in June 2002, a majority of the voting skilled workers chose to decertify the union. We have two reports that were written just before the decertification election that profile these workers and explore some of the issues that played out in that election. Tyson Burchak describes the workers, their former union, and what was at the time a forthcoming election. Jeremy Porter, himself an off-campus electrician who is not a member of a union, offers what he calls an "editorial treatise" on unions and "pursuing the prevailing wage."
