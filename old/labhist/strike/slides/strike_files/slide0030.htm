Workers eating at labor-managed dining halls. There were twenty-one such halls throughout the city during the strike, serving from 20,000 to 30,000 meals per day.
With the city shut down, unionists organized an entire network of worker-run institutions including kitchens, milk stations, laundries, garbage collection, and fire and police services.
Perhaps more than the strike itself, it was this largely successful demonstration of workers’ control that made the Seattle general strike one of the most radical labor actions in American history.
