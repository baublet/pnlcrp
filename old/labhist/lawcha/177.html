Race, "Free Labor," and Working-Class Politics in the Nineteenth Century

Dennis Halpin1, Charles Williams2, Moon Ho-Jung3, Lorenzo Costaguta4
1Virginia Tech, History Department, 2University of Washington Tacoma, 3University of Washington, 4University of Nottingham

Abstract

□ “Regardless of Color”:The American Socialist Movement and African Americans between 1876 and 1890 Labour historians such as Philip S. Foner argue that during the Gilded Age the American socialist movement - especially the Socialist Labor Party – was “oblivious” to the problem of race discrimination. Socialists, according to Foner, failed to understand that African Americans’ reality of discrimination was structurally engrained in the social fabric of the ex-Confederate States, and that, consequently, it required socialists to work out exceptional measures in order to fight for their aim of universal equality (Foner 1977). My paper challenges this orthodoxy, critically engaging with scholars such as Foner in order to shed new light on our understanding of the relationship between socialism and race at the turn of the century. By critically examining German-language socialist sources - something that is lacking from current studies - I will track down debates on the race question amongst SLP members, explaining the different positions within the party. Socialists such as the black teacher Peter H. Clark and Adolph Douai correctly understood that race discrimination and class exploitation had a mutual correlation. Others saw the scourge of racial discrimination against blacks but argued that only class struggle and the achievement of the Socialist Commonwealth could deliver racial equality. Some others, finally, justified blacks’ discrimination adopting scientific racialist and social Darwinist approaches that proved, in their opinion, the biological inferiority of blacks. It is my contention that the concept of “scales of struggle” is extremely useful to shed light on the strengths and limits of socialists of the time. On the one hand, a key shortcoming of the SLP was its failure to organise African Americans in the South, something that was at least in part caused by the structural weakness of the party’s Southern sections and their inability to create connections with black workers’ communities. On the other hand, only by adopting a transatlantic analytical perspective, which considers the strong connections between the German and the American socialist movements, it is possible to understand how American socialist racial thought developed in this period. In sum, by placing the Gilded Age American socialist movement in a context that considers its different “scales of struggle”, my paper will offer a reassessment of the complex relationship between socialism and race in a key moment for the history of the multiracial American working class.

□ “A Union of All Reforms": The Industrial Congress and the Antislavery-Labor Alliance, 1845–1860 The relationship between the early American labor movement and abolitionism has often been portrayed as fraught with tension and mutual animosity, a hostility that sometimes spilled over into violence in such episodes as the Anti-abolitionist Riots of 1834 and the Draft Riots of 1863. Indeed, labor reformers and abolitionists were often in conflict with one another over such issues as economic inequality, race, and the legitimacy of wage labor in an evolving capitalist economy. But out of the discursive debates over slavery, labor, and political economy in the 1830s and 40s emerged, I argue, a degree of cooperation and coalition-building between labor reformers and abolitionists that has been largely overlooked by most of the recent literature on antislavery as well as the newest work on “capitalism and slavery.” Land reformers, trade unionists, ten hours-law supporters, abolitionists and early socialists made common cause in the pre-Civil War Industrial Congresses, held between 1845 and 1860 and hailed by progressives of various stripes as a “union of reformers” that would help to bring about what they referred to as an “integral emancipation,” liberating workers from both chattel and wage slavery.

While the Industrial Congresses ultimately fell far short of these

overly sanguine expectations, they did lead to an unprecedented gathering together of the representatives of various reforms, as well as to a tentative but transformative political alliance between working-class land reformers affiliated with the National Reform Association and the antislavery Liberty Party. In keeping with the theme of “Borders and Coalitions,” I propose to examine the impact of this alliance in the context of the political realignments bought about by the aftermath of the Mexican-American War and Wilmot Proviso and the heightened internationalism of the revolutionary year 1848. The advent of an aggressive slaveholder expansionism and the prospect of the loss of formerly “free soil” to slave plantations provided the catalyst for an unprecedented degree of cooperation between antislavery and pro-labor forces. While the alliance between labor reformers and the Liberty Party was short-lived, it resulted in an unprecedented and perhaps never-replicated free-labor experiment between abolitionist Gerrit Smith and landless workers, both black and white, in New York City and State. Although the Industrial Congresses lost much of their momentum by the early 1850s, the representatives it gathered and the ideas they propounded ultimately contributed to the ideological and social base of the new Republican Party. After the war, the Industrial Congresses would be once again revived to serve as a vehicle for a reanimated labor militancy.

□ “Driven to Desperation:” Race, Navassa Island, and the Problem of Free Labor in the Nineteenth Century My paper uses the 1889 uprising on Navassa to complicate our understandings of the transition from slave to “free labor” at the end of the nineteenth century. Throughout the 1880s and 1890s, the Navassa Phosphate Company lured African-Americans with promises of high wages, company housing, and benefits. The reality failed to live up to these lofty promises; in fact, The New York Times proclaimed that the company’s employees were in effect “slaves.” The events of 1889 focused national attention on the island, the company’s employment practices, and galvanized labor and civil rights activists.

□ Labor Ideology and the Local Politics of Chinese Exclusion: The 1885 Chinese Expulsion in Tacoma, Washington Territory The expulsion of the Chinese community from Tacoma in 1885 was a prominent event in the wider anti-Chinese politics of the late 19th-century United States. This paper focuses on the labor ideologies espoused by key figures in the expulsion, including in particular the city mayor, Jacob Weisbach, who led the year-long campaign that culminated in the forced removal of all Chinese residents. By combining arguments tied to labor republicanism, a producer-controlled economy, and the threat of Chinese “slavery,” the expulsion reveals how the egalitarian rhetoric of labor radicalism could be turned to endorsing the repressive racial politics that integrated European immigrants into a white republic. The prominence of the mayor, a German immigrant and radical, further reveals how a local labor-based political coalition could frame anti-corporate and race-based politics as a unified vision in the face of an emerging industrial order, in this case embodied in the power of the Northern Pacific Railroad that dominated the city's economy. Understanding how such politics played out in Tacoma is not just a matter of tracing a representative experience, however, but instead points to the need to see how concrete political actors drew upon and modified inherited conceptions of racial hierarchy in response to specific economic and political conditions.
