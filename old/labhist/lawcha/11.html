Female Wobblies in Wartime

Heather Mayer
Portland Community College

Abstract

World War I was a tumultuous time for the Industrial Workers of the World in the Pacific Northwest. In addition to the massive trial of Wobbly leadership in Chicago, and the smaller but infamous cases of Louise Olivereau and Marie Equi and Seattle and Portland, little-known Wobbly women were also at risk during this period.

Female Wobblies risked arrest for simple membership in the organization during the repressive years of the Red Scare. While the male leadership was rounded up in raids across the country, women were arrested for supporting the organization behind the scenes by editing and distributing the Industrial Worker, providing support for men in jail, and working with defense organizations to raise money for trial expenses and to support the families of those in jail. The authorities, however, recognized this support as essential to the Wobbly cause, and therefore women were targets of federal surveillance as much as any of the well-known male Wobblies of the period. This paper examines how the massive arrests affected Wobbly communities in the Pacific Northwest and how women worked to support those in jail and risked arrest themselves.
