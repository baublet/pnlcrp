The Role of the Precariat Sociologist in Transforming Academia

Sarah Grunberg
Ithaca College

Abstract

Abstract

This short personal memoir of my life as an adjunct in a sociology department addresses the lived condition of the precariat in higher education today and calls for the academic community generally, and sociologists specifically, to become more publicly and actively engaged with revolutionary solidarity and the fight to transform higher education. While sociologists often speak abstractly about concepts such as C. Wright Mills’ Sociological Imagination, this article challenges contingent and non-contingent professional scholars to apply this knowledge to their own lived experiences in academia. As contingent workers, we are caught in a state of false consciousness, believing that our job market chances are based on our individual achievements and ability to “market ourselves”, rather than on a system that is more invested in our exploitation than it is in our, or our student’s, futures.

How can we “do” sociology without actively challenging and changing the systems that oppress us as workers and as human beings? Sociology teaches that collective action can be effective. The adjunct professor can move toward empowerment. As such, this memoir also concerns my experiences in labor union organizing at my college and solidarity in activism surrounding racial and economic justice with my undergraduate students in our fight against the hierarchies of college administration and the corporatization of higher education. This is accomplished through the application of sociological thought to my own position in academia and my fight to bring a voice to marginalized individuals who have the least amount of power and authority in an institution where governance and decision making power is left to the disconnected few.
