Religious Leaders, Grassroots Responses, and Political Change

Cassie Miller1, Ian Rocksborough-Smith2, Susan Breitzer3, Susan Glenn4
1Southern Poverty Law Center, 2Corpus Christi College, 3Independent Scholar, 4University of Washington

Abstract

□ Radical Religion: The Catholic Church and the War on Poverty in Brooklyn The Catholic Church was (and remains) defined by boundaries – between parishes, dioceses, and priests and parishioners. By the late 1960s, those boundaries were also racial and political. The majority of Catholic parishioners were white and conservative, but a small and active contingent of black Catholic churches emerged to challenge the politics of their coreligionists. This paper will examine the alliances that arose to overcome parish and racial boundaries in Brooklyn and, in particular, how a contingent of “radical” priests and nuns allied with liberal white Catholics, black and Puerto Rican Catholics, community activists, and like-minded organizations to spearhead a number of progressive programs – including non-profit housing corporations and welfare rights organizations – that were inspired by the Gospel but performed in the streets of the city. Significantly, the majority of these efforts were made possible through the Great Society’s Office of Economic Opportunity, as well as a “diocesan war on poverty.” This paper thus explores the combined efforts of the Church and the state to intervene in systems of poverty and discrimination, but also chronicles their ultimate failures. We are rich in histories of the Great Society from the perspective of politicians and bureaucrats, but this paper provides a grassroots perspective of the community institutions that carried out the OEO’s mission – from its well-funded initial outburst to its slow withdrawal that crippled progressive organizations. This paper will argue that the Catholic Church is an exemplary case study for examining the failures of the War on Poverty because it allows historians to view the program in microcosm, revealing how progressive, interracial efforts to alleviate poverty were abandoned to fund those that, as one Brooklyn Catholic argued, would not disrupt “the sanctity of the status quo.” The Church, like the state, failed to truly challenge the boundaries of inequality in American cities.

□ The Ambiguities of Catholic Inter-racialism in 1950s Chicago: Friendship House and the Catholic Interracial Council This paper examines the activities of the Catholic Interracial Council in Chicago as well its affiliated Friendship House from the mid-1940s through the 1950s under the liberal Bishop Bernard J. Sheil. Indeed, Sheil preached about the "doom" of racism in 1943 in light of the atrocities committed in Nazi Germany. This paper considers the relationship of these progressive Catholic institutions to the political dynamics of the Catholic archdiocese of Chicago and the U.S. Catholic church generally during the early years of the Cold War as well as the important missions the church had with working class migrant communities in the Windy City, particularly Polish and African American communities. It considers especially how issues of racial discrimination were discussed in the context of growing early Cold War acrimonies between the radical left and Catholic workers movements which often leaned towards conservative ends of the political spectrum and the complexities of ethnic and racial identities that came to light as a result of these dynamics.

□ Love of Labor Lost? Religion, Human Rights, and the Argentine “Dirty War" The Argentine “Dirty War,” which followed the 1976 anti-Peron coup and the international human rights movement it sparked are well-studied topics. This paper, touching on the conference subtheme of War and Empire will spotlight the activist role of religious leaders such as Rabbi Marshall T. Meyer, who was well known for his role in promoting domestic and international social justice and for bringing the widespread human rights abuses in Argentina to public attention. Meyer's role in raising awareness of the human rights abuses of the Argentine junta is laudable in terms of bringing social justice concerns into the Jewish and general religious mainstream. However, it is also important to look at Meyer's role (and that of similar religious leaders) in downplaying the labor aspects of the Argentine human rights tragedy, the better to deflect negative associations of human rights with political leftism in the waning Cold War, and to broaden the base of support. These efforts, in turn, may have played into the hands of the Videla regime, which pointedly used terror to silence the workers' movements that formed an important component of the opposition. Meyer and his like-minded clerical colleagues therefore may also have contributed to the marginalization of labor and class issues from the progressive religious activism they helped to create, and with it, the popular conception of human rights.
