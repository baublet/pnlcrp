The Unbalanced Scales of Borderlands Struggles: Human Traffickers, Organizers, and Farmworkers in South Texas

Timothy Bowman1, Andrew Hazelton2, John Weber3, Sonia Hernandez4
1West Texas A&M University, 2Texas A&M International University, 3Old Dominion University, 4Texas A&M University

Abstract

This panel casts the U.S.-Mexico borderlands as a region of unbalanced struggle for workers, organizers, immigrants, and Border Patrol officers. It considers the borderlands as both a tightly integrated and deeply divided region united by capital and labor flows but separated by the geopolitical division of national borders. John Weber examines the activities of Laredo-area Border Patrol agents in the 1920s who trafficked European immigrants into the U.S. from Mexico while simultaneously ignoring undocumented Mexican migration in deference to agricultural interests. Andrew Hazelton explores the careers of Clemente and Federico Idar, two brothers who organized for the American and Mexican labor movements during the 1920s as part of their broader efforts to improve conditions for Mexican-descended people in the borderlands. Looking to the 1960s, Tim Bowman analyzes the efforts of Antonio Orendain, whose negative experiences in the La Casita Farms strike in Starr County made him cynical about the organizing potential of South Texas and stifled his advocacy in later decades.
