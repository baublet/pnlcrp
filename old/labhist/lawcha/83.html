Labor’s Role in a ‘Tax the Rich’ Ballot Measure Campaign: The Lessons of California’s Success Story

Fred Glass
California Federation of Teachers

Abstract

In November 2012 the California electorate passed Proposition 30, which levied the biggest increases on the top marginal state income tax rates since World War II. These revenues halted the deep cuts to public education and services brought on by a combination of the Great Recession and years of anti-tax, anti-government rhetoric and policy. The passage of Prop 30 demonstrated that a labor-community coalition with the right messaging and a serious investment of resources could overcome the perception that progressive tax policies were simply not possible to enact. While part of the successful effort relied on the changing demographics of the California electorate, the more important lesson for labor forces to consider in other states is that you can’t get there if you don’t try. California provides a case study in how the convergence of growing economic inequality, and insights initially provided by the Occupy movement and reinforced by the Sanders campaign, provide the context today for a labor-backed renewal of fair tax solutions to the crisis of public sector financing.
