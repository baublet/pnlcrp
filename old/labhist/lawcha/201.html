Thinking Globally, Resisting Locally: What Can Labor Learn From Seattle and Washington State?

Nancy MacLean1, Jeff Johnson2, Nicole Grant3, Rebecca Saldana4
1Duke University, 2Washington State Labor Council, 3M.L. King County Labor Council, 4Washington State Senator 37th LD

Abstract

For almost twenty years, since the 1999 “Battle of Seattle” WTO demonstrations, the labor movement in Washington State has pioneered new strategies in the fight for workers’ rights while helping turn Seattle into one of the most politically progressive and activist cities in the country. Recent minimum-wage, sick-leave, and secure-scheduling laws are only some of the victories that have been won by a coalition of social movements led by the State Labor Council and M.L. King County Labor Council. In this time when labor victories are rare and when strategies of resistance more important than ever, this panel will offer lessons from the state where labor and social justice movements have been aggressive, smart, and successful.

Nicole Grant is an electrician and Executive Secretary-Treasurer of the M.L. King County Labor Council

Jeff Johnson is President of the Washington State Labor Council representing 600 union locals and councils and 400,000 members

Senator Rebecca Saldana represents the 37th District in the Washington Legislator. She is the former Executive Director of Puget Sound Sage.

Nancy MacLean is a professor of History at Duke University and immediate past president of LAWCHA
