Labor Ideology and the Local Politics of Chinese Exclusion: The 1885 Chinese Expulsion in Tacoma, Washington Territory

Charles Williams
University of Washington Tacoma

Abstract

The expulsion of the Chinese community from Tacoma in 1885 was a prominent event in the wider anti-Chinese politics of the late 19th-century United States. This paper focuses on the labor ideologies espoused by key figures in the expulsion, including in particular the city mayor, Jacob Weisbach, who led the year-long campaign that culminated in the forced removal of all Chinese residents. By combining arguments tied to labor republicanism, a producer-controlled economy, and the threat of Chinese “slavery,” the expulsion reveals how the egalitarian rhetoric of labor radicalism could be turned to endorsing the repressive racial politics that integrated European immigrants into a white republic. The prominence of the mayor, a German immigrant and radical, further reveals how a local labor-based political coalition could frame anti-corporate and race-based politics as a unified vision in the face of an emerging industrial order, in this case embodied in the power of the Northern Pacific Railroad that dominated the city's economy. Understanding how such politics played out in Tacoma is not just a matter of tracing a representative experience, however, but instead points to the need to see how concrete political actors drew upon and modified inherited conceptions of racial hierarchy in response to specific economic and political conditions.
