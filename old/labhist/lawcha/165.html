The Frontlines of “Global” Movements: Re-considering Working-Class Struggle in the “Anti-Globalization” Movement

Eric Larson
University of Massachusetts Dartmouth

Abstract

How did working-class organizations participate in the Global Justice, or “Anti-Globalization,” movements of the 1990s and early 2000s, particularly in the U.S. context? Most scholarship on the movement has focused on non-governmental organizations, advocacy networks, and direct action collectives. This paper re-considers this scholarship in light of the activities of the Jobs with Justice coalition, a U.S.-based working-class coalition that sought to expand labor unions’ work with the networks of activists and organizations confronting sweeping neoliberal economic reforms. Like many grassroots groups that collaborated in the massive protests and world-scale fora of the era, the unions and organizations that led the Jobs with Justice coalition were frontline organizations, since they directly faced the consequences of multilateral trade agreements and the new policies of the World Trade Organization. Jobs with Justice, in trying to “localize global justice” to better respond to working-class needs, sought to re-adjust the scales of struggle to win concrete local battles even as relating struggles to transnational networks. This paper is based on interviews and archival work in local Jobs with Justice offices as well as in collections at the AFL-CIO and several key protest sites. It directly relates to the conference theme of Scales of Struggle, and also speaks to sub-themes of War and Empire, Borders and Coalitions, and Struggling for Justice.
