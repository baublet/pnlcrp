From Stewardess to Flight Attendant: Popular Culture, Workplace Relations and Women’s Quest for Workplace Dignity in the Canadian Airline Industry .

Joan Sangster
Trent University

Abstract

This paper draws on notions of the ‘stewardess’ in popular culture and interrogates these against other historical sources detailing flight attendants’ experience of the workplace. Although labor historians recognize the importance of probing the ideological context in which workers work, we tend to see popular culture as distorting or mystifying the ‘real’ world of work. In this paper, we acknowledge that popular culture can play a mystifying role, but we also argue that images created by popular culture nonetheless have an important impact on how jobs are perceived and experienced by workers, as well as how management shapes the workplace. While our example – flight attendants – may seem particularly rich in terms of popular culture context, we believe some of our conclusions about gender, race and class emerging from the paper might be extrapolated to other jobs. Our paper examines popular culture sources (advertising, novels, comic books, young adult fiction and movies) from the 1940s to the early 1980s, detailing how these created images of ‘the stewardess,’ with particular attention to gender roles, sexuality and the family. We examine both Canadian and American primary sources, indicating some of the cultural differences between this occupation as it evolved in these two countries – and why. We also examine images changed over time, and the way in which popular culture may have contained within it themes that later aided the resistance of flight attendants to their sexualization on the job. Finally, we contrast these popular sources to workplace grievances and conflicts over issues relating to sexuality and the family expressed through union battles, arbitration and media sources.
