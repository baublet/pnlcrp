Digital Tools for Labor Organizing & Labor History

Alex Morrow1, Richard Wells2, Veronique Emond-Sioufi3
1UW Tacoma, 2Van Arsdale Center for Labor Studies, Empire State College/SUNY, 3Simon Fraser University

Abstract

□ Class in Session: Teaching Labor History Through Digital Tools Students learn best when we invite them to be scholars and researchers. My presentation explores some of the techniques and sources I have been using to “flip the classroom” in labor studies courses at UW, Tacoma. These sessions of class utilize digital resources and open-source archives to engage students in various topics in the study of work and labor history. I want to highlight two projects in particular. The first utilizes Social Explorer, the online prescription database tool, which my students have utilized to create an economic report card of their region for the past three years. The second project invites students to create and revise topics in Wikipedia. To date, the majority of entries concerning labor strikes during the Great Depression were created by my students at UW and UW, Tacoma. These kinds of assignments help students transcend the boundaries of the campus and lends gravity to the otherwise abstract concepts we teach in the classroom.

□ Labor and Digital Media: Organizing the "Creative City" In the Spring of 2015, writers for the news and gossip website Gawker announced their intention to organize with the Writers’ Guild of America East (WGAE). Gawker’s management recognized the union without any resistance. Soon thereafter writers at other digital outlets, from Vice Media to Salon to The Huffington Post, sought and gained union recognition, some joining up with WGAE, others with the NewsGuild, and most through a routine card check. Observers saw in this burst of organizing an important sign of life for organized labor, not least because it involved a demographic thought to be immune to the attractions of trade unionism.

This paper takes a step back. First, I map this burst of organizing onto the transformation of the political economy of the news media. Then I examine the history of unionization in the cultural industry and in journalism to draw out the themes—such as the running tension between the romance of the writer’s life and the need for economic security, between an accommodating professionalism and a more oppositional trade unionism—that might best inform an understanding of the present situation. Finally, I will shift the perspective a bit. Since many of these workers are indeed young and college-educated, those “creative class” types that thanks to Richard Florida have fired the early 21st century urbanist imagination, does unionization in their ranks represent a new scale of struggle in the neo-liberal city? And how might it fit in with current efforts to re-think organized labor more generally?

□ Unions & Social Media Recent shifts in socioeconomic discourse have been widely perceived as opening up potential opportunities in a widespread union renewal effort. Given the digital media expansion that has run concurrent with labour’s decline, it seems clear that any potential shift toward a more dynamic and influential union movement will not only require significant social and ideological changes, but an active role on the part of labour activists and academics in the understanding and use of social media. This presentation will provide an overview of the academic research that has been done on unions’ use of social media and will highlight the challenges faced by the labour movement in utilizing social media as a means of expanding their profile and perceived significance to those sectors that could benefit from a larger role for labour. Finally, this presentation will explore potential lessons for the labour movement that can be drawn from research on social movements’ use of social media.
