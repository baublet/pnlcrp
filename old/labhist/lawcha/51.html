Not a labor Town: Transforming Labor and Gender Politics in a Deindustrialized City?

Jillian Crocker
SUNY Old Westbury

Abstract

What factors strengthen worker claims in labor-hostile cities? A case study of a 21st century nurses’ strike sheds light on this question. On Friday March 31st, 2000, approximately 535 nurses at St. Vincent’s Hospital and Worcester Medical Center in Worcester, Massachusetts went on strike against Tenet Healthcare Corporation over issues related to staffing and pay. The strike lasted 49 days and had broad support, including that of local and state lawmakers. When the strike concluded with satisfactory provisions on key issues, it was widely deemed a win for workers. In fact, that win gave strength to nurse organizing on safe staffing and mandatory overtime issues across the U.S. Despite this success, conventional wisdom remains that Worcester, once a diverse manufacturing center, is not a city that is friendly to labor. In this case study I analyze local, regional, and trade news coverage to offer a contextualized understanding of the factors that facilitated community support for this influential strike. In particular, the paper explores the ways in which the gendered, classed, and racialized notion of nurses as professionals and as care providers – for individuals as well as for communities – was deployed to situate these workers as altruistic advocates. While such strategies facilitate labor gains in the short term, they risk reproducing social divisions and inequalities that may limit their impact in the long term.
