Rethinking Precarious Work and Care through Janitorial Workers’ Struggle in South Korea

Jiwoon Yulee
University of Washington

Abstract

Neoliberal restructuring after the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis in South Korea produced a sharp division between regular and contingent workers, creating a new legal category of worker pijŏnggyujik. Examining female janitorial workers’ struggle in the context of the new precarious class formation, this paper discusses the deepening gap between those who perform unpaid care work and those who are perceived as valuable subjects of care in the neoliberal labor regime. Weaving the narratives of female janitorial workers who perform various forms of unpaid domestic care work for their young and old family members, this paper delineates changing boundaries between the public and the private spheres. It reveals how aging female bodies are situated in the chain between the paid labor and the role of a caretaker at home, and how they contribute to the crisis-driven economy through double surplus value creation in both realms. In doing so, I argue that the new precarity is instituted and acted upon the vulnerable bodies through the old forms of regionally specific patriarchal and capitalist social relations.
