The Ambiguities of Catholic Inter-racialism in 1950s Chicago: Friendship House and the Catholic Interracial Council

Ian Rocksborough-Smith
Corpus Christi College

Abstract

This paper examines the activities of the Catholic Interracial Council in Chicago as well its affiliated Friendship House from the mid-1940s through the 1950s under the liberal Bishop Bernard J. Sheil. Indeed, Sheil preached about the "doom" of racism in 1943 in light of the atrocities committed in Nazi Germany. This paper considers the relationship of these progressive Catholic institutions to the political dynamics of the Catholic archdiocese of Chicago and the U.S. Catholic church generally during the early years of the Cold War as well as the important missions the church had with working class migrant communities in the Windy City, particularly Polish and African American communities. It considers especially how issues of racial discrimination were discussed in the context of growing early Cold War acrimonies between the radical left and Catholic workers movements which often leaned towards conservative ends of the political spectrum and the complexities of ethnic and racial identities that came to light as a result of these dynamics.
