Abstract

Timber Unions and Social Policy in the Pacific Northwest

The wood products industry represented the industrial backbone of the Pacific Northwest economy for nearly all of the twentieth century. Two unions representing most of its workers, the International Woodworkers of America and the Lumber and Sawmill Workers affiliate of the United Brotherhood of Carpenters, built a strong system of pattern bargaining that raised wages for thousands of workers in the rural communities of British Columbia, Washington, Oregon and northern California.

In the latter part of the century union power was challenged as the industry evolved and environmental concerns led to increasing calls to limit the further cutting of timber in the region.

This panel explores the efforts of the two unions to engage the larger social issues affecting their members as a result of changing employment trends and increasing environmental restrictions on production.
