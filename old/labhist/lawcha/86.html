U.S. Labor’s Modernizing Mission: Exporting Industrial Pluralism in the “Development Decade”

Jeff Schuhrke
University of Illinois at Chicago

Abstract

This essay considers the relationship between U.S. labor and international development in the 1960s. It centers the AFL-CIO’s Cold War foreign activities on modernization theory and ties to the U.S. Agency for International Development, instead of on covert ops and ties to the CIA, as other histories have done. Labor and industrial relations were central components of America’s overseas modernization project in the 1960s. “Industrial pluralism”—the political and legal paradigm that defined U.S. labor relations from the end of World War II to the 1970s, which emphasized class collaboration, productivity, and limited state intervention—was one of the defining features of what industrial relations scholars, labor leaders, and liberal policymakers in the 1960s considered to be “modern.” They aimed to export this paradigm to the global South in the name of development and anti-Communism.
