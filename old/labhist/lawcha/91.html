A Great Vision: An Activist Family’s History in the Fight for Social Justice

Richard March
Independent Folklorist

Abstract

Personal accounts by people who have directly experienced historical events can provide vivid and empathetic insights into history. Typically such accounts in the form of memoirs encompass the life span of one individual. Multi-generational family histories that comprise multiple voices are very rare. This presentation synopsizes one such work, my recently-released book “A Great Vision” published by Hardball Press. There is the rich oral tradition of my family’s narrative, of our Old World struggles as Croatian nationalists resisting nascent Italian fascism in the turbulent northern Adriatic region, our immigration story and arrival in industrial Chicago. A vivid picture of life in the midst of 1930s organizing efforts emerges out of the extensive excerpts from oral history interviews with my father Herb March, a key organizer of Chicago’s packinghouse workers, and personal experience stories from my mother Jane March, an initiator of the Back-of-the-Yards Neighborhood Council. The tragic story of the fates of my three uncles who, in the depths of the Great Depression, after a decade in the USA, returned to Europe to join an American-founded commune in the Soviet Union provides a glimpse into the little-studied phenomenon of migration of Americans to the USSR. How family values of struggle were conveyed to ensuing generations is revealed in the account of my own participation in the civil rights, peace, and student New Left movements in Los Angeles, Berkeley and Mississippi, and of my niece Sara’s principled stand as a tree-sitting environmental activist in northern California. The family story is useful to historians and imbued with relevant issues such as trans-nationalism and gender dynamics.
