Labor Internationalism: Insiders and Outsiders

Brandon Williams1, Jill Jensen2, Eileen Boris3, Anna Sailer4
1University of California, Berkeley, 2Penn State University and University of Redlands, 3UC, Santa Barbara, 4Centre for Modern Indian Studies - Georg August University Goettingen

Abstract

It has been nearly thirty years since a pioneer in the study of international political economy, Robert Cox, published his book, Production, Power and World Order. Within, he assessed the “reciprocal relationship between power and production” based upon the organization of world labor forces, noting the political and economic “consequences of output.” Setting his study in an historical frame, Cox drew inferences from the International Labor Organization (ILO) where he had worked twenty-five years.

In a comparable way, this panel seeks to analyze the ILO via a set of provocative new questions concerning the dynamics of global production networks since the 1980s. What has happened within the international labor standards regime due to the transitions in power and leverage between labor unions, other movements in support of workers’ rights, and the “state” in various locales? What has been the influence of trends assessing labor value within global supply chains (“value chains”), including the value of female labor in relation to male, of workers in former colonial regions versus former metropoles, between racial minorities and majorities, or via the employment of migrants compared to citizens?
Finally, based upon the explosive rise of power in the form of the multinational corporation, what has been the effect on the current international labor standards regime?

India and Indonesia posed two of the earliest postwar challenges for a newly retooled International Labor Organization following WWII. How would a global organization advocate for workers while simultaneously supporting bids for economic sovereignty in leading postcolonial states? Drawing on sources from official archives in Geneva, New Delhi, and Jakarta, B.K. Williams’ paper interrogates the ILO’s relationship with India and Indonesia after each state achieved independence. It argues that the ILO cooperated with local governments to foster a political economy that produced a workforce that emerged as the vanguard of globalization. By boosting the fortunes of workers in the Global South, ironically, the ILO undermined the foundation of the United States’ Fordist economic order. How can historians explain the roots of income inequality and structural challenges facing American workers? The answer can be traced to the corridors of the ILO’s headquarters in Geneva and its field offices in New Delhi and Jakarta.

The notion of worldwide development, involving an articulation of knowledge and mobilization of power, was set in motion in the late 1950s and perfected in the decades that followed. As part of the UN Development Decade, the ILO sought to anchor development with basic human needs, also offering attention to workers’ human rights. Yet how did ILO ambitions stand up to the “economics of production” and heightened intensity of global economic expansion amid the global division of labor, by gender and geography? Developing nations were pressured to allow industrially advanced ones to help in the “modernization of the structures,” including the structures of production for profit. Thus, Jill Jensen’s paper looks at the ILO in the 1960s and 70s and the conflict over productivity and growth, on one hand, and “rights” and human worth on the other. Within, the ILO’s World Employment Program came into conflict with the rising US multinational enterprise.
