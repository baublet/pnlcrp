“The Question of ‘Self-Determination’”: HUAC Investigates the Specter of Cross-Border Communism in Texas, 1938-39

Adam Hodges
University of Houston - Clear Lake

Abstract

The creation of the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) in spring 1938 coincided with a wave of Communist Party (CP) activism in Texas toward unifying Mexican Americans and their organizations. This campaign sparked fears among anticommunists of the northward infiltration of communists from Mexico over a period of tolerance for the Party there during the leftist presidency of Lazaro Cardenas. The CP did not create this activism, which was fostered by the consular offices of Mexico in Texas, but it did actively seek to co-opt it. The stated goals of the consular project were the uplift and unity of Mexicans living in Texas to achieve both their well-being there and also aid, and in some essential skills cases return, to Mexico. Anticommunists feared that the permeable border would enable circulation of communists, including possibly thousands of returning Spanish Civil War veterans of the international brigades offered a home by the Cardenas government. Would an independent state of Mexican Americans closely allied with Mexico emerge during a revolutionary crisis? HUAC alarm over this possibility predates the escalation of anticommunism over fall 1939 to summer 1941 during the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact. HUAC investigation into fears of Mexican, not just Russian, communism and the potential radicalization and unification of the largest minority group of the Southwest, while African American activism escalated in the Southeast, inserts the southern border as a key component of our understanding of race and region in the evolution of anticommunism.
