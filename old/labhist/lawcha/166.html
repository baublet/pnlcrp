Labor History On-Line and On-Screen: Exploring the Possibilities of MOOCs and More

Alice Kessler-Harris1, Peter Kaufman2, Thavolia Glymph3, Toby Higbie4, Emily E. L.B. Twarog5
1Emerita-Columbia University, 2Intelligent Television and MIT, 3Duke University, 4University of California, Los Angeles, 5University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign

Abstract

A round-table built around a paper prepared by film/video/MOOC maker, Peter Kaufman. Kaufman challenges labor historians to produce a history of the American labor movement and its achievements that will reach out to every American. The core paper in this session outlines the possibilities of producing an online history of American labor that would be at once a MOOC, a museum display, a book, a documentary, a set of articles or documents, a raft of images and recordings. The notion is inspired by the late Roy Rosenzweig who, a decade ago, asked the historical profession whether history could, like Wikipedia, become free and open source. American Labor: A History could help fulfill this dream. Kaufman proposes a series produced and curated by leading labor historians and their institutions, involve a wide array of labor history archives, and be open to everyone and any archive or collection no matter how small. This would be an online course/display/collection/encyclopedia with three defining characteristics: it would be open, free, and alive.
