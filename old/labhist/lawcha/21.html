Hard-Hitting Songs by Hard-Hit People: The Music of Woody Guthrie and John Handcox, 1936-1941, A Performative Presentation

Michael Honey1, Darryl Holter2, Richard March3
1University of Washington Tacoma, 2University of Southern California, 3Independent Folklorist

Abstract

Woody Guthrie, born in 1912, fled the depression and the dustbowl of Oklahoma and migrated to the skid row of Los Angeles in 1937. He started a musical career that would produce hundreds of songs, among them some of America’s best-known folk ballads. Singing and talking to fellow immigrants through an LA radio program, he also made his way into the camps of migrant workers and identified his music with the American working class. Coming from a racist family background, he met African Americans and Latinos and became associated with the Popular Front of the Communist Party and became a strident anti-racist. John Handcox, born in Arkansas in 1904, grew up on a self-sufficient farm but his family hurtled down into poverty during the boll weevil and cotton crisis of the 1920s. When a team of runaway mules killed his father, John became head of a large family. Jim Crow laws and practices, lynching, and sharecropping combined to place him and his family into a deeply oppressed class of black agricultural workers. Bin 1936, he helped to organize the Southern Tenant Farmers Union, a remarkable bi-racial organized led by Socialist Party activists. Run out of Arkansas by a lynch mob after an STFU strike, John produced a flood or poems and songs for the movement. He became the “Sharecroppers’ Troubadour,” and like Woody Guthrie on the West Coast, his music helped to lift working-class organizing, but equally important, created a legacy of labor music that has infused American culture ever since. Guthrie and Handcox left behind memories of the Great Depression and multi-racial and multi-national working-class organizing that continues to inspire people to this day.

One cannot do justice to either of these singer-songwriter stories without performance that goes deeply down into the heart of their lives and their work. Darryl Holter, a Labor Studies scholar and teacher and now an independent business person, scholar, and singer, has researched, recorded and produced Guthrie’s seminal work, in Woody Guthrie LA, 1937-1941, and a remarkable CD by the same name. Michael Honey, a historian of African American and Labor History at the University of Washington Tacoma, wrote Sharecroppers’ Troubadour: John L. Handcox and the African-American Song Tradition (Palgrave MacMillan, 2013), and has co-written songs with Handcox and set a number of his poems to music. Holter and Honey will tell and sing about these working-class artists helped to forge a musical literature of protest that blazed a path for subsequent generations of singers from Bob Dylan, Bruce Springsteen, to Pete Seeger and Sweet Honey in the Rock. These singing and song-writing radicals helped to ignite the organizing of the Popular Front era, and left an artistic legacy the informs struggles for justice today that will be explored through story and song in this performative presentation.
