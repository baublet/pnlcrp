Part-time Faculty and Collective Bargaining Outcomes

Dan Jacoby
University of Washington

Abstract

Controversy swirls over the extent to which faculty unionization benefits part-time or contingent faculty. Many contingent faculty are dubious that bargaining units controlled by full time or tenure track faculty do or can represent their interests. Indeed, not all tenure track faculty embrace the idea of regularizing appointments for contingent faculty or improving their conditions lest that weaken the institution of tenure or the conditions tenured and full time faculty experience. However, very little data analysis has been presented in order to indicate contemporary outcomes related to contingent faculty. By merging several data bases including the National Center for the Study of Higher Education's Directory of Collective Bargaining we are able to begin assessing some outcomes including the extent to which results diverge where contingent faculty are a) not represented b) where they are including in general units with tenure track faculty, and c) where they create separate units. Where the data allows, this paper will focus on earning and upon the reliance of institutions upon part-time faculty.
