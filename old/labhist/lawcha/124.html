To Defuse a "Powder Keg": Historicizing the 2016 Milwaukee Uprising

Will Tchakirides1, Joe Walzer1, Wes Sutermeister2, John Terry3, Robert S. Smith1
1UW-Milwaukee, 2Marquette University, 3South Texas College

Abstract

On August 13, 2016, Milwaukee, Wisconsin joined Ferguson, Baltimore, and other American cities in a new wave of racial unrest. Following yet another violent black death at the hands of the police, the "Sherman Park Uprising" saw confrontations between law enforcement and disaffected residents, particularly black youth, last well into the night. As businesses burned and arrests mounted, city leaders organized an impromptu news conference to publicly sort out what transpired in the northwest side neighborhood. Mayor Tom Barrett and Common Council President Ashanti Hamilton updated reporters first, detailing the city’s efforts to restore order and admonishing parents to get a hold of their recalcitrant teens. Their comments reflected the conventional wisdom that unrest in predominantly black urban spaces derives from community shortcomings and the prevailing crisis in police-community relations. This view has largely shaped civic responses to the Sherman Park Uprising and has helped justify a recent "Public Safety Plan" that proposes to dramatically increase Milwaukee's police force, administer longer prison sentences, and establish "boot camps" for "at-risk" youth among other measures.

Conversely, Alderpersons Khalif Rainey and Russell Stamper II offered a powerful alternative narrative: the Sherman Park Uprising reflects a "powder keg" of inequities and injustices that Black Milwaukeeans disproportionately experience daily, including alarming rates of unemployment, mass incarceration, and under-education. "The black people of Milwaukee … are tired of living under this oppression," Rainey explained, calling for the city's racial problems to "not [be] examined, but rectified... immediately."

By suggesting that the unrest is rooted in racial and economic problems, beyond (but including) the scope of policing, Rainey, Stamper, and local community activists identified the "Milwaukee Uprising" as part of a larger struggle against capitalism's uneven landscapes of power and privilege---a process that largely tracks congruent lines of race and class in American cities. In this view, the "crisis of policing" is not in how the police "get along" with communities, but in the legitimacy of the police institution itself and its ability to meet a growing list of vital community needs and demands. This roundtable aims to square these countervailing narratives, unspooling and analyzing different threads of Milwaukee's "Sherman Park Uprising" specifically and the role of policing and surveillance in American society more broadly; it does so from a range of inter-related perspectives.
