Negotiating Neoliberalism: Working-Class Activism and Race in the Late Twentieth Century

Marisa Chappell1, Eddie Bonilla2, Michael Reagan3, Nancy MacLean4
1Oregon State University, 2Michigan State University, 3University of Washington, 4Duke University

Abstract

This panel features three very different approaches to considering the role of working-class communities in neoliberalism’s emerging ascendancy in the 1970s and 1980s. In his exploration of New York City’s 1970s austerity policies as an elite reaction to racial justice activism, Michael Reagan argues that working-class communities of color served as powerful symbols in a campaign to undermine redistributive policies and other alternative approaches to fiscal crisis. In Reagan’s story, working-class New Yorkers were both tools and victims in an elite neoliberal project. In contrast, Eddie Bonilla and Marisa Chappell ask how working-class urban residents themselves engaged with shifting political and policy environments in the 1970s and 1980s. Bonilla explores radical leftist Chicano activists in California whose inability to negotiate the evolving complexities of gender and immigration status in the postindustrial labor market left them without an effective political strategy. Chappell, on the other hand, explores low-income activists’ engagement in both challenging and contributing to narratives of ownership and choice that fueled neoliberal policymaking. Taken together, the papers offer not only different but perhaps conflicting answers to fundamental questions regarding the role of working class actors in the emergence of neoliberal political and policy regimes in the American postindustrial city.
