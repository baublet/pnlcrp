The Art of Photographing Work in the Twentieth Century

Kate Sampsell-Willmann1, Carole Quirke2, Ed Slavishak3, Fred Lonidier4
1Tacoma Community College, 2SUNY, Old Westbury, 3Susquehanna University, 4UCSD Visual Arts Dept. & AFT

Abstract

Photography itself is industrial labor. Only occasionally has the camera been turned on the worker as a subject of importance. The members of this panel want to examine how work (and its absence) were been depicted photographically in the twentieth century.

The camera changed the landscape of cultural production. Photography as mechanical reproduction became part of the industrial assembly line from design through production to marketing and advertising. Visual communication became dominant in the twentieth century, especially a style of visual storytelling that featured careful composition by photographers close to their subjects and presented as unmanipulated, “straight” images, which could be confused with unmediated reality and thus evidence of the veracity of what was depicted. This form of communication became especially important in the period dominated by glossy photomagazines, but the style originated somewhere and carried on, inspiring diverse innovation. In this panel, Kate Sampsell-Willmann will endeavor to define what is meant by the term “social documentary” and outline its intellectual boundaries as initiated in Lewis Hine’s “social photography” of child labor. Carol Quirke will present her recent research on the photography of Arthur Leipzig, who represented workers in deeply textured documentary photographs for major corporate clients, and Ed Slavishak will turn to Appalachia in mid-century at the time when the region was in economic crisis to reinterpret work outside of employment.
