Struggling with the State: Farmworker Activism and the Promises and Perils of Gov't Policy

Erin Conlin1, Andrew Hazelton2, Larisa Veloz3, John Weber4
1Indiana University of Pennsylvania, 2Texas A&M International University, 3University of Texas El Paso, 4Old Dominion University

Abstract

This panel posits farmworkers in the United States, both foreign and domestic, face similar challenges yet employ different forms of resistance in response to the constrained choices available to them at the time of a particular labor conflict. In this panel, Erin L. Conlin explores how Bahamian guestworkers responded to employment opportunities and subsequent conditions in the United States, from the 1940s through the 1960s, and how those experiences helped shape their emerging labor consciousness at home and abroad. Andrew J. Hazelton examines how the National Farm Labor Union in the United States attempted to extend labor law coverage to farmworkers in the 1950s, even though they were denied boycott protection under the Taft-Hartley Act.

Paper 1: In 1942 the Bracero program, a bi-nationally created temporary labor program sent over 4.2 million Mexican migrants to the United States. The parameters of program participation demanded that migrants would be temporary, male, young, and able bodied. This was a challenge to the decades long tradition of family migration in which migrants of all ages and both genders often migrated in family groups to take on enduring and repeating migrations together. Yet, the human nature of migration would not be so easily re-ordered. In fact, when ever possible, women and families would counter this re-ordering and would undertake their own migrations to the United States. This paper will explore how the actions of family members and women constituted a form of resistance to an imposed and engineered labor system that explicitly undermined a pattern of human migration that had always been social and familial at its core.

Paper 2: In June 1942 Bahamian workers in Nassau shattered windows along Bay Street, the economic and political capital of the island nation, protesting the Jim Crow-wage system utilized by the United States as it built air and naval bases on New Providence. A year later, foreign agricultural guestworkers in Florida rioted, with some news outlets reporting workers were celebrating the anniversary of the Burma Road Riot. Taken together, these events mark a key moment of labor consciousness in the Bahamas.

This paper examines Bahamian guestworkers' bitter sweet experiences in the temporary foreign labor program (“The Contract”), which the Bahamas and United States engaged in from 1943 to 1966. It explores how participants saw The Contract as an opportunity to secure better wages and see the world, yet were often severely disappointed when confronted with American racism and deplorable living and/or working conditions. It explains how as workers' developed a more profound labor consciousness they invented new forms of struggle to air their grievances and seek remediation.

Paper 3: In the 1960s and 1970s, the United Farm Workers used consumer and secondary boycotts to protest agricultural employers’ unfair labor practices and strikebreaking tactics in California. However, the Farmworker Movement could pursue some of these boycott tactics only because farmworkers were excluded from the nation’s labor laws, which restricted boycott activities after the passage of the Taft-Hartley Act.

This paper examines a critical moment in the legal status of farmworker unions by focusing on the early 1950s. During the DiGiorgio Strike, the National Farm Labor Union was enjoined from boycott activities under Taft-Hartley despite the fact that the workers it represented were not covered by that act. Arguing that the law was being used to penalize farmworkers while simultaneously withholding benefits like collective bargaining, the right to strike, and National Labor Relations Board procedures, the National Farm Labor Union waged an unsuccessful battle to extended labor law coverage to farmworkers.
