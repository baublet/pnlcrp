Political Struggles over Workers’ Well-Being in Industrial America

Dave Obringer1, Alan Derickson2, Elaine Bernard3
1Edinboro University, 2Pennsylvania State University, 3Harvard Law School

Abstract

1) Obringer: Pennsylvania passed the first child labor law in 1848. Embedded with this law was a 10 hour work day provision. In 1849 the law changed. Surrounding the passage of these laws was the labor movement's call for a ten-hour day. This paper will attempt to distinguish whether the factors that convinced the government to pass these laws was born of the protests of working people, or pressures from elsewhere.

2) Derickson: The proposed paper addresses a struggle over workers’ right to know about health hazards they faced on the job. In the 1910s, New York set a progressive policy of providing access to hazard information such as the findings of state inspections and the reports of occupational disease cases filed by physicians. Prior to the 1930s, Connecticut was the only state impounding that sort of evidence of hazrds. New York placed regulation of occupational health under its state labor department; Connecticut gave that authority to its state health agency. A clause in the Social Security Act promoted development of occupational health capability in state health departments by offering funding to establish new industrial-hygiene units, shifting the scale of public-policy struggle over this matter to the national-government level. As states pursued the federal funds and followed the Public Health Service’s guidance to emulate Connecticut, the labor movement fought back by defending the state-level labor agencies. At the end of the 1930s, liberal Senator James Murray offered a proposal to allow state labor departments to share in the Social Security grants. The battle over his bill brought to light the injustice of supporting health bureucracies that refused to share life-and-death information with the workers at risk.

3) Barclay examines how white, disabled working class men of Western Pennsylvania reformulated or reinscribed masculinity during the collapse of the steel industry in the late 1970s and early 1980s.
