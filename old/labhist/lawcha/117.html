Bridging the Class Divide?: How Class Dynamics Shape Labor-Environmental Coalitions in Rural Areas

Daisy Rooks
University of Montana

Abstract

In recent years activism around climate change, energy development, and green jobs has caught the attention of scholars and media outlets. Labor, environmental, and community organizations have endorsed large-scale demonstrations in urban areas, and have mobilized their members to attend. In the past few months, protests against the Dakota Access Pipeline suggest that labor, environmental, and community organizations are collaborating on environmental issues in rural areas as well. This paper explores the role of class in these rural coalitions, asking whether and how the class dynamics in rural coalitions are distinct from their urban counterparts.

The scholarly literature on labor-environmental coalitions has demonstrated that divergent interests and discordant organizational cultures make it challenging for labor and environmental organizations to work together. While labor unions’ interests are generally economic, environmental groups focus almost exclusively on the natural environment. Although labor unions are organized hierarchically, most involve their members in their day-to-day operations, inviting them to weigh in on major decisions and policy changes. This stands in stark contrast to environmental groups, which tend to be non-hierarchical but provide limited opportunities for members to influence day-to-day operations and influence important decisions (Andrews 2005; Longo and Baker 2014; Minchin 2002; Orbach 2004; Payne 2013). There is limited but convincing evidence that the class composition of labor and environmental organizations contributes to these dissimilar organizational cultures.

This paper draws upon three case studies in order to understand the role of class in rural labor-environmental coalitions. I conducted in-depth interviews with 21 activists involved in three labor-environmental coalitions: one addressing development and tourism in Montana, one tackling oil drilling in North Dakota, and one confronting pipeline construction in Nebraska.

My preliminary analysis suggests that class plays a different role in rural coalitions than it does in urban and suburban areas. Despite the divergent class backgrounds of their leaders and members, the labor and environmental organizations involved in the rural coalitions described above, had a great deal in common. Their organizational cultures diverged far less than the scholarly literature would suggest. Rural labor organizations in the three states had less combative, hierarchical cultures, and rural environmental organizations in the three states provided more avenues for robust member involvement, than scholars have predicted. Even more telling, members and leaders of the two types of organizations tended to share hobbies and recreational activities in rural areas. The time that they spent hunting, fishing and hiking in public lands shaped their organizations’ commitments to preserving the rural character and natural beauty of their local communities. As the analysis proceeds, I expect to find additional evidence of the centrality of class to these rural coalitions.

While the Green Jobs initiatives of the mid-2000s and labor’s robust participation in the 2014 People’s Climate March suggest the tremendous potential of labor-environmental coalitions, the fevered pitch of fracking and pipeline debates indicate their fragility. In order to understand the potential and limits of collaboration between labor and environmental groups in rural areas, we must understand whether and how class shapes their internal dynamics.
