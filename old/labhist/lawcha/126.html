Power Struggles: Workers, Business, and the Political Economy of Natural Resource Extraction

Jonathon Free1, Sarah Stanford-McIntyre2, Jacob Remes3, Trish Kahle4
1Duke University, 2College of William & Mary, 3New York University, 4University of Chicago

Abstract

Energy is so fundamental to our lives that we often take it for granted. But each time we flip a light switch or fill our cars with gas, we enter a chain of relationships connecting us to the workers, businesses, and natural forces that produced the power we expect to harness. Historians have long recognized the importance of those networks, but the political and environmental challenges of our time make them even more salient. As human-induced climate change inches toward catastrophe and the shifting geography of natural resource extraction fosters economic insecurity and political resentment among working people, a growing number of scholars are searching for new ways of understanding the history of energy. 

    This panel contributes to that trend by presenting three studies of

work, labor relations, and politics in the oil and coal industries. In papers that range from the early twentieth century to the 1980s, we examine forces that shaped conflicts on the local, national, and global scale. Through her study of oil production in the Texas Permian Basin, Sarah Stanford-McIntyre argues that working-class drillers developed an intimate knowledge of ecology and geology. That expertise not only provided a sense of power and prestige for workers in a dangerous and rapidly changing industry, but also bolstered ambivalent attitudes toward the environmental consequences of their work. Likewise, Jon Free shows how growing concern over fuel shortages in the early 1970s impacted coal miners’ efforts to control their workplaces through democratic revolt. The energy crisis—which Free argues predated the 1973 OPEC oil embargo—both increased and limited miners’ negotiating power, as the Miners for Democracy and wildcat strike movements butted against financial imperatives at the heart of the business of coal mining. 

    By examining the nexus of work, business, and energy on various scales,

our papers combine insights gleaned from generations of labor and working-class historians with new perspectives afforded by environmental studies, critical geography, and the history of capitalism. Thus this panel points toward a deeper and more nuanced understanding of power; one that acknowledges the contingency and conflict embedded in the most elemental aspects of human history.
