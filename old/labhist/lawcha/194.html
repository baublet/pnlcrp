Shifting Terrains of Labor Relations

Joshua Davis1, Declan O'Reilly2, Jesse Halvorsen3, Colin Davis4
1University of Baltimore, 2University of East Anglia, 3UCSB, 4University of Alabama-Birmingham

Abstract

□ The Rise and Fall of Union Cooperatives Labor unions’ fluctuating engagement with co-ops stands out as one of the bigger mysteries of both organized labor and cooperative economics. Why did a business form that once so appealed to unions lose its attract for them, to the point that today connections between organized labor and co-ops are few and far between? This is the central question I will explore in this paper.

□ The Tragedy of the 1971 IRA: British Labour Relations and Modern Conservatism The 1971 industrial relations Act was the most contentious piece of UK labour law in history. It caused considerable industrial unrest in Britain during the less than two years of its operation and was repealed by the Wilson Labour government in 1974. However, the 1971 IRA was the first genuine attempt at comprehensive modern labour reform in Britain, this paper seeks to contextualise it with Britain changing political landscape of the 1970s and wider European events at the same time.

□ Men & Machines: Labor, Business, & the State's Approach to Automation, Productivity, Collective Bargaining, & Jurisdictional Disputes at West Coast Ports, 1957-1974 My paper will examine how transportation workers in Southern California in the 1960s and 1970s both contested and eventually accommodated significant developments in labor saving technology, regulatory reform, and industry practices in longshoring, short haul trucking, and warehousing. Far from a benign march of progress centered on market efficiencies, productivity gains, and whiggish sense of inevitability, the very terms for port modernization and its interrelated goods handling infrastructure served as a site of struggle between business and labor. The outcome of these transformations somewhat paradoxically contained the ILWU’s militancy and capacity to the docks while it simultaneously facilitated the dispersal of drayage and warehouse infrastructure and work to the urban fringe in Southern California’s Inland Empire. This process of geographic reorganization of regional transportation infrastructure, then, coupled with competitive pressures and volatility in the trucking and warehouse sectors from significant regulatory reform, eroded the Teamsters’ organizational strength and ability to exert some measure of control over the working conditions and hiring practices in short haul trucking and warehouse work. While ports and warehouses may at first seem only tangentially related, I will argue that they are symbiotic parts of a complex regional whole that plays an important part in facilitating the global movement of goods, and serve as an important site of struggle for transportation workers.

This is not to say that Southern California is exceptional, rather as transportation hubs across the United States, indeed the world, modernized to accommodate the shipping container, adapted to a highly competitive deregulated environment, and adapted business practices to exploit gaps in employment classification and labor law, these developments reshaped the global movement of goods. Since these transformations hold enormous implications for most productive sectors, this also broadly holds significance for where work is done, by whom, and under what conditions. This allows firms to benefit from not only significantly reduced time and cost of transporting goods, but allowed for more precision, predictability, and the ability to forecast and project needs in logistics and distribution. And though these connections are far from readily apparent and are further obscured by the disaggregated nature of production characteristic of postwar business practices, my study will detail how transportation workers in Southern California contested these larger processes, and struggled to retain a measure of control over geographically fragmented work, and how workers contested, and then adapted to work in these new conditions.
