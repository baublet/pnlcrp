World War I and Shifting Currents of Gender, Class and Ethnic Anti-Colonialism

Heather Mayer1, Aaron Goings2, David Brundage3, Cecelia Bucki4
1Portland Community College, 2Saint Martin's University, 3University of California, Santa Cruz, 4Fairfield University

Abstract

□ Female Wobblies in Wartime

World War I was a tumultuous time for the Industrial Workers of the World in the Pacific Northwest. In addition to the massive trial of Wobbly leadership in Chicago, and the smaller but infamous cases of Louise Olivereau and Marie Equi and Seattle and Portland, little-known Wobbly women were also at risk during this period.

Female Wobblies risked arrest for simple membership in the organization during the repressive years of the Red Scare. While the male leadership was rounded up in raids across the country, women were arrested for supporting the organization behind the scenes by editing and distributing the Industrial Worker, providing support for men in jail, and working with defense organizations to raise money for trial expenses and to support the families of those in jail. While women’s work in jail support and defense is important, it was typical for women to be supportive behind-the-scenes, rather than as frontline agitators. The authorities, however, recognized this support as essential to the Wobbly cause, and therefore women were targets of federal surveillance as much as any of the well-known male Wobblies of the period. This paper examines how the massive arrests affected Wobbly communities in the Pacific Northwest and how women worked to support those in jail and risked arrest themselves.

□ Finnishing the History of the 1910s Pacific Northwest Lumber Wars This paper will provide a brief overview of the key events and themes of the 1910s labor upsurge in lumber, easily the Pacific Northwest’s largest industry. It will focus special attention on the central place of Finnish immigrant men and women in the making of the great 1917 Lumber Strike, the post-strike efforts to maintain the Northwest Industrial Workers of the World (IWW, or Wobbly) movement, and the violent repression experienced by strikers and their supporters. In so doing, I will be exploring the internal politics of the IWW lumber workers’ movement, looking at how identities of race, gender, ethnicity, and politics were negotiated by the radical group, and how those negotiations shaped the boundaries of union, socialist, and Wobbly movements in the Pacific Northwest. It concludes that the 1917 strike and subsequent IWW struggles were the product of years of previous labor and left-wing organizing, which, in several Northwest lumber towns communities, was based among Finnish immigrants.

A close examination of Finnish workers’ activism during the Pacific Northwest labor conflicts yields richer and more accurate understandings of labor relations in the Northwest’s biggest industry during the early twentieth century. Additionally, emphasizing the place of women Finnish radicals will help restore Finnish-American working people to the center of Pacific Northwest labor and women’s history.

□ Civil Liberties and Anticolonialism in a Time of War: The National Labor Defense Council, 1917-18 Founded by labor lawyer Frank P. Walsh shortly after the US entry into World War I, the National Labor Defense Council (NLDC) has been all but forgotten. Avoided by most pro-war progressives and ridiculed by AFL president Samuel Gompers, the poorly-funded NLDC proved even less effective in defending antiwar activists than Roger Baldwin’s National Civil Liberties Bureau (forerunner of the ACLU). Nonetheless, as my paper will show, the links that the NLDC forged between antiwar labor radicals and anticolonial activists (especially those working within America’s small South Asian population)—along with Walsh’s own experiences defending such activists—paved the way for a vibrant post-war anticolonial coalition, exemplified by the founding of the broadly-based League of Oppressed Peoples in 1919 and the emergence of the Farmer-Labor political upsurge of 1920.

Drawing heavily on the voluminous Frank P. Walsh collection in the New York Public Library, my paper provides an elaboration of themes that I discuss in my forthcoming essay, “The Easter Rising and New York’s Anticolonial Nationalists,” in Ireland’s Allies: America and the 1916 Easter Rising, ed. Miriam Nyhan Grey (University College Dublin Press, 2016).
