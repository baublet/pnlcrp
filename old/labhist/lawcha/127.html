WalMart University: A Precarious Faculty and a Precarious Education Framing the Conversation, Organizing the Struggle

Claire Goldstene1, Maria Maisto2, Ricardo Lopez3
1New Faculty Majority, 2Cuyahoga Community College and New Faculty Majority, 3Western Washington University

Abstract

The growth in the numbers of part-time faculty is one expression of the increasing corporatization of universities. Currently, a majority of college teachers in the county is temporarily employed, often with no more than 15 weeks of job security, and working for low pay, with no health or retirement benefits. Along with this casualization of labor, however, are attacks on faculty governance and a devaluation of a liberal arts education. Rising numbers of contingent faculty have undermined the status of all faculty and allowed universities to more easily adopt corporate agendas and priorities. Departments and faculty who have opposed the neo-liberalization of the university have often been targeted for removal. The problems faced by temporary instructors cannot be addressed separately from the broader transformation of higher education. WalMart University is here. We propose a roundtable discussion, organized around a series of very short presentations and generative questions to explore the historical, political, and institutional forces driving this move toward a “faculty-free” university. As Paulo Freire continually warned: Education is not neutral; educators are accept the status quo or oppose it. The current changes in the administration of universities affect not only faculty and students, but society more broadly because they alter the very nature of the educational experience. The panel, comprised of historians, activists, and adjuncts, will offer a variety of perspectives on this salient topic. To extrapolate from Marx, in a postindustrial economy education constitutes an essential factor of production and, as such, illuminates the operation of political and economic power in this neoliberal moment. Labor conditions among higher education faculty mirror national trends for many workers – minimal-to-no job security, low pay, few benefits, and little room for advancement. Not surprisingly, women and people of color are disproportionately excluded from stable jobs. Further, since most faculty now teach without the financial and intellectual protections of tenure threatens, academic freedom is threatened and the university no longer serves as a public sphere for the open exchange of ideas.
All of this speaks to the political contentiousness of education, its meaning, and purpose. The financial precariousness of faculty is deeply enmeshed with the increasing precariousness of education itself. As colleges come to depend more on more on faculty that occupy positions of both financial and intellectual insecurity, and faculty governance collapses, the foundations of democracy in our society are seriously undermined.
