Organizing Among Professionals and Service Sector Workers in Canada & the United States: Past and Present

Amanda Walter1, Julia Smith2, Joan Sangster3, Liesl Orenic4
1Wayne State University, 2Rutgers University, 3Trent University, 4Dominican University

Abstract

□ Relationship-Based Organizing: AFSCME's Clerical Unionization Campaigns at the University of Minnesota By the 1970s, clerical workers in higher education increasingly reached out to the large unions for help in bargaining, as they encountered a lack of job security, reorganization of work, and difficult bargaining conditions due, in part, to diminishing state funding for universities, and turned to affiliation and unionization to strengthen the workers’ power. AFSCME, which represented more university office workers than any other union nationally, hoped to appeal to higher education clericals by drawing on their long experience in the public sector and paying attention to the majority women clerical workers’ gender-specific needs. Through an examination of the University of Minnesota, Relationship-Based Organizing: AFSCME’s Clerical Unionization Campaigns at the University of Minnesota shows AFSCME’s tactical change in unionization campaigns, switching from unsuccessfully focusing on campaign literature in the 1982 campaign to a highly successful person-to-person organizing model in the 1989-1991 campaign. The tactical change drew lessons from the model employed by the AFSCME’s clerical organizing campaign at Harvard University and the working women’s movement. The working women’s movement had a tremendous impact on the labor movement, including style of organizing, what workplaces could be seen as unionization targets, and what issues should be prioritized in campaigns and contracts. This research, based on AFSCME archival collections at the Walter Reuther Library at Wayne State University, the University Archives at University of Minnesota, and oral histories with union organizers, provides depth to the largely unknown history of the university workplace, working women’s movement, and their union entry into the clerical organizing. Both the local case study and the national context of organizing will be examined.

□ Jurisdictional Conflicts and the Battle for Canadian Bank Workers in the 1970s To contribute to the conference theme of “Scales of Struggle” and the sub-themes of “Borders and Coalitions” and “Struggling for Justice,” this paper will examine the conflicts that occurred within the Canadian labour movement in the 1970s over union organizing in the banking industry. This period witnessed the greatest surge in bank worker unionization in Canadian history. Initially, the unions and labour organizations involved worked separately but alongside one another. Things changed in 1977 though, when two independent unions successfully challenged a federal labour board ruling on single-branch bargaining units. With branch certification now possible, large unions and labour organizations that had failed or not bothered to organize bank workers turned their attention to the banks. Bitter inter-union battles soon erupted, most notably between the country’s largest labour federation – the Canadian Labour Congress (CLC) – and a small feminist union – the Service, Office, and Retail Workers’ Union of Canada (SORWUC). As unions expended time, energy, and resources debating who had jurisdiction over bank employees, the banks waged a steady war against unionization in the branches, in the media, and in the courts and, in the end, emerged victorious. The labour movement’s inability and unwillingness to overcome ideological differences and jurisdictional issues played a key role in the failure of the drives. As such, the battle for Canadian bank workers that occurred in the 1970s serves as a useful case study of why jurisdictional conflicts occur and how they harm workers and the labour movement.

□ From Stewardess to Flight Attendant: Popular Culture, Workplace Relations and Women’s Quest for Workplace Dignity in the Canadian Airline Industry This paper draws on notions of the ‘stewardess’ in popular culture and interrogates these against other historical sources detailing flight attendants’ experience of the workplace. Although labor historians recognize the importance of probing the ideological context in which workers work, we tend to see popular culture as distorting or mystifying the ‘real’ world of work. In this paper, we acknowledge that popular culture can play a mystifying role, but we also argue that images created by popular culture nonetheless have an important impact on how jobs are perceived and experienced by workers, as well as how management shapes the workplace. While our example – flight attendants – may seem particularly rich in terms of popular culture context, we believe some of our conclusions about gender, race and class emerging from the paper might be extrapolated to other jobs. Our paper examines popular culture sources (advertising, novels, comic books, young adult fiction and movies) from the 1940s to the early 1980s, detailing how these created images of ‘the stewardess,’ with particular attention to gender roles, sexuality and the family. We examine both Canadian and American primary sources, indicating some of the cultural differences between this occupation as it evolved in these two countries – and why. We also examine images changed over time, and the way in which popular culture may have contained within it themes that later aided the resistance of flight attendants to their sexualization on the job. Finally, we contrast these popular sources to workplace grievances and conflicts over issues relating to sexuality and the family expressed through union battles, arbitration and media sources.
