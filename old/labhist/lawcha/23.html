A Worker Writes History?

Robert Rossi
LAWCHA

Abstract

I have been working on a social history of Colorado coal miners in the 1920s and an account of the 1927-28 Colorado miners' strike since 1982. I am not an academic and I do not have academic experience; I have done my research and writing as a union organizer and left activist, a worker and as someone whose family members and friends worked in the mines. Most of my research has been done with Colorado miners and their families and most of these people are now deceased. Certain questions have arisen from my research: can the assembly forms of workers' organizations be made relevant today? how do we understand and work with the pivotal people and institutions which make workers' mass movements possible? how do we understand the connections and lack of connections between workers' (sub)cultures, work communities and communities and our ability and willingness to act? How do workers live between dramatic moments of heightened class struggle? These questions have arisen from my study of Colorado miners in the 1920s, and I think that I am finding answers. More broadly, other questions have arisen as well as I have done my work: can an "ordinary" worker write working-class history? what do union activists get out of labor and working-class history? why do we do working-class history and how do we communicate the "why" of this to other workers? Does Brecht's poem "A Worker Reads History" have meaning today? NOTE: I'M GOOD WITH BEING PART OF A PANEL OR TALKING ABOUT THIS IN ANY FORMAT.
