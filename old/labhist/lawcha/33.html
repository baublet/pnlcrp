Activism and Social Movements in Québec: A Transnational Perspective

Mathieu Houle-Courcelles1, Camille Robert2, Benoit Marsan3, Victor Silverman4
1Université Laval, 2Université du Québec à Montréal, 3Université du Québec à Montréal et Université du Québec en Outaouais, 4Pomona College

Abstract

With the vast majority of its population speaking French, Québec represents a peculiar case in North America. This situation has deeply influenced the development of ideas and practices on its territory, including workers, nationalist and student movement. The impact of this geographical and historical specificity is also reflected in the province’s political and national history, which has long emphasised the exceptional nature of Québec’s development compared to English-speaking Canada and, more largely, North America. While such tendencies has often produced closed historiographical debates, social historians interested in working class history and social movements history have shown a greater appreciation for wider, more encompassing spatial scales. Importantly, they recognise that social movements in Québec are in constant dialogue with other movements in Europe and other part of North America. This panel proposes to question social movements and activism in Québec by taking into account its transnational dimension in order to see Québec’s through an international prism. Paper 1 On May 1, 1906, more than 1,000 people took the streets in Montreal to mark International Worker’s Day. The event had considerable impact on local politics. For the first time, socialists and anarchists demonstrated in Canada's metropolis, red flags flying. But far from being a strictly local event, this action was part of a wider mobilization straddling Europe and North America. Through this presentation, we will try to understand the links between Montreal activists and transnational militant networks. This analysis will allow us to better understand the complexity of their commitment and the scope of their demands, while shedding new light on the people of various ethnic and political backgrounds who were involved in this demonstration. Paper 2 As in many other Western societies, Quebec witnessed a feminist resurgence at the turning of the 70s. While access to abortion and contraception are generally retained as the most marking struggles of this second wave of feminism, several groups developed, during this same period, discourses and activism around the issue of housework. In Quebec, feminists demanded a recognition of the social value of housework, with some even going on to build connections with the international movement Wages for Housework. For other feminists, it was more through socialization, by the collective sharing of housework, that women would be liberated from the burden of housework. Finally, certain women’s organizations focused on government reforms in order to reduce the financial insecurity of mothers and housewives. These debates were all influenced by the intellect of American, Italian, French and English feminists. We wish to reflect on this period in Quebec’s feminist movement, by highlighting this aforementioned intellectual input on Quebecker feminist activism. Paper 3 During the Great Depression Era, as unemployment take critical proportions never seen before in Canada, unemployed people put forward a rich repertoire of individual and collective protests. At the time, no Canadian wide measures exist to help the working class cope with unemployment. Relief and public works are administered by municipalities. In such a context, this paper offer to study Montreal’s example to illustrate that beyond appearances of a local based unemployed movement, that emerge out of necessity, in reality, it possesses larger ramifications that interweave at different geographic scales, from Montreal’s working class neighborhoods to the international stage, passing by provincial and federal level. Those extents have many repercussions at different degrees on protest forms, claims, gender relations, ethnicities, religions, class formation and solidarities.
