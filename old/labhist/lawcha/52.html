Sparks Fly at Seattle City Light: Forging Links Between Workers to Combat Discrimination

Megan Cornish1, Jerry Lawson2, Ellie Belew3
1Pioneering electrical tradeswoman at Seattle City Light. Lifelong feminist and radical activist., 225-year veteran at City Light; one of first Black crew chiefs in Underground division., 3Community historian writing history of electrical tradeswomen at City Light

Abstract

Dramatizing the conference sub-theme "Struggling for Justice," this workshop will recount a crucial fight for gender justice in the Pacific Northwest that successfully built solidarity across lines of race, sexuality, and job status. The organizing effort connected labor and community movements, and drew on this dynamic to implement affirmative action and advance union support for women and people of color in the trades.

In 1974, ten women entered the electrical utility trades at Seattle City Light through a unique Electrical Trades Trainee (ETT) program designed by a well-known local socialist feminist leader, Clara Fraser. The aspiring tradeswomen – Black, white, Chicana and Asian American – joined the largely white, male workforce at a moment of high employee morale following a victorious 10-day walkout. But they soon encountered a utility-wide culture of sexism, racism and anti-labor practices that was entrenched at the highest levels of management. The ETTs and the women who came after them fought hard to stay in the workforce and experienced discriminatory layoffs, intense harassment, and serious injuries. Many found that the secret to survival lay in building alliances with men of color, white union brothers, and office staff at the utility. Black men, who had encountered similar obstacles in their own recent entry into the trades, were a particularly strong source of support.

The groundbreaking women confronted bigotry while demonstrating their common interests with other workers. They sought to expose city management and hold City Light accountable as a publicly owned utility. They motivated the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers Local 77 to be more responsive to its diverse membership. They formed an employee rights organization, the Committee for Equal Rights at City Light (CERCL), which bridged union boundaries and fought discrimination against all workers, both organized and unorganized. They successfully defended and expanded the city’s and county’s anti-discrimination laws as co-founders of a community-labor coalition, the Ad Hoc Committee for Fair Employment and Open Housing. They worked closely with a range of groups including the City Light Black Employees Association (CLBEA), Seattle Employee Association of Gays and Lesbians (SEAGL), Women in Engineering, Radical Women, Freedom Socialist Party, and the Seattle Light Brigade, a consumer organization.

The workshop will feature veterans of these momentous struggles and a public historian currently writing a book on the saga. The presenters’ knowledge and experiences will offer inspiration and concrete lessons for today’s movements about how to build working-class solidarity across lines of gender, race, and sexuality.

Presentations will be supplemented with a PowerPoint slideshow of photos and documents from the City Light organizing, and charts showing historical statistics on wages in the trades, comparable worth and affirmative action, and women’s participation in the trades.
