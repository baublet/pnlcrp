The Triumph of Credentials: Professional Work and Inequality in Progressive-Era Boston

Cristina Groeger
Harvard University

Abstract

This paper explores professionalization as a political strategy of job control in the early 20th century U.S. at multiple "scales": the city, region, and nation. As craft union control was undercut, middle-class Americans pursued “professional” strategies of control through advanced schooling. Local degree-granting colleges and universities reoriented themselves to provide training for the traditional professions such as law and medicine, as well as the “new professions” of business management, engineering, and education. Through regional and national forms of accreditation, formerly local elite institutions regulated and set the standards for the most prestigious credentials and highest-paid work. This is one chapter of a larger dissertation on the transformation of training for work across the employment structure in the city of Boston from 1870-1940, and its implications for social inequality at the national scale. While class conflict drove a "race to the bottom" in the mutual regulation of apprenticeship by unions and employers, preventing the national coordination of training for industry, the very different political configuration of white-collar and professional training drove a “race to the top” of educational credentials. While an expanding system of decentralized local school systems allowed many to enter white-collar jobs, the triumph of credentials primarily benefited a new, nationally-connected, educated elite.
