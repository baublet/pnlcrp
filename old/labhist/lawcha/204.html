Teaching Labor History: What’s Happening in the States? Roundtable

Nikki Mandell1, Cecelia Bucki2, Nancy Quam-Wickham3, Fred Glass4
1University of Wisconsin-Whitewater, 2Fairfield University, 3Washington State University, 4California Federation of Teachers

Abstract

Working people have comprised the majority of the population in all societies across all times and places. Over the past fifty years historians have reversed previous decades of neglect to study and publish extensively on working class experiences, ideas, activities, and roles in historical change. Yet, transferring these exciting and critical stories from scholarly research to the broader public's historical thinking has been sketchy at best. Labor and working class history continues to be largely neglected and narrowly episodic in standard history curriculum. Why is this the case? What is and can be done to reintegrate the history of work and working people into the history they made?

This panel will address these questions by examining developments at the state and local levels, where history curriculum are developed, shared and taught. Panelists will explore specific efforts by government and non-government groups to bring workers and labor history into history teaching and learning – including formal inclusion in state teaching/learning standards, in recommended content topics, and the creation of lesson plans, curricular units and resource lists.
