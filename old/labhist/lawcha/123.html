Perspectives on the Issue of Union Corruption in the Era of the McClellan Committee, 1956-1959

David Witwer1, Robert Donnelly2, Liesl Orenic3, Eric Arnesen4, Catherine Rios5
1Penn State, 2Gonzaga University, 3Dominican University, 4George Washington University, 5Penn State Harrisburg

Abstract

The McClellan Committee hearings (1957-1959) raised public concern about the issue of union corruption and made the leadership of the Teamsters Union a notorious symbol of the problem of labor racketeering.

Allegations of corruption involving Dave Beck were followed by a series of other high profile hearings that focused on his successor James Hoffa and a group of other local Teamster leaders, such as Don Peters, who shared Hoffa’s willingness to adopt aggressive union organizing tactics, and his apparent willingness to work with men who had mob ties. The AFL-CIO responded by holding a parallel set of investigations, run at the behest of its own Ethical Practices Committee and intended to demonstrate the labor movement’s ability, and willingness, to deal with the problem of corruption within its ranks.

These papers offer a fresh look at that history, providing an alternative perspective by offering new research on the history of Teamster leaders spotlighted by the McClellan Committee and by exploring the behind-the-scenes mechanics of the AFL-CIO’s Ethical Practices Committee.

Robert Donnelly’s paper, “Teamster Boss: Dave Beck,” argues for a revisionist perspective on the Seattle labor leader, challenging the one-sided depiction of him that emerged in the McClellan Committee hearings and which has come to predominate in almost all subsequent accounts.

Donnelly’s account will place Beck firmly in the context of Seattle’s labor history.

Liesl Orenic focuses her paper on the organizing activities Don Peters, the dynamic Chicago labor leader, whose Teamsters Local 743 became the subject of a McClellan Committee probe. Orenic’s research offers a more complex understanding of the aggressive organizing tactics that the McClellan Committee tagged as “racket picketing.”

Catherine Rios and David Witwer draw on AFL-CIO records to document the federation’s effort to mount an anti-racketeering purge that would respond to the revelations of the McClellan Committee. In doing so the Ethical Practices Committee revealed the distinctive Cold War framework in which this history unfolded as well as the limitations facing a non-governmental anti-corruption effort.
