Labor Coalitions and Anti-Globalism

Verity Burgmann1, Eric Larson2, Beth Robinson3, Fred Lonidier4
1Monash University, 2University of Massachusetts Dartmouth, 3Texas A&M-Corpus Christi, 4UCSD Visual Arts Dept. & AFT

Abstract

□ Labour in the Global Carnival Against Capital Though not the first of such events, the ‘Battle of Seattle’ late in 1999 was the seen as the coming out party for the anti-corporate movement. Where was labour in this important new movement? Did unions participate in blockading the citadels of corporate global power? Evidence from case-studies of four of the ‘summit-hopping’ episodes (Seattle November-December 1999, Melbourne September 2000, Quebec City April 2001 and Genoa July 2001) suggests strong working-class involvement, especially of white-collar workers from the public sector, and important contributions from union activists and particular radical unions as organizations, white-collar and blue-collar, public and private. However, in some cases trade union officialdom at higher levels preferred union contingents keep a safe distance from centres of action. Such reticence did not present the union movement in the best light to anti-corporate activists; and it revealed significant tensions within unions between militant, class-conscious activists and more co-opted and conservative officials.

□ The Frontlines of “Global” Movements: Re-considering Working-Class Struggle in the “Anti-Globalization” Movement How did working-class organizations participate in the Global Justice, or “Anti-Globalization,” movements of the 1990s and early 2000s, particularly in the U.S. context? Most scholarship on the movement has focused on non-governmental organizations, advocacy networks, and direct action collectives. This paper re-considers this scholarship in light of the activities of the Jobs with Justice coalition, a U.S.-based working-class coalition that sought to expand labor unions’ work with the networks of activists and organizations confronting sweeping neoliberal economic reforms. Like many grassroots groups that collaborated in the massive protests and world-scale fora of the era, the unions and organizations that led the Jobs with Justice coalition were frontline organizations, since they directly faced the consequences of multilateral trade agreements and the new policies of the World Trade Organization. Jobs with Justice, in trying to “localize global justice” to better respond to working-class needs, sought to re-adjust the scales of struggle to win concrete local battles even as relating struggles to transnational networks. This paper is based on interviews and archival work in local Jobs with Justice offices as well as in collections at the AFL-CIO and several key protest sites. It directly relates to the conference theme of Scales of Struggle, and also speaks to sub-themes of War and Empire, Borders and Coalitions, and Struggling for Justice.

□ “Made in the USA?”: Resisting Sweatshops in the 1990s My paper examines the 1990s, as a significant period of anti-sweatshop activism in the United States. During this decade activists fought many of the same challenges as in previous eras, but adapted to a new landscape, with an increasingly mobile and global sweatshop industry. As parts of a larger anti-globalization, pro-worker, and pro-environment movement taking place at the turn of the century, these activists forced the means of production back to the forefront.
