Migrant Labor: Global Intellectual Histories

Julie Weise1, Cindy Hahamovitch2, Elliot Young3
1University of Oregon, 2University of Georgia, 3Lewis and Clark College

Abstract

For the past two decades, the best histories of migrant laborers have adopted a transnational approach, using sources and interpretations from workers’ places of origin as well as their work sites. Now, some scholars are trying to expand their frames even further. They hope to consider the phenomenon of migrant labor on a global scale. This work is methodologically dicey, presenting challenges of research, interpretation, and narration.

In this panel, scholars at three different stages of their academic careers demonstrate possible ways to further our understanding of migrant labor as a phenomenon that has been not only national or binational, but global. Recent Ph.D. María Quintana examines the discourses of the Mexican national and British colonial governments in the 1940s-60s, finding insightful parallels in the ways in each justified its decision to cooperate with U.S. labor recruitment attempts. Just-tenured historian Julie Weise examines primary sources on Mexican braceros and their Spanish contemporaries working in France to unearth connections between the ideas each brought into their official and unofficial labor actions. And senior scholar Cindy Hahamovitch examines the relationship between British abolitionism and the rise of indentured (or “coolie”) labor systems, illuminating more continuity than change between slavery and its successor.

In all, these three papers foreground the importance of ideas by and about labor migration to situate such migrations as central phenomena in modern world history.
