Mirroring the Empire: Cigar Makers' International Union and Anti-Cuban Sentiment, 1898-1901

Andrew Gomez
University of Puget Sound

Abstract

This paper looks at anti-Cuban sentiment within the Cigar Makers’ International Union (CMIU) during and immediately after the Spanish-American War. Dating back to 1868, Cubans formed émigré enclaves in various parts of the United States with Key West and Ybor City (in present-day Tampa) in Southern Florida serving as two of the larger hubs. Both communities were centered around the Cuban cigar industry and developed labor unions with deep ties to the Cuban independence movement and Spanish anarchism. The CMIU made several attempts to organize Cuban workers in Southern Florida but were repeatedly rebuffed. This paper argues that the CMIU adopted the language of American colonialism in order to justify replacing the Cuban-led unions of Southern Florida. Following the Spanish-American War, the United States military and government characterized Cubans as unfit for self-rule and democracy. These characterizations were used to justify the occupation of Cuba and subsequent interventions made possible by the 1901 Platt Amendment. The CMIU adopted many of these same characterizations to demean Cuban-led unions and argued that Anglo leadership was needed in order to properly lead Southern Florida’s cigar unions. By 1901, the CMIU was able to situate itself as the alternative to the radical labor unions of Southern Florida and became the dominant representative of cigar workers as more radical unions were quelled by vigilantism.
