Jurisdictional Conflicts and the Battle for Canadian Bank Workers in the 1970s

Julia Smith
Rutgers University

Abstract

To contribute to the conference theme of “Scales of Struggle” and the sub-themes of “Borders and Coalitions” and “Struggling for Justice,” this paper will examine the conflicts that occurred within the Canadian labour movement in the 1970s over union organizing in the banking industry. This period witnessed the greatest surge in bank worker unionization in Canadian history. Initially, the unions and labour organizations involved worked separately but alongside one another. Things changed in 1977 though, when two independent unions successfully challenged a federal labour board ruling on single-branch bargaining units. With branch certification now possible, large unions and labour organizations that had failed or not bothered to organize bank workers turned their attention to the banks. Bitter inter-union battles soon erupted, most notably between the country’s largest labour federation – the Canadian Labour Congress (CLC) – and a small feminist union – the Service, Office, and Retail Workers’ Union of Canada (SORWUC). As unions expended time, energy, and resources debating who had jurisdiction over bank employees, the banks waged a steady war against unionization in the branches, in the media, and in the courts and, in the end, emerged victorious. The labour movement’s inability and unwillingness to overcome ideological differences and jurisdictional issues played a key role in the failure of the drives. As such, the battle for Canadian bank workers that occurred in the 1970s serves as a useful case study of why jurisdictional conflicts occur and how they harm workers and the labour movement. SORWUC’s status as an independent feminist union focused on organizing women workers in a variety of sectors positioned it as radically different from most other unions and helped the union organize bank workers. Its independence, ideology, and success, however, also led CLC officials to view the feminist union as a threat. Rather than support SORWUC and build on its initial progress, CLC leaders chose to establish a new bank workers’ organizing committee, to isolate the independent union, and to defend representatives and affiliates who attempted to undermine it. Without support from the CLC, SORWUC leaders felt unable to counter the banks’ efforts to thwart unionization; the union withdrew from negotiations and cancelled its certifications. The CLC continued with its campaign, but the congress’s strategy proved less successful than SORWUC’s; the banks’ anti-union behaviour compounded matters. Analyzing the conflict that occurred in the 1970s bank drives thus highlights the problems with jurisdiction and top-down organizing and the importance of labour movement solidarity and support for new ways of organizing.
