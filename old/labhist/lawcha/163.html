“Finnishing the History of the 1910s Pacific Northwest Lumber Wars”

Aaron Goings
Saint Martin's University

Abstract

This paper will provide a brief overview of the key events and themes of the 1910s labor upsurge in lumber, easily the Pacific Northwest’s largest industry. It will focus special attention on the central place of Finnish immigrant men and women in the making of the great 1917 Lumber Strike, the post-strike efforts to maintain the Northwest Industrial Workers of the World (IWW, or Wobbly) movement, and the violent repression experienced by strikers and their supporters. In so doing, I will be exploring the internal politics of the IWW lumber workers’ movement, looking at how identities of race, gender, ethnicity, and politics were negotiated by the radical group, and how those negotiations shaped the boundaries of union, socialist, and Wobbly movements in the Pacific Northwest. It concludes that the 1917 strike and subsequent IWW struggles were the product of years of previous labor and left-wing organizing, which, in several Northwest lumber towns communities, was based among Finnish immigrants.

A close examination of Finnish workers’ activism during the Pacific Northwest labor conflicts yields richer and more accurate understandings of labor relations in the Northwest’s biggest industry during the early twentieth century. Additionally, emphasizing the place of women Finnish radicals will help restore Finnish-American working people to the center of Pacific Northwest labor and women’s history.
