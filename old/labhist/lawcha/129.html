The Rural Working Class Confronts New Capitalisms

Keith Orejel1, Tim Paulson2, William Goldsmith3, Joshua Freeman4, Nelson Lichtenstein5
1University of Missouri, 2University of California, Santa Barbara, 3Duke University, 4City University of New York, 5University of California-Santa Barbara

Abstract

Rural spaces in the United States of America have been inextricably bound up with advanced Capitalism since at least the mid-nineteenth century, when distant commodity markets and capital investors began to shape the types of crops grown and the organization of farm work and space. “Agrarian Capitalism,” as geographer Richard Walker names it, was firmly entrenched by the twentieth century, and it only grew stronger as agricultural producers and the United States Department of Agriculture worked to make “every farm a factory” (Richard Walker 2004; Deborah Fitzgerald 2003). However, in the mid-twentieth century, rural workers and communities increasingly confronted capitalisms that were not “agrarian.” These Capitalisms did not so much shape rural space as dissolve it, and they drew the rural working class into a global capitalist space in new and deeper ways.

Our panel examines the rural working class’s response to developments in Industrial, Financial, and Post-Industrial Capitalism from the 1940s to 1990s. How were these changes different from changes that preceded them? What were the consequences for rural workers and communities? How did rural workers and their advocates make sense of these changes, and what did they do to resist or take advantage of them? Our panel engages this year’s conference theme of “Scales of Struggle” by examining the way rural workers and rural spaces suffered, resisted, and embraced bigger, broader Capitalist transformations around them.
