Paper title: “Guest Workers, the Cold War, and Dreams of Development in Mexico and Spain”

Michael Snodgrass
Indiana University Purdue University Indianpolis

Abstract

Looking back after 30 years of service to Mexico’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the distinguished diplomat Jaime Torres Bodet pondered his most consequential policymaking endeavor: the 1942 negotiation of the bilateral agreement that permitted millions of young Mexican men to leave their families and communities each year to harvest American crops. Known as the Bracero Program, the accord last more than 20 years and became among the most controversial policies enacted by Mexico’s post-revolutionary government. Its critics - both in Mexico and the USA - demonized the Bracero Program as an exploitative labor system that benefitted American growers at the expense of both Mexican workers and national pride. The program’s proponents defended it as a model guest worker accord that guaranteed basic rights for farm workers while also serving to improve US-Mexican relations during the Cold War. Building upon social science research from the 1920s, its defenders also portrayed it as a form of foreign aid in that braceros returned home with the hard-earned savings and newly-acquired skills to achieve the rural development goals of the Mexican state. Torres Bodet, for his part, admitted that “renting out the health and vigor of a large contingent of our countryman to foreign bosses must be seen, under any circumstances, with sorrow.” But he considered migration “inevitable” and thus defended a Mexican state policy meant to defend migrants’ working and living conditions while they labored abroad. Himself the son of Spanish and French immigrants, the one-time ambassador to France also likened the Bracero Program to the postwar European guest worker accords instituted during his posting to Paris (1955-58). From this perspective, the Mexican braceros were no different from the Spanish peasants who harvested crops or mined coal in postwar France and Belgium. Many arrived with the assistance of the Spanish Emigration Institute, the Franco-era agency that promoted emigration to Europe’s industrial core as an official antidote to rural unemployment and underdevelopment. Like the Mexican counterparts, the majority returned to a homeland undergoing rapid economic and social change.

Taking Torres Bodet’s lead, this paper examines the North American Bracero Program from the comparative perspective offered by the migratory labor agreements that sent millions of southern European guest workers to labor in postwar western Europe. Of course, such programs served fundamentally to deliver temporary labor to agricultural and industrial employers. But they ostensibly served broader ends, ranging from their role in promoting economic development in sending regions to their service as political safety valves, an ostensible antidote to communism. Based upon research in US, Mexican and Spanish archives, this paper will analyze these guest worker accords in a global and comparative perspective by analyzing the developmentalist agenda of the sending states. It then explores both the economic and cultural effects of emigration and return migration in Cold War-era Spain and Mexico.
