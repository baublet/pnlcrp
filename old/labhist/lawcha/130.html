Comparing Cities at War: Contradictory Patterns of Urban Working-Class Opposition to World War I

Keith Laybourn1, Erik Eklund2, Shelton Stromquist3, Rosemary Feurer4
1History, University of Huddersfield, UK, 2History, Federation University, Australia, 3University of Iowa, 4History, Northern Illinois University

Abstract

Workers in the belligerent countries faced contradictory pressures during World War I as their nations slid into war and demanded ever-increasing loyalty. Caught between intensifying nationalism and residual internationalism, faced with volatile wartime labor and housing markets and persistent pockets of unemployment, burdened by sharply rising prices and food shortages, threatened with unprecedented levels repression and harassment of war opponents, not to speak of military conscription itself, workers and their movements (both trade unions and political parties) responded differently. These contradictions deserve closer examination in local contexts and specifically in the urban crucibles in which the war pressures were most acutely felt. This session will explore and compare these contradictions and the evolving patterns of opposition and repression in specific cities in the United States, Germany, the United Kingdom, and Australia. By moving beyond the political machinations of national parties and parliamentary elites, we may see more clearly the cross pressures of daily life in wartime with which city workers were faced and the often difficult political choices they made.
