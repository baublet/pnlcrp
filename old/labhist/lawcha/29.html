Ludlow, Greek Americans in the Colorado Coal War (documentary film)

Frosso Tsouka1 and Rosemary Feurer2
1Apostolis Berdebes Non Profit, 2Northern Illinois University

Abstract

The Colorado Coal War of 1913-14, a class war between miners, led by the United Mine Workers of America, and mine-owners, led by Rockefeller, is a very important page of Labor history. It laid the groundwork for contemporary labor laws and rights, not only in the US but in all the industrialized world.

In 1903, after a big strike of miners in Southern Colorado, the coal companies decided to replace their English speaking work force with immigrants from 22 countries of Southeastern Europe, brought in, unknowingly, as scabs. Ten years later, the inhuman living and working conditions and the efforts of bilingual union organizers, had turned the scabs into stalwart union members, who endured a long and violent strike and in the end took up arms to defend their lives after the bloodbath of the Ludlow Massacre on April 20, 1914.

The film documents this struggle through archival interviews with strikers, songs, photos and videos from that period and interviews with historians, authors and descendants of strikers who proudly share their family history. For the first time the important contribution of the Greek - and Italian - miners in the strike is presented in this film.

The film has been very well received by the public and the critics since its release this January in Greece and has received two awards until now in Greek documentary festivals.

This film works with several themes of the conference, in particular the main theme of scales of struggle; it focuses on the role of Greek labor migration and immigrants role the class conscious struggle of miners in Colorado. Global connections, with a Greek film crew enhance these connections. Greek memorials, including the Colorado UMWA fight song performed in Greece, make a statement about the role of recovering memory from the past in a powerful way.
