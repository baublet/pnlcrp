Relationship-Based Organizing: AFSCME’s Clerical Unionization Campaigns at the University of Minnesota

Amanda Walter
Wayne State University

Abstract

By the 1970s, clerical workers in higher education increasingly reached out to the large unions for help in bargaining, as they encountered a lack of job security, reorganization of work, and difficult bargaining conditions due, in part, to diminishing state funding for universities, and turned to affiliation and unionization to strengthen the workers’ power. AFSCME, which represented more university office workers than any other union nationally, hoped to appeal to higher education clericals by drawing on their long experience in the public sector and paying attention to the majority women clerical workers’ gender-specific needs. Through an examination of the University of Minnesota, Relationship-Based Organizing: AFSCME’s Clerical Unionization Campaigns at the University of Minnesota shows AFSCME’s tactical change in unionization campaigns, switching from unsuccessfully focusing on campaign literature in the 1982 campaign to a highly successful person-to-person organizing model in the 1989-1991 campaign. The tactical change drew lessons from the model employed by the AFSCME’s clerical organizing campaign at Harvard University and the working women’s movement. The working women’s movement had a tremendous impact on the labor movement, including style of organizing, what workplaces could be seen as unionization targets, and what issues should be prioritized in campaigns and contracts. This research, based on AFSCME archival collections at the Walter Reuther Library at Wayne State University, the University Archives at University of Minnesota, and oral histories with union organizers, provides depth to the largely unknown history of the university workplace, working women’s movement, and their union entry into the clerical organizing. Both the local case study and the national context of organizing will be examined.
