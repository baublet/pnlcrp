Workers’ Struggles on Stage: Australian Radical Theatre

Lisa Milner1 and Cathy Brigden2
1Southern Cross University, 2RMIT University, Melbourne, Australia

Abstract

This paper considers connections within international left cultural activity by introducing the New Theatre in Australia, which has seen very little academic attention. Taking inspiration from the New Theatre of the USA and the Unity Theatre in the UK, the New Theatre employed a novel mix of conventional theatre forms, experimental performative styles and communist theories of ‘art as a weapon’ in their presentation of left-wing theatre within a society that was largely resistant to their broader political ideals. Their work and organisation were avowedly working class, and support from the Communist Party of Australia and left-wing unions was vital. Drawing from primary research and oral history interviews, the paper highlights their struggle for industrial justice in a marriage of culture and union activism in Australia. To illustrate this intersection, a 1952 performance of The Candy Store, written by an American activist, Bernard Rubin, is discussed. What makes the performance of this play notable is the location and audience: 1500 feet down a mineshaft to coal miners staging a stay-in strike.
