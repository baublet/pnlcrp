Public Sector Unions and Conservative Politics Since the 1970s

Joseph Slater1, Joseph Hower2, Josiah Rector3, Elizabeth Faue3
1University of Toledo (panel chair), 2Southwestern University, 3Wayne State University

Abstract

Since the 1970s, public sector unions have occupied a position of relative strength within a shrinking organized labor movement in the United States. In recent years, attacks on public sector unions by conservative governors and corporate lobbying organizations in Wisconsin, Ohio, Illinois, and Michigan (to name only a few examples) have raised the question: can public sector unions retain their position as a stronghold of union density and political influence? How have public sector unions responded to similar political challenges in the past? More broadly, what is the relationship between public sector unions and conservative politics in the United States? While public sector unions have challenged the neoliberal contraction of public services and social programs, some have also abetted the expansion of the carceral state, and conservative “law and order” politics. The relationship between public sector unions and conservative politics is thus a complex and, at times, paradoxical one. This panel examines the complex relationship between public sector unions and conservative politics in three different areas. Joseph Hower’s paper shows how AFSCME, under the leadership of Jerry Wurf, responded to anti-tax populism (and related attacks on public sector workers) in the 1970s. Elizabeth Faue’s paper turns to the issue of school violence during the same period, considering how the AFT under Albert Shanker advocated “zero tolerance” juvenile justice policies in response to rising rates of assault against teachers. The paper considers the relationship between teachers’ unions and the “school to prison pipeline.” Finally, Josiah Rector’s paper focuses on how conservative politicians and lobbying groups in Michigan have used Emergency Manager laws to undermine public sector unions in the 21st century. The paper considers the effectiveness of union responses to Emergency Manager laws, including strikes, lawsuits, ballot referenda, and other strategies.

Joseph E. Hower, “Every Candidate . . . Is Running Against Our Union”: AFSCME’s Response to Tax Cut Fever in the Late 1970s”

    Dropping earlier legalistic objections to unionization, the Public

Service Research Council and other similar groups seized on the growing salience of tax issues during the late 1970s to craft a critique of union power that stressed the threat posed to the citizen-taxpayer. At the same time, anti-tax activists like Howard Jarvis found in the anti-public worker language a compelling tool to wield in their own campaigns, one which effectively masked the fiscal implications of their tax-cutting logic. By casting government workers as parasites on the body politic, Jarvis and others were able to suggest that revenues could be reduced without cutting services, simply by shrinking the share directed to government workers through pay cuts, benefit reductions, and layoffs. Proposition 13, then, was not solely a California issue, nor was it even strictly about the distribution of the costs of suburbanization. It was also the manifestation of an older anti-public worker impulse fused with an increasingly urgent concern over taxes, a potent combination that posed little less than an existential crisis for the public sector labor movement. My paper will explore the response of one union, the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees, to the emergence of anti-tax politics in the late 1970s. Although it had pressed for state and local tax reform for the better part of a decade, the union initially underestimated the political power of the fused anti-tax / anti-worker / anti-union argument. President Jerry Wurf and other union officials assumed that middle-class taxpayers would never risk valued public services by sharply curtailing public revenues, so their initial response to rising anxieties over taxes focused on a multi-million dollar advertising campaign designed to establish AFSCME as "The Union that Works For You." Predicated on defending public sector unions by defending public services, the strategy was designed to instill in taxpayers of the inextricable link between the tax bill and basic government services. Forced into an even more defensive posture in the aftermath of Proposition 13, AFSCME adopted a two-track response to the crisis, trading short-term concessions to stunt the growth of the backlash while also pushing progressive alternatives to the "meat axe" approach in states like Michigan and Oregon. Partly successful at mitigating the worst effects of the Tax Revolt, the union’s campaign nonetheless rested on a misreading of the new political landscape. AFSCME in general, and Wurf in particular, misunderstood the extent to which the politicization of public workers (and their unions) had altered the basic taxes-services equation. Far from undercutting the core message of conservative critics, AFSCME's defense of government services only served to strengthen the perception that those services were, at least in part, self-serving.

Elizabeth Faue, “Teachers and the School to Prison Pipeline: The AFT, “Zero Tolerance Policies,” and School Violence, 1970-2000” During the 1970s, a series of government studies and media reports sounded the alarm about rising rates of violence, drug use, theft, and vandalism in the nation’s public schools. For teachers, such violence led to deteriorating work environments that put teachers, school workers, and students at risk. Historians of the carceral state have analyzed the “law and order” politics of this period and the increased investment of the state in the criminal justice system, but in general they have paid little attention to schools. The politics of school violence played a critical role in the expansion of the juvenile justice system, which activists have called the “school-to-prison pipeline.” In less obvious ways, public investment in the criminal justice system—increasing government expenditures—competed with education in government budget battles. This paper shows how AFT officials, particularly AFT President Albert Shanker, responded to rising rates of assault against AFT members by students by promoting “zero tolerance” policies against juvenile offenders. It argues that, while these policies developed in response to school violence, “zero tolerance” contributed to the problem of mass incarceration, by substituting heavy-handed policing and surveillance for therapeutic approaches and structural solutions to school segregation and underfunding. In the 1990s and 2000s, activists from other teachers’ unions (and even within the AFT) advocated policies that sought to protect teachers and students from violence but avoided the criminalization of youth. By examining how the AFT under Shanker responded to the crisis of school violence in the 1970s and 1980s, this paper provides a fresh perspective on the origins of the “school to prison pipeline.”

Josiah Rector, “Deposing the ‘Modern-Day Cincinnatus’?: Public Sector Union Responses to Emergency Manager Laws in Michigan, 2000-2015” In Michigan in recent decades, Emergency Manager laws have radically expanded the power of the state government to take over municipalities and school districts in fiscal crisis. Most critiques of Emergency Manager laws focus on their troubling implications for voting rights and racial equity. In 2013, for example, over half of African American voters in the state of Michigan lived in municipalities run by unelected Emergency Managers, rendering citizens’ votes for mayors and city council members void. Commentators have paid relatively little attention to the impact of Emergency Manager laws on Michigan’s public sector unions. However, this paper argues that efforts by conservative politicians and wealthy donors to undermine public sector unions are central to the design and implementation of Emergency Manager laws. Right-wing intellectuals, like Michael Van Der Beek of the Mackinac Center for Public Policy, celebrate the Emergency Manager as a “modern-day Cincinnatus” with dictatorial powers to cast aside “self-serving union bosses” and dissolve “inept local school boards.” These laws enable gubernatorial appointees to void public sector union contracts; eliminate local school boards; privatize schools and other public assets; replace unionized public workers with private contractors; and suspend collective bargaining for up to five years. By weakening Michigan’s public sector unions, moreover, supporters of these laws hope to set an example for other states. This paper examines how public sector unions in Michigan have responded to Emergency Manager laws in Michigan during the past fifteen years. Union locals in Detroit and other cities have used lawsuits, electoral campaigns, and strikes to battle specific Emergency Manager initiatives, such as outsourcing, pension cuts, and layoffs. In 2012, unions played a central role in a statewide ballot measure to repeal Public Act 4, which a majority of Michigan voters approved. However, the replacement of the law with the referendum-proof Public Act 436 posed an unprecedented challenge to public sector unions, raising questions about the viability of their current political strategies. This paper argues that, while local contractual fights are necessary, unions should develop broader coalitions with other groups disadvantaged by Emergency Manager laws.
