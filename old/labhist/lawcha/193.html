Abstract

□ Guest Workers, the Cold War, and Dreams of Development in Mexico and Spain Looking back after 30 years of service to Mexico’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the distinguished diplomat Jaime Torres Bodet pondered his most consequential policymaking endeavor: the 1942 negotiation of the bilateral agreement that permitted millions of young Mexican men to leave their families and communities each year to harvest American crops. Known as the Bracero Program, the accord last more than 20 years and became among the most controversial policies enacted by Mexico’s post-revolutionary government. Its critics - both in Mexico and the USA - demonized the Bracero Program as an exploitative labor system that benefitted American growers at the expense of both Mexican workers and national pride. The program’s proponents defended it as a model guest worker accord that guaranteed basic rights for farm workers while also serving to improve US-Mexican relations during the Cold War. Building upon social science research from the 1920s, its defenders also portrayed it as a form of foreign aid in that braceros returned home with the hard-earned savings and newly-acquired skills to achieve the rural development goals of the Mexican state. Torres Bodet, for his part, admitted that “renting out the health and vigor of a large contingent of our countryman to foreign bosses must be seen, under any circumstances, with sorrow.” But he considered migration “inevitable” and thus defended a Mexican state policy meant to defend migrants’ working and living conditions while they labored abroad. Himself the son of Spanish and French immigrants, the one-time ambassador to France also likened the Bracero Program to the postwar European guest worker accords instituted during his posting to Paris (1955-58). From this perspective, the Mexican braceros were no different from the Spanish peasants who harvested crops or mined coal in postwar France and Belgium. Many arrived with the assistance of the Spanish Emigration Institute, the Franco-era agency that promoted emigration to Europe’s industrial core as an official antidote to rural unemployment and underdevelopment. Like the Mexican counterparts, the majority returned to a homeland undergoing rapid economic and social change.

Taking Torres Bodet’s lead, this paper examines the North American Bracero Program from the comparative perspective offered by the migratory labor agreements that sent millions of southern European guest workers to labor in postwar western Europe. Of course, such programs served fundamentally to deliver temporary labor to agricultural and industrial employers. But they ostensibly served broader ends, ranging from their role in promoting economic development in sending regions to their service as political safety valves, an ostensible antidote to communism. Based upon research in US, Mexican and Spanish archives, this paper will analyze these guest worker accords in a global and comparative perspective by analyzing the developmentalist agenda of the sending states. It then explores both the economic and cultural effects of emigration and return migration in Cold War-era Spain and Mexico.

□ The Construction of the Social Collectives and Social Roles: The Silent Majority in China’s Urbanization There is a large scale but often neglected marginal population in Chinese cities. They are scattered in large and small cities and are connected every day by common daily life. Few notice them or feel their existence, but when the Spring Festival and the Mid-Autumn festival come, and with them vacations, urban areas notice that life is more inconvenient without them. Nearly every single building and road in China has been built by them. They are supposedly the most respected group in the country. However, in reality, they lack basic social security, they suffer from the limitation of the household registration system (Hukou), and they are worried about their children's educational prospects. There is a special derogatory term for them-- "peasant workers." This group comes from major historical developments as in the early years of China’s opening-up and reform, the main body of this group were young farmers who went to the city to seek jobs so as to earn a living. Often as "floating population", or "external population," these workers exist on the margins of prosperity, incapable because of legal, cultural, and social biases to become fully integrated into their new urban setting, but differentiated from the rural workers who have always remained in the countryside. According to statistical data from China's National Health and Family Planning Commission, China's floating population was 253 million in total in 2015. This vast group accounts for approximately 20% of China's population, often appearing as hot topics for mass media, newspapers, and academic research. Typically, their plight is associated with polemics denouncing the Hukou system of China, a legal system that designates people as rural or urban dwellers, and which is often associated with the social problems of people being unable to settle, access social welfare, and gain education. This presentation is an exploration of these workers, the Hukou system, and how popular polemic works have done little to understand the underlying problems of China’s migrant population. Examining the history of the Hukou system, this paper explores the deeper issues at play in popular biases toward these rural workers, and concludes that in order to gain a deeper understanding of workers around the world we first must understand the complex, and ever changing nature of urban planning, slum developments, and the more important cultural biases that contribute to marginalization. There is no denying that until today the fate of these migrants and their city dreams has been uncertain. Therefore, in keeping with this conference’s theme of global analysis, this article aims to utilize their case so as to analyze the impact of the construction of social collectives and roles on different social groups from an interdisciplinary perspective. This is done in hope to regain the attention of both academics and common readers to the issues vulnerable and specific workers face in places like China and around the world.

□ Forging an Immigrant Workers’ Movement in the Shadows of Border Militarization, State Anti-Unionism, and the Neoliberal Non-Profit Industrial Complex
The U.S.-Mexico border along the 100-mile stretch of South Texas land known as the Rio Grande Valley carries with it some of the most unique and perilous conditions for laborers in the country. What this region has historically struggled with in regards to labor rights is a confluence of factors ranging from geographic isolation to high immigrant populations to Texas’ longstanding anti-union history among others. More recently, however, the level of militarization in this border region has escalated to the highest point since the early 20th century efforts against Mexican sediciosos, anti-American, Mexican dissidents, in which the National Guard was introduced into the region in order to maintain social control. In 2014, the National Guard was again summoned, but far from the days of the sediciosos, these armed military members were mostly patrolling a group of people working and living in perhaps the worst conditions in America without the crimes rates to justify this extra oversight. To make matters worse, those who are currently fighting against the labor injustices of yesterday situated in the border military zone of today, are also having to deal with the convoluted world of what many have termed “the non-profit industrial complex.” The border militarization handicaps potential worker organizing efforts as many fear retribution via deportation against themselves or family members if they speak up and the non-profit industrial complex makes it difficult for grassroots efforts to succeed as the more heavily funded efforts always seem to be those that least challenge the status quo and in essence least push the envelope for worker rights. Yet in spite of all this and in spite of the popular anti-union sentiment that has long plagued the state of Texas, a budding workers center has emerged in South Texas known as Fuerza Del Valle Workers Center which, using other worker center models, is creating possibilities for worker liberation in this politically arid terrain.

Fuerza del Valle is a grassroots workers center which provides support to workers in the area in cases of wage theft through grassroots methods of protest and public shaming while utilizing its attachment to the lawyers of the South Texas Civil Rights Project to leverage power against those who underpay and/or grossly mistreat South Texas workers in the form of litigation. Fuerza is small in that it employs only two staff members, but is huge in that it has an active membership upwards of thirty to forty workers who have either won claims against former employers through Fuerza or simply choose to proactively support a worker-based organization knowing that their time of need could come at any moment in this context of precarious employment on the border. Included in this group of workers is a sub-committee of female domestic workers who attempt to find solutions collaboratively to the problems that they face in their jobs which are notorious for mistreatment and abuse. As well, Fuerza regularly organizes marches, information sessions and other events in support of immigrant rights considering that the vast majority of workers involved with Fuerza are immigrants. The point of this presentation is to detail how grassroots labor organizing in a place like the South Texas border is taking place in order to provide insight into the present and future of this work in immigrant, anti-union, and/or militarized, border communities in the age of neoliberalism. It is through dialogue with one another while being situated in different contexts that we believe we can build the best opportunities for the advancement of worker rights and organizing efforts in spite of the complex manner in which labor efforts are daily attacked.
