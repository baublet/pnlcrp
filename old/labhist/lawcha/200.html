Abstract

Most of us know the song “Nine to Five,” sung with an edge by Dolly Parton and the star-studded Hollywood movie NINE TO FIVE. Yet few realize that these two icons of popular culture grew out of a social movement that spanned over twenty-five years, which had a profound impact on work for women and the labor movement.

In the early 1970s, a group of women office workers in Boston decided that they had suffered in silence long enough and created 9 to 5 to force changes in their workplace. Soon chapters were springing up across the country! 9 to 5 addressed sexual harassment, equal pay for equal work, the glass ceiling and FMLA.

Clericals were the low-wage workers of their era, and the stories and strategies of these bold, creative women have deep resonance in contemporary life. The story and legacy of the 9 to 5 movement speaks directly to today's low wage workers, the Fight for $15 and the growing inequality of today's America.

Julia Reichert is an Oscar-nominated filmmaker. Her earlier films include "Union Maids" (1976), "Seeing Red" (1983), "A Lion in the House" (2006), and "The Last Truck: Closing of a GM Plant" (2009)
