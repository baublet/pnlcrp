Labor and Digital Media: Organizing the "Creative City"

Richard Wells
Van Arsdale Center for Labor Studies, Empire State College/SUNY

Abstract

In the Spring of 2015, writers for the news and gossip website Gawker announced their intention to organize with the Writers’ Guild of America East (WGAE). Gawker’s management recognized the union without any resistance. Soon thereafter writers at other digital outlets, from Vice Media to Salon to The Huffington Post, sought and gained union recognition, some joining up with WGAE, others with the NewsGuild, and most through a routine card check. Observers saw in this burst of organizing an important sign of life for organized labor, not least because it involved a demographic thought to be immune to the attractions of trade unionism.

This paper takes a step back. First, I map this burst of organizing onto the transformation of the political economy of the news media. Then I examine the history of unionization in the cultural industry and in journalism to draw out the themes—such as the running tension between the romance of the writer’s life and the need for economic security, between an accommodating professionalism and a more oppositional trade unionism—that might best inform an understanding of the present situation. Finally, I will shift the perspective a bit. Since many of these workers are indeed young and college-educated, those “creative class” types that thanks to Richard Florida have fired the early 21st century urbanist imagination, does unionization in their ranks represent a new scale of struggle in the neo-liberal city? And how might it fit in with current efforts to re-think organized labor more generally?
