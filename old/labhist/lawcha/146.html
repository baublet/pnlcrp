Creative Uses of a Changing State: Unions Engage in the Postwar Political Arena

Nick Juravich1, Emily Lieb2, Lindsey Dayton1, Kelly Goodman3
1Columbia University, 2Seattle University, 3Yale University

Abstract

Our panel shows how unions used the levers of state power to advance a variety of progressive causes in the postwar era. While the dominant narrative of labor and the state after World War II decries bureaucratization and waning militancy, our papers reveal generative interactions between unions and structures of governance at multiple scales, from local political campaigns to federal antipoverty policies and Supreme Court jurisprudence. This political activity was facilitated, in part, by major changes to postwar government organization in the United States, including new civil rights and antipoverty legislation at the federal level. Kelly Goodman explores the UAW's role in securing state protection for teacher organizing and school desegregation through local school board elections in Detroit. Lindsey Dayton shows how HERE deployed legal resources to help workers file individual workplace claims under the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Nick Juravich charts the AFT's organizing among community-based workers hired with funding from the Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 1965. Chair Emily Lieb will comment on the interplay between state policy and organizing of many kinds. Labor's efforts in the political arena connected unions with ongoing struggles for black freedom, economic justice, and women's liberation in this era, creating openings for new forms of social movement unionism. While these campaigns yielded uneven results, they point to the possibility of using political action and state structures to build and expand the labor movement.
