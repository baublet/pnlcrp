Organizing Strategies: Lessons from the Past

Tom Juravich1, Kathy Ferguson2, Wesley Bishop3, Steven Parfitt4, Randi Storch5
1UMass Amherst, 2University of Hawai'i, 3Purdue University, 4University of Nottingham, 5SUNY Cortland

Abstract

□ Strikes, Comprehensive Campaigns and the Revival of the Labor Movement This paper troubles the assertion that reviving the strike is labor’s primary strategy for union renewal. It beings with the understanding that unions have been successful when they developed forms of resistance that take into account how employers are structured and how they operate in the context of the state. Strikes constituted a successful union tactic in the early part of the 20th century given their ability to exert economic pressure on relatively simple firms. By mid-century firms were becoming increasing complex, but the post-war labor accord established a legal and political framework around the strike – which was a regular feature of the post-war industrial relations system. With firms increasingly taking on post-modern forms the contestation of power today is often not on the picket line or the plant gates which raises the question if strikes are automatically the appropriate form of resistance. Given the complexity of 21st century firms, one cannot assume that any one tactic (such as the strike) is appropriate without extensive corporate research to identify how power flows in a firm and its potential vulnerabilities. The real promise of comprehensive/strategic campaigns is the utilization of deep corporate research to build multifaceted campaigns that draw from structural, institutional, associational and symbol power that operate at a number of different scales. In this way comprehensive campaigns can create new opportunities for militancy that the strike cannot.

□ Creating a City to Resist the State: The Seattle General Strike of 1919 The Seattle general strike of 1919 was a remarkable moment in the process that Eugene Holland characterizes in Nomad Citizenship as a slow-motion general strike. The Seattle general strike rose out of the immanent historical practices of Seattle’s labor communities as they interrupted state and capitalist formations and instead organized to govern themselves. Defying the conservative national leadership of their own unions as well as Seattle’s bombastic governmental and economic elites, these workers organized themselves into a viable alternative structure for making and carrying out decisions necessary to running the city. The writings of the strike’s historian, Anna Louise Strong, and of the “two-card men” whose dual union membership combined the fight to improve existing conditions with the larger struggle for a better world, both reflect and create a constructive dissidence that still resonates today. The Seattle general strike ended on February 11. Yet perhaps it did not. I suggest that Seattle’19 can still be available to us as an example of Eugene Holland’s idea of affirmative nomadology, an opportunity to distill principles and practices for organizing ourselves outside the confines of capital and the state. Holland encourages us to identify assemblages of resonating processes that serve as relays for material and political operations. Seattle’19, I argue, is a resonating moment within Holland’s imagining of “a long-term process of becoming-general of the general strike.” (p. 156)

□ Imagining the Commonweal: Coxey's Army, Cox's March, and Working Class Political Conceptions of the Public Sphere, 1896-1932 In 1896 Jacob Coxey, a populist reformer and small town business leader, led a group of unemployed workers on a march to Washington D.C. Covering hundreds of miles, and generating large amounts of newspaper coverage, Coxey and the other marchers used the conception of the "commonweal" as a central theme in their rhetoric to challenge popular Gilded Age ideas of the political economy. Routed at the Capitol Building by police, the protest ended with no formal action taken by Congress or the Cleveland administration to address public concerns of the economy. Decades later a radical labor priest, James Renshaw Cox, led a group of unemployed once again from Pennsylvania to D.C. Echoing many of the ideas of Coxey and the marchers of the 1890s, Cox was part of a growing segment of American Catholics who used the commonweal as a framework to reimagine how the public sphere, politics, and the economy should relate. Once again the marchers were largely ignored by the President, yet the marchers did gain the support of Hoover's Treasury Secretary, support that would eventually lead to political scandal and his impeachment. This paper is an examination of how working class political movements have reimagined the public sphere, specifically with the conception of the commonweal, and those reimaginings were, and still are, radical critiques of capitalist political economy. In a period where social movements like Occupy Wall Street and Black Lives Matter rely on the taking of public space, making radical critiques of political economy, and responding to hostile government responses, such a history is critical for academic and popular understanding

□ The Fraternal Tradition and the Global History of the Knights of Labor The history of international labour movements during the nineteenth century is generally told in a certain way. Socialists, anarchists and trade unionists from different countries came together in a variety of networks, movements and organisations to win strikes, regulate migration, and agitate for political change across national borders. The First and the Second Internationals were the largest and best-known of these movements.

Has this narrative obscured other trends and traditions in that story? This paper will draw on my research and work into the global history of the Knights of Labor, and on Robert Weir’s groundbreaking work concerning the fraternal side of the Knights, to think about whether we as historians have missed or underplayed the importance of fraternal culture in our understanding of international labour history. Fraternal orders were, after all, among the largest organisations that catered to a working-class audience. The United Order of Oddfellows, the Ancient Order of Foresters and other orders recruited working-class members across the world and provided them with the social insurance that states did not yet provide. Movements like the Knights of Labor were also organised on fraternal lines, and secret, fraternal organisations were present at the formation of the First International and other major global movements. There is an entire tradition here that needs much greater examination.

This paper has contemporary relevance as well. Many commentators have pointed to a decline in solidarity at the workplace and in the community as both a cause and effect of the decline in trade union membership. Fraternal culture, with its encouragement of brotherhood (or sisterhood) through shared ritual, once fulfilled this function. While there is very little chance of a revival of fraternal culture right now, we should consider other ways of building solidarity that do not rely only on economic self-interest.
