Neoliberalism and Its Discontents: Defending Public Institutions

Jillian Crocker1, Amy Zanoni2, Dawson Barrett3, Joe McCartin4
1SUNY Old Westbury, 2Rutgers University, 3Del Mar College, 4Georgetown University

Abstract

□ Not a labor town: Transforming Labor and Gender Politics in a Deindustrialized City? What factors strengthen worker claims in labor-hostile cities? A case study of a 21st century nurses’ strike sheds light on this question. On Friday March 31st, 2000, approximately 535 nurses at St. Vincent’s Hospital and Worcester Medical Center in Worcester, Massachusetts went on strike against Tenet Healthcare Corporation over issues related to staffing and pay. The strike lasted 49 days and had broad support, including that of local and state lawmakers. When the strike concluded with satisfactory provisions on key issues, it was widely deemed a win for workers. In fact, that win gave strength to nurse organizing on safe staffing and mandatory overtime issues across the U.S. Despite this success, conventional wisdom remains that Worcester, once a diverse manufacturing center, is not a city that is friendly to labor. In this case study I analyze local, regional, and trade news coverage to offer a contextualized understanding of the factors that facilitated community support for this influential strike. In particular, the paper explores the ways in which the gendered, classed, and racialized notion of nurses as professionals and as care providers – for individuals as well as for communities – was deployed to situate these workers as altruistic advocates. While such strategies facilitate labor gains in the short term, they risk reproducing social divisions and inequalities that may limit their impact in the long term.

□ “Hard Times at the Cooker:” The Struggle to Save Chicago’s Public Hospital My paper explores the impact that disinvestment and privatization had on staff and patients at Cook County Hospital during the late 1970s and early 1980s, and the movement they formed in response. In the 1970s, Chicago’s economy, like that of the nation’s, had collapsed while medical costs skyrocketed. Facing pressure from the state, Cook County enacted a series of austerity policies. Officials laid off nurses’ aides and clerical workers and reduced the number of hospital beds. When the County contracted out the hospital’s management to Hyatt Medical Management Enterprises, Inc. in 1980, the subsidiary of the Hyatt Hotel Corporation sent collections agencies after patients. Deteriorating working conditions and health care delivery moved the hospital community to action. Medical professionals, hospital workers, and community members mobilized their unions and formed the Committee to Save Cook County Hospital to defend the public institution that was integral to the livelihoods and health of many.

□ This is NOT a Local Campaign: Protest Movements in the Post-Liberal Era The United States’ abandonment of the liberal welfare state model of the mid-twentieth century has had drastic impacts on the American working classes. Attempts to turn public property into private profits through austerity, privatization, and de-regulation not only greatly expanded corporate power, they also re-shaped nearly every aspect of American life, from policing and education to the environment and the workplace. But while corporate power has become increasingly concentrated in the last several decades, corporate vulnerabilities have been decentralized through global supply chains and increasingly complicated methods of outsourcing and subcontracting. These shifts have altered the ways that protest movements can pressure those in power.

My paper presentation explores these dynamics through two fascinating case studies in which Americans at the political margins were forced to build large-scale coalitions to pressure local opponents on national and international levels. The first was a decade-long campaign to convince the University of Arizona to select a less ecologically vulnerable location for a new telescope complex. As the project’s many backers were revealed throughout the late 1980s and early 1990s, the campaign expanded from a small coalition of American Indian activists and environmentalists to an international pressure campaign targeting dozens of college campuses in the US and Canada, the Smithsonian Institution, and the Vatican. The second case study was a four-year campaign by Florida farm workers to force Yum! Brands, the largest restaurant corporation in the world, to change its labor policies. By the time the boycott concluded, there were active affiliates at 300 American high schools and colleges, and students at twenty-two schools had successfully ousted Taco Bell franchises. Through these stories, I outline some of the complexities of the economic and political shifts of the post-1960s period, as well as how these changes have impacted the efforts of protest movements.
