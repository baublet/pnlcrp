Defining Class and Race in the 1920s Coal Mines: Transnational Perspectives

Robert Rossi1, Benjamin Schmack2, Moshe Lakser3, Caroline Merithew4
1LAWCHA, 2University of Kansas, 3Seneca College, Toronto, ON, 4University of Dayton

Abstract

□ A Worker Writes History? I have been working on a social history of Colorado coal miners in the 1920s and an account of the 1927-28 Colorado miners' strike since 1982. I am not an academic and I do not have academic experience; I have done my research and writing as a union organizer and left activist, a worker and as someone whose family members and friends worked in the mines. Most of my research has been done with Colorado miners and their families and most of these people are now deceased. Certain questions have arisen from my research: can the assembly forms of workers' organizations be made relevant today? how do we understand and work with the pivotal people and institutions which make workers' mass movements possible? how do we understand the connections and lack of connections between workers' (sub)cultures, work communities and communities and our ability and willingness to act? How do workers live between dramatic moments of heightened class struggle? These questions have arisen from my study of Colorado miners in the 1920s, and I think that I am finding answers. More broadly, other questions have arisen as well as I have done my work: can an "ordinary" worker write working-class history? what do union activists get out of labor and working-class history? why do we do working-class history and how do we communicate the "why" of this to other workers? Does Brecht's poem "A Worker Reads History" have meaning today?

□ Combating the Klan: Constructing Radical Identity in Southern Illinois My work examines how the radical immigrant miners of Franklin County, Illinois preserved radical sentiment in southern Illinois during the mid 1920s, despite the decline of both the coal industry in the region and radicalism within the United Mine Workers of America (UMWA). In particular, this paper focuses on the grassroots activism and interethnic solidarity displayed by union miners from southern Illinois at the 42nd annual convention of the Illinois Federation of Labor in 1924. A number of mining representatives disrupted the proceedings and demanded that the conference openly denounce the Ku Klux Klan and their use of terrorism within mining communities. Despite the prevalence of Klan attacks on union members, the miners met heavy opposition from the UMWA hierarchy present at the convention. This was due to not only the political influence held by the KKK in the area, but also the southern Illinois organizers’ affiliation with the Communist party. The dispute at this convention spoke to the presence of a divided working class in southern Illinois that often erupted in violent conflicts based on ethnic identity, national origin, and radical affiliation. I contend that the events at this convention informed the next decade of labor disputes in the coalfields of Franklin County, in which the UMWA consistently neglected the needs of their immigrant members and left them exposed to repression at the hands of the coal operators, the KKK, and the courts. This paper differs from previous literature in that it pertains to trade union activism during the 1920s, which is a period often neglected by labor historians. Additionally, the majority of the existing literature neglects thorough analysis of Franklin County radicals, despite the remarkable window that they provide into the everyday realities and tribulations of immigrant miners during the 1920s.

□ Coal Miners and Class Identity in the Japanese Labor Movement: 1912-1928 The historiography of the Japanese labor movement in the early 20th century has typically adopted a simplistic narrative, pitting the burgeoning union movement against state and corporate efforts to ameliorate the so-called “labor problem” through appeals to industrial harmony. Within this narrative framework, the fate of an already-formed working class was determined through legislation, company reforms, and violent encounters. However, the mobilization of the working class by labor leaders, itself a contentious process demanding the articulation of a shared class identity, remains unexamined in these works.

My paper analyzes the challenges faced by local labor activists as they attempted to foster and articulate class identifications through an analysis of the works of two activists from the coal mining region of Chikuhō in southwest Japan: Mitsuyoshi Esshin and Hashimoto Eikichi.

Both Mitsuyoshi and Hashimoto are notable for being former coal miners who joined Japan’s national labor movement. Both were committed to mobilizing Chikuhō’s miners in the wake of increasingly violent unrest in the region. However, they faced a challenge in articulating a class identity for miners that both integrated the latter into the larger labor movement while appealing to existing regional and occupational identities. The resulting works betray an underlying tension between their affirmation of a class identity that crossed occupational boundaries and their tendency to ground their narrative of social revolution in the specificities of mining life. Consequently, in mobilizing their personal histories and intimate knowledge of mining work, both writers often reinforced claims that Japan’s miners possessed a unique occupational culture, which was simultaneously being used by industrialists to legitimize paternalistic management practices and challenge state or union intervention.

My paper thus explores two themes that permeated coalition building and class mobilization efforts in Japan. Firstly, it analyzes the tension between particularity and universality in class discourse. Secondly, it emphasizes the permeability of class identity itself and the indeterminacy of its articulation, especially in non-Western contexts.
