Traveling the World: Workers' Transnationalism

James Barrett1, Richard March2, Gigi Peterson3, Dana Frank4
1University of Illinois, Urbana, 2Independent Folklorist, 3State University of NY at Cortland, 4University of California-Santa Cruz

Abstract

□ Blue-Collar Cosmopolitans: Sailors, Pullman Porters, and Working-Class Sophistication in the Industrial Era The term “cosmopolitan” is rarely used in relation to working-class people. Yet some with broad perspectives and travel experience appear to qualify for the label – the globe-trotting immigrant, the well-read deep water sailor, the African American Pullman Porter, the socialist militant. Long before current notions of “globalization,” such workers linked their local communities with the broader world. Judging from autobiographies, some sailors were not only more widely-traveled, but also often better-read than middle class people. Aside from their cosmopolitan experience and perspective, maritime workers occupied a vital place in labor history, as they often provided the links required for transnational social and political movements. Likewise, Pullman Porters helped to spread Black freedom movement literature and ideas, connecting activists as they traveled through cities around the U.S. The socialist machinist’s or garment worker’s personal life may have been circumscribed by their factory walls and city neighborhood, but with the rise of socialist movements, her/his worldview, political goals, and strategies were inherently transnational. In placing such experiences in relation to our understandings of the cosmopolitan, we not only illuminate the lives of these workers but also broaden our notions of the cosmopolitan.

□ A Great Vision: An Activist Family’s History in the Fight for Social Justice Personal accounts by people who have directly experienced historical events can provide vivid and empathetic insights into history. Typically such accounts in the form of memoirs encompass the life span of one individual. Multi-generational family histories that comprise multiple voices are very rare. This presentation synopsizes one such work, my recently-released book “A Great Vision” published by Hardball Press. There is the rich oral tradition of my family’s narrative, of our Old World struggles as Croatian nationalists resisting nascent Italian fascism in the turbulent northern Adriatic region, our immigration story and arrival in industrial Chicago. A vivid picture of life in the midst of 1930s organizing efforts emerges out of the extensive excerpts from oral history interviews with my father Herb March, a key organizer of Chicago’s packinghouse workers, and personal experience stories from my mother Jane March, an initiator of the Back-of-the-Yards Neighborhood Council. The tragic story of the fates of my three uncles who, in the depths of the Great Depression, after a decade in the USA, returned to Europe to join an American-founded commune in the Soviet Union provides a glimpse into the little-studied phenomenon of migration of Americans to the USSR. How family values of struggle were conveyed to ensuing generations is revealed in the account of my own participation in the civil rights, peace, and student New Left movements in Los Angeles, Berkeley and Mississippi, and of my niece Sara’s principled stand as a tree-sitting environmental activist in northern California. The family story is useful to historians and imbued with relevant issues such as trans-nationalism and gender dynamics.

□ Chilean Exile Activists in Seattle: Solidarity Across Borders From the late 1970s to the present, the Seattle area’s political culture has been influenced by Chilean activists whose influence outstripped their relatively small numbers. Between 1976 and 1979, some forty Chilean families came to Seattle, part of an international diaspora fleeing the dictatorship of Augusto Pinochet, who had participated in a 1973 coup against Chile’s elected president, with support from the CIA and Nixon-Kissinger Administration. Over the years, many of these exiles worked to restore democracy in Chile by supporting popular organizations there and by reshaping US policy and public awareness. “Chileans for Democracy” (CFD), founded in Seattle in 1984 and built on precursor organizations, built coalitions with religious, human rights, labor, leftwing, and Latin American solidarity activists in the city and beyond. They influenced Seattle not only by drawing attention to Chilean politics but by recreating aspects of Chilean culture in public events. The CFD contributed to change in Chile, including a 1988 plebiscite against Pinochet, and his replacement by an elected president in 1990. In the early 1990s the CFD dissolved, though many individuals continued community and transnational activism. Members came to live what historian Dirk Hoerder has termed “transcultural lives” that bridge “competing cultures” and “permit moves and linkages” between them. This presentation will discuss the history of Seattle’s Chilean exile community and a project to preserve their history in the Labor Archives of Washington State (LAWS) at the University of Washington Libraries Special Collections.
