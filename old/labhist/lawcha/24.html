Imagining the Commonweal: Coxey's Army, Cox's March, and Working Class Political Conceptions of the Public Sphere, 1896-1932.

Wesley Bishop
Purdue University

Abstract

In 1896 Jacob Coxey, a populist reformer and small town business leader, led a group of unemployed workers on a march to Washington D.C. Covering hundreds of miles, and generating large amounts of newspaper coverage, Coxey and the other marchers used the conception of the "commonweal" as a central theme in their rhetoric to challenge popular Gilded Age ideas of the political economy. Routed at the Capitol Building by police, the protest ended with no formal action taken by Congress or the Cleveland administration to address public concerns of the economy. Decades later a radical labor priest, James Renshaw Cox, led a group of unemployed once again from Pennsylvania to D.C. Echoing many of the ideas of Coxey and the marchers of the 1890s, Cox was part of a growing segment of American Catholics who used the commonweal as a framework to reimagine how the public sphere, politics, and the economy should relate. Once again the marchers were largely ignored by the President, yet the marchers did gain the support of Hoover's Treasury Secretary, support that would eventually lead to political scandal and his impeachment. This paper is an examination of how working class political movements have reimagined the public sphere, specifically with the conception of the commonweal, and those reimaginings were, and still are, radical critiques of capitalist political economy. In a period where social movements like Occupy Wall Street and Black Lives Matter rely on the taking of public space, making radical critiques of political economy, and responding to hostile government responses, such a history is critical for academic and popular understanding.
