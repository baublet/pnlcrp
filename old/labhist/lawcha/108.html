Tales from the Boeing Workplace: Gender, Economic Crisis, and Corporate Restructuring at the World's Largest Aerospace Company

Andrew Hedden1, Polly Myers2, Leon Grunberg3, Tom Lux4, Larry Brown5, Stan Sorscher6
1Harry Bridges Center for Labor Studies, 2University of Washington, 3University of Puget Sound, 4Pacific Northwest Labor History Association, 5IAM District 751, 6SPEEA-IFPTE Local 2001

Abstract

This panel brings together four scholars whose work has examined the experiences of workers and their unions, at different times and from different perspectives, at the aerospace giant Boeing Company.

Polly Myer's work examines the familial culture of stability and belonging that set apart the Boeing Company in its youth and the evolution of its workplace culture in connection with social, political, and economic changes since.

Andrew Hedden's paper examines the responses of Boeing's largest union, International Association of Machinists District Lodge 751, to the company's near bankruptcy in the late 1960s and early 1970s.

In "Emerging from Turbulence," Leon Grunberg and Sarah Moore share in-depth interviews with 36 recent hires, longtime employees, and retirees to create a personal portrait of the significant changes that have occurred within Boeing over the last ten years, what they mean to the workers they affect, and how those changes are reshaping our attitudes towards work.
