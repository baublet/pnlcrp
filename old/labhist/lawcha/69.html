Abstract

Paper #1:

“The Present Slavery—The Coming Freedom”: Organized Labor and the Civil War Metaphor in the Gilded Age

Dyer Daniel Lum construed Decoration Day less as a day of collective mourning than one of collective action. Taking a “wider view of the field” on Decoration Day 1885, Lum, who had served as an officer in the 14th New York Cavalry, maintained that while United States soldiers such as himself had enlisted and fought the Civil War for principles of human liberty and the preservation of the Union, the war’s disastrous results included “the enrichment of army contractors; financial speculators in national necessities; the centralization of authority; the creation of monied aristocracy; and the tendency of industrial enterprises to concentrate into monopolies.” Although the war had enlarged the bounds of human freedom by freeing and “embracing the southern slaves as equals,” the aims of the Civil War were not anywhere near finished, Lum concluded. A “new war cry” was necessary to achieve genuine mental, political, and economic freedom. Having become a stanch anarcho-syndicalist activist and publisher and Labor Reform Party candidate after the war, Lum’s war memory was that of an unfinished revolution against material inequality. “In preserving the union we created a new union—of capital.”

By late April 1886, one day before a turn of events in Chicago’s Haymarket Square and the International Workers’ Day commemoration that followed altered forever the destinies of both Lum and the entire American labor movement, Lum published a piece entitled “American Workingmen are called upon to Arm Themselves.” Insisting that peaceable and electoral politics were necessary but insufficient, this “Old Soldier’s…Cry of Warning” proposed that American workers could either choose to be either “slaves” or “free men,” but that the latter would require directly confronting the extortive force of the Money Power and the “arbitrary states of the markets.” Indeed, in an about-face from the Civil War, the legions of bayonets now belonged to private capital and workingmen were left unto themselves to stake their own “emancipation.” Positing direct agitation as very much within the American republican and individualist traditions, he called on workers to “Be your own man…Seek your own self” in order to undermine their “economic masters.” “Personal independence,” Lum explained, was not subjugation by the fickleness of the invisible hand, but the “right to labor” as a social right. Contrasting capital aggregation and the “fixed” status of the working classes with the producerist ideal of the Civil War era, he probed, “Do you not realize…times have changed since our civil war, if your memory goes back beyond that event?”

This paper examines how Gilded Age workingmen used the language, symbols, and identity of the Civil War in oratory and official literature to imagine a “second Emancipation Proclamation.” Indeed, the conflict of 1861-1865, and chattel slavery and black emancipation in particular, proved a model for how black and white workingmen imagined, dialogued, and strategized about their own class and material conditions. Knights of Labor, Chicago anarchists, Federationists all consistently evoked the memory and rhetoric of the war era--“battle,” “master,” “slave,” “emancipation,” “abolition,”--often through formal and informal “Grand Army” and “Blue and the Gray” auxiliaries. Dyer Lum and others saw themselves as metaphorical soldiers in an army of human liberation. “We are, or should be, all soldiers in one army advancing to the conquest of one sole aim,” resolved one worker, avowing that the “different divisions of the grand army of labor” unify for “the struggle for the emancipation of the working class…as a successive series of skirmished.” Workingmen of various organizations and parties widely employed this “labor-veteran approach” because the war’s severity and urgency still reverberated through the public consciousness. Indeed, white workingmen, especially Union veterans, used a familiar martial language of slavery and emancipation--a message of radical change--even as they moved away from labor radicalism and toward respectability politics.

Paper #2:

Joined in Struggle: Civil War Veterans and the Search for Stability in America’s West, 1865-1930

The surrender of Confederate forces in the spring of 1865 marked the end of the war for most Union soldiers. These newly minted veterans from all backgrounds and walks of life returned to the homes they had left years before. Some, suffering from lingering disabilities, struggled to find stable employment and lived the remained of their lives in tenuous financial circumstances, relying on charity and federal aid for survival. Others flourished, parlaying their military service into successful careers and long-term financial stability for themselves and their families. Ever aware of opportunity, many veterans looked, migrating across the continent to California where they set down deep roots in new communities that grew there in the second half of the 19th century.

San Bernardino County emerged as an important transportation hub and citrus community during the later half of the 19th and early decades of the 20th century as men and women flooded west. Civil War veterans played a vital role to the growth of this community, first as pioneers and settlers and then as lawyers, doctors, farmers, and politicians. Men who fought to preserve the Union mustered out of military service and undertook a new and arduous task: community building. This paper focuses on two complimentary narratives. First, the social, political, and economic growth of Southern California and second, the role of Union veterans within these communities and as they became part of the great march of American progress that occurred during the decades after the war.

Broadly, the role that Civil War veterans as part of community building that occurred in the latter half of the 19th century in an understudied, yet important avenue by which we may expand our understanding of bot the impact of war on these men and their families and the place of these veterans within their communities. Traditionally, historians have approached the study of veterans from the same analytical framework. Utilizing letters, memoirs, and published accounts, historians have linked veterans together as part of a shared post-war experience in which these men were, inevitably, disconnected from their local communities. Their status as veterans, in other words, made them intrinsically different from other Americans and created a unique, but segregated, post-war existence for the men who donned blue in defense of the Union. They were, as one historian has noted, men “set apart” from American society. This paper will examine the ways in which a new methodological approach to the study of Civil War Veterans--focusing on one community and delving into the comparative experiences of over 400 men who migrated west and settled in one geographic area—can expand our historical understanding of the social, political, and economic place of veterans in the west during America’s rebirth in the post-Civil War era.

Paper #3

The Grand Army of the Social Republic: Civil War Veterans and the Making of American Socialism

The struggle for the survival of the Federal Union in 1861-1865 became a struggle for the victory of “Free Labor,” though those who struggled on its behalf understood this to mean different things. While ultimately, “the Second American Revolution” would give rise to a new birth of capitalism, many participants hoped for a different outcome, as reflected in the thousands of signatures urging radical land reform and redistribution during the course of this national crisis.

Some of these played a decisive role in the formation of the first socialist efforts in the U.S. Some had a great deal of prominent, such as Richard J. Hinton, who officered black soldiers in the West as part of that triracial Federal force there or Charles Matchett, a socialist presidential candidate before Gene Debs. More deeply, perhaps, we will examine some of the rank and file figures whose presence certainly shaped the actual feel of socialism at the local and state level.
