Giant Factories and Dreams of Modernity

Joshua Freeman1, Shelton Stromquist2, Nelson Lichtenstein3, Alice Kessler-Harris4
1City University of New York, 2University of Iowa, 3University of California, Santa Barbara, 4Emerita-Columbia University

Abstract

In recent decades, the largest factories in history have been built in China and Vietnam. Some sprawling shoe and electronics plants employ 100,000 workers or more, housing tens of thousands in adjacent dormitories. These industrial behemoths have been the sites of repeated labor conflicts, including a strike at a shoe factory in Ho Chi Minh City that involved 90,000 workers, one of the largest single-site strikes in history. The outsized factory – factories larger and more advanced than other contemporary facilities, that mark the forefront of industrial development – have been built repeatedly since the first English textile mills in the late eighteenth century. Over and over again they have been associated with visions of modernity and utopia, while often sites of intense labor exploitation and conflict.

This paper will compare contemporary factory giants with earlier episodes of industrial giantism, including English and American textile mills, U.S. steel factories, Ford’s Highland Park and River Rouge plants, the “gigant” factories of the Soviet Union’s First Five Year Plan (like the Stalingrad Tractor Factory and the Magnitogorsk steel complex), and the Nowa Huta Lenin steelworks in post-World War II Poland. It will discuss the economic and political reasons for building such very large scale factories, the differences and similarities of giant factories under capitalist and socialist regimes, their cultural role as emblems of modernity, and how workers experienced being at the heart of the largest industrial facilities of their age. The talk will be accompanied by images of industrial giantism through the years.
