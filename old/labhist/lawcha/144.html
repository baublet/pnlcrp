Mujeres Trabajadoras: Labor, Power, and Gender in Latino History

Delia Fernandez1, Alyssa Ribeiro2, Mauricio Castro3, Lilia Fernandez4
1Michigan State University, 2Allegheny College, 3Purdue University, 4Rutgers University

Abstract

Recent scholarship has shown how Latina/o experiences in the United States are shaped by national origins, governmental reception, regional economies, and local demographics. This panel builds upon that literature by providing three diverse case studies that illustrate the experiences of different Latino groups, more specifically the women in these communities, through the lens of work and labor relations in the mid-twentieth century. In the disparate locations of Grand Rapids, Michigan, Miami, and Philadelphia, labor served as the hub around which these communities accumulated economic, social, and political capital, and through which they built identities. Work determined the areas in which they lived, the institutions they interacted with, and the activities they engaged in outside of work. Latinas actively sought to engage and affect their environments as they adjusted to local circumstances and created survival strategies for themselves and their families. While the bulk of Latina/o labor history has centered on rural experiences in earlier decades, this panel focus on life in urban centers during the Cold War era and deindustrialization.

The subjects of Delia Fernandez’s study of West Michigan encountered a labor market that did not allow for families to fit comfortably within urban/industrial and rural/agricultural divides. Starting in the 1940s and through the 1970s, Latinas challenged these artificial divisions through the planning and executing of seasonal adaptation, split households, and regional mobility as they contributed to their family economies. So too, the presence of Cuban exiles in Miami reshaped local labor markets and welfare provision. Mauricio Castro demonstrates how Cuban exiles, particularly women, leveraged their experience and educational backgrounds and took advantage of an unprecedented level of federal largesse throughout the 1960s and into the early 1970s. These aid programs helped establish the Cuban community’s power in South Florida while simultaneously serving as a social laboratory for federal work education and welfare programs. In Philadelphia, Puerto Rican and African American workers faced a rapidly deindustrializing landscape. Organized women workers responded in part by challenging their union leadership throughout the 1970s. By supporting diverse candidate slates in union elections and balking at union officials’ decrees, these workers took on local neoliberalization and sought to create more equitable and inclusive work sites.

In each of these circumstances, Latina/o workers confronted large economic and political questions with impacts far beyond Michigan, Florida, and Pennsylvania. How could working-class people survive in a piecemeal labor market? How should the government assist with job training and placement? How could workers influence huge economic transformations through local venues? Relatively recent migration patterns and ethnically segmented labor markets disproportionately exposed Latina/os to shifts in the structural forces undergirding urban economies. This panel thus provides telling examples of how everyday people adjusted to changing economies and social realities in the middle of the twentieth century.
