Turn of the Century Technê: Anarchist Newspapers as Social Media in Los Angeles, Borderlands, and Mexico in the Early Twentieth Century

David Struthers
Copenhagen Business School

Abstract

The vast cultural production of the transnational radical community in Los Angeles, California and the borderlands is exceeded by the overlapping network of individual and organizational connections that sustained it. By the early twentieth century the shrinking of space and time through technological change facilitated the ability of anarchists to turn a global movement into reality. Through personal networks, mass meetings, and print media anarchists in Los Angeles formulated the terms of their struggle, coordinated their actions, broadcasted their views to a broader tier of supporters and sympathizers, and attempted to spread their message to the public at large. Anarchist newspapers printed in Los Angeles, such as Regeneración, maintained a nearly-global readership as improvements in transportation technologies lowered the cost of international mail. During this period the leftist press as a whole fostered an imagined global community of radicals, which served as the base for expressions of solidarity. At moments of local crisis, usually during a strike or the imprisonment of participants, organizers sent letters and telegrams communicating appeals for financial contributions, and supporters responded.

Newspapers functioned as the most public manifestation of a layered radical community spanning individual association, organizational solidarities, and the printed page. In this sense, anarchist newspapers in the early twentieth century functioned as “social media” in a remarkably similar way as digital social media today. Further, the necessity of using mail delivery service to the transport newspapers and letters across distance became their biggest weakness. Most governments around the world—democratic or not—relied on their control of mail service to refuse delivery of publications deemed objectionable and to monitor personal communications. Governments controlled this crucial piece of the dissemination infrastructure. This paper will provide an examination of a movement that remained predominantly analogue in its communication technologies, but global in its reach.
