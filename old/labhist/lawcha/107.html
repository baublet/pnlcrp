Civil Liberties and Anticolonialism in a Time of War: The National Labor Defense Council, 1917-18

David Brundage
University of California, Santa Cruz

Abstract

Founded by labor lawyer Frank P. Walsh shortly after the US entry into World War I, the National Labor Defense Council (NLDC) has been all but forgotten. Avoided by most pro-war progressives and ridiculed by AFL president Samuel Gompers, the poorly-funded NLDC proved even less effective in defending antiwar activists than Roger Baldwin’s National Civil Liberties Bureau (forerunner of the ACLU). Nonetheless, as my paper will show, the links that the NLDC
forged between antiwar labor radicals and anticolonial activists (especially those working within America’s small South Asian population)—along with Walsh’s own experiences defending such activists—paved the way for a vibrant post-war anticolonial coalition, exemplified by the founding of the broadly-based League of Oppressed Peoples in 1919 and the emergence of the Farmer-Labor political upsurge of 1920.

Drawing heavily on the voluminous Frank P. Walsh collection in the New York Public Library, my paper provides an elaboration of themes that I discuss in my forthcoming essay, “The Easter Rising and New York’s Anticolonial Nationalists,” in Ireland’s Allies: America and the 1916 Easter Rising, ed. Miriam Nyhan Grey (University College Dublin Press, 2016).
