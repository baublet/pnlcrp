Love of Labor Lost? Religion, Human Rights, and the Argentine “Dirty War”

Susan Breitzer
Independent Scholar

Abstract

The Argentine “Dirty War,” which followed the 1976 anti-Peron coup and the international human rights movement it sparked are well-studied topics. This paper, touching on the conference subtheme of War and Empire will spotlight the activist role of religious leaders such as Rabbi Marshall T. Meyer, who was well known for his role in promoting domestic and international social justice and for bringing the widespread human rights abuses in Argentina to public attention. Meyer's role in raising awareness of the human rights abuses of the Argentine junta is laudable in terms of bringing social justice concerns into the Jewish and general religious mainstream. However, it is also important to look at Meyer's role (and that of similar religious leaders) in downplaying the labor aspects of the Argentine human rights tragedy, the better to deflect negative associations of human rights with political leftism in the waning Cold War, and to broaden the base of support. These efforts, in turn, may have played into the hands of the Videla regime, which pointedly used terror to silence the workers' movements that formed an important component of the opposition. Meyer and his like-minded clerical colleagues therefore may also have contributed to the marginalization of labor and class issues from the progressive religious activism they helped to create, and with it, the popular conception of human rights.
