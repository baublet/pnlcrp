From National to Transnational: An Exploration of Changing Configurations of Labor Solidarities during the Cold War

Yevette Richards1, Lara Vapnek2, Quenby Hughes3, John Stoner4, Will Jones5
1George Mason University, 2St. John’s University, 3Rhode Island College, 4University of Pittsburgh, 5University of Minnesota

Abstract

The roundtable will explore the ideas, tensions, conflicts, and strategies driving the activism of individuals and informing the policy perspectives of labor organizations and governments in their quest to speak on behalf of workers’ concerns and shape transnational worker solidarities during the Cold War. In the Cold War struggle from 1947 to the fall of the Berlin Wall, national and transnational labor organizations have fallen along a spectrum from voluntary to coercive compliance to the foreign policy objectives of the countries and regions under which they operated. At other times they have sought to influence the policy perspectives of governments to support their own foreign policy positions and economic objectives. During and after the period of decolonization, the divisions between East and West intersected with the divisions of North and South to produce a variegated politics shaped by identities of race, gender, nation, and ethnicity. This roundtable seeks to understand how these disparate identities influenced rhetoric, perspectives and activism, and solidarities in a transnational context. In what ways did activists and labor organizations react to the Cold War confrontation between a bipolar order and the rising tide of neutralism? How did contact across borders affect the way individual labor activists assessed the Cold War, anti-colonialism, and inequalities and injustices within the various blocs? To what extent did the mapping of the East-West conflict onto the newly independent governments produce corrupting influences and political differences that constricted movement toward a greater transnational labor solidarity? How did the Cold War affect transnational labor’s views of solutions to global economic disparities?

Yevette Richards will examine how Cold war and decolonization affected the relationship between Ugandan labor and government officials and the non-communist global labor body, the International Confederation of Free Trade Unions, which operated a labor college in the country.

This study exposes fissures within the Ugandan government that had repercussions for the labor movement, some of whose leaders doubled as government officials. Ethnic rivalries, gender politics, and internal power plays within Uganda were mapped onto a Cold War landscape in which the ICFTU found itself challenged to support major tenets of pan-Africanism without abdicating its principles of trade unions free from government control.

Lara Vapnek will discuss American women’s participation in the Women’s International Democratic Federation (WIDF) an organization formed in Paris in 1945 to fight fascism, promote gender equality, and support decolonization. In 1949, the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) accused the WIDF, and its American affiliate, the Congress of American Women, of being communist front organizations. This episode suggests the challenges American activists faced in building left-feminist alliances across national boundaries during the Cold War.

Quenby Hughes will discuss the intersection between AFL and CIO Cold War international and domestic policy/rhetoric of the AFL and the CIO, specifically as it relates to race and racism at home.

John Stoner will discuss the implications of the AFL-CIO’s engagement with tenets of modernization theory in its outreach efforts to fledgling labor organizations, especially those in the Africa.
In post-Nkrumah Ghana, the AFL-CIO’s African American Labor Center (AALC) partnered with USAID to propose an ambitious scheme for workers’ housing. The narrative of the project’s failure includes a critique of labor-government collusion that prioritized political ends over social good.
