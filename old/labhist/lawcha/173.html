A Century of Pacific Labor for the United States

Holger Droessler1, JoAnna Poblete2, Roneva Keel3, Kornel Chang4
1Bard College, 2Claremont Graduate University, 3University of Washington, Seattle, 4Rutgers University

Abstract

This panel provides a diverse overview of issues and experiences facing laborers in and from the Pacific, who have toiled in difficult but essential manual labor jobs for the growth and protection of the United States throughout the twentieth century. Starting with Roneva Keel’s study of early twentieth century sugar production in the U.S. West and the Philippines, she explores how the transpacific migrations of workers highlight the ways in which the colonization of Hawai‘i and the Philippines was tied to processes of settler colonialism in the West. In Holger Droessler’s paper, he focuses on Pacific Islander war workers during World War Two. Faced with pervasive racism and demeaning work conditions, these laborers managed to create new opportunities both for themselves and for their home societies. And finally in JoAnna Poblete’s essay, she examines the variable labor standards that were created in the unincorporated territory of American Sāmoa and supported by the U.S. federal government. Debates surrounding the minimum wage issue in American Sāmoa has loomed over the tuna canning industry on-island since the 1950s and culminated in major shifts to policies and jobs in 2009. All three papers examine some aspect of workers laboring under liminal and often invisible statuses as non-citizens, while being pressured to conform to continental U.S. expectations, standards, ideologies, and authority.
