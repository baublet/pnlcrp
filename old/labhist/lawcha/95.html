Chilean Exile Activists in Seattle: Solidarity Across Borders

Gigi Peterson
State University of NY at Cortland

Abstract

From the late 1970s to the present, the Seattle area’s political culture has been influenced by Chilean activists whose influence outstripped their relatively small numbers. Between 1976 and 1979, some forty Chilean families came to Seattle, part of an international diaspora fleeing the dictatorship of Augusto Pinochet, who had participated in a 1973 coup against Chile’s elected president, with support from the CIA and Nixon-Kissinger Administration. Over the years, many of these exiles worked to restore democracy in Chile by supporting popular organizations there and by reshaping US policy and public awareness. “Chileans for Democracy” (CFD), founded in Seattle in 1984 and built on precursor organizations, built coalitions with religious, human rights, labor, leftwing, and Latin American solidarity activists in the city and beyond. They influenced Seattle not only by drawing attention to Chilean politics but by recreating aspects of Chilean culture in public events. The CFD contributed to change in Chile, including a 1988 plebiscite against Pinochet, and his replacement by an elected president in 1990. In the early 1990s the CFD dissolved, though many individuals continued community and transnational activism. Members came to live what historian Dirk Hoerder has termed “transcultural lives” that bridge “competing cultures” and “permit moves and linkages” between them. This presentation will discuss the history of Seattle’s Chilean exile community and a project to preserve their history in the Labor Archives of Washington State (LAWS) at the University of Washington Libraries Special Collections.
