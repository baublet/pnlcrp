Reclaiming Working Class Studies

Theresa Case1, Keri Leigh Merritt2, Bob Hutton3, Dana Caldemeyer4
1University of Houston-Downtown, 2Independent Scholar, 3University of Tennessee, 4South Georgia State College

Abstract

As scholars of labor and working class history, our research addresses the people who comprise the largest proportion of the population in any given time or place. In an age of rapidly intensifying economic disparity and an increase in workers who "vote against their interests," labor and working class historians are especially positioned to address some of the most pressing issues of our age. Despite these facts, however, the fields of labor and working class history seem to suffer from the same "decline" as the working class itself. Why?

We propose to hold a roundtable discussion in which a younger generation of working class and labor scholars assess the field in an effort to answer this question. Our panel will discuss what scholars have done right, where we can improve, and how we should develop the field to the benefit of the historical discipline as a whole. It is our hope that this panel will stimulate deeper thought and conversation among our colleagues and lead to changes that can renew interest and appreciation for working class studies.
