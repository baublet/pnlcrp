Speaking for Ourselves, To Each Other: LELO’s Worker to Worker Organizing from the 1970s to the Present

Trevor Griffey1, Harley Bird2, Michael Woo3, Michael Simmons4, Cindy Domingo2, Ricardo Ortega2
1N/A, 2LELO, 3LELO & Got Green?, 4Raday Salon

Abstract

This panel will provide an opportunity for labor activists affiliated with LELO (Legacy of Equality, Leadership and Organizing) to informally describe their more than four decades of community-based worker to worker organizing. LELO is a social movement formation that is unique to Seattle— a community based labor organization that is part worker center, part international human rights organization, and part non-profit organization. It grew out of an alliance between black construction workers and Filipino cannery workers in early 1970s Seattle, and evolved into a network of workers of color in Seattle who connect their struggles for jobs in Seattle to worker struggles against capitalism and imperialism around the world.

The panel will include participants from some of the different parts of LELO’s activism over its more than four decades, and provide each with the opportunity to connect the history of their activism to contemporary issues of labor rights and social justice. • Trevor Griffey will moderate the roundtable, but will not present. • Harley Bird will describe the worker to worker organizing of the United Construction Workers Association (UCWA), which represented black construction workers who entered Seattle’s building trades under court order. • Michael Woo will describe how the UCWA founded LELO as part of its effort to develop a united front with in struggle to desegregate the American workplace in the 1970s, working closely with Filipino cannery workers. • Michael Simmons, if he is able to attend, will talk about co-founding LELO and helping organize eight black worker centers in Arkansas, Louisiana, Texas and Oklahoma in 1973. • Cindy Domingo will describe the importance of LELO’s internationalism to its ongoing labor and human rights activism, drawing from her experience as a longtime leader of women’s delegations to Cuba and Philippine solidarity work. • Ricardo Ortega will describe the work of LELO to facilitate worker-to-worker networks globally, in order to place workers rather than government bureaucrats and business people at the center of the project to rewrite the rules of the global economy.
