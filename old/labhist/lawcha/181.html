Abstract

□ “The Question of ‘Self-Determination’”: HUAC Investigates the Specter of Cross-Border Communism in Texas, 1938-39 The creation of the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) in spring 1938 coincided with a wave of Communist Party (CP) activism in Texas toward unifying Mexican Americans and their organizations. This campaign sparked fears among anticommunists of the northward infiltration of communists from Mexico over a period of tolerance for the Party there during the leftist presidency of Lazaro Cardenas. The CP did not create this activism, which was fostered by the consular offices of Mexico in Texas, but it did actively seek to co-opt it. The stated goals of the consular project were the uplift and unity of Mexicans living in Texas to achieve both their well-being there and also aid, and in some essential skills cases return, to Mexico. Anticommunists feared that the permeable border would enable circulation of communists, including possibly thousands of returning Spanish Civil War veterans of the international brigades offered a home by the Cardenas government. Would an independent state of Mexican Americans closely allied with Mexico emerge during a revolutionary crisis? HUAC alarm over this possibility predates the escalation of anticommunism over fall 1939 to summer 1941 during the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact. HUAC investigation into fears of Mexican, not just Russian, communism and the potential radicalization and unification of the largest minority group of the Southwest, while African American activism escalated in the Southeast, inserts the southern border as a key component of our understanding of race and region in the evolution of anticommunism.

□ "Our sweat and our blood have fallen on this land to make other men rich”: Narrative, Context and the Articulation of Workers’ Dreams in El Plan de Delano “To those who oppose us, be they ranchers, police, politicians, or speculators, we say that we are going to continue fighting until we die, or we win.”

What were the ideological foundations of the Plan of Delano, drafted in 1966 by Cesar Chavez and Luis Valdez? How did Emiliano Zapata’s agrarian dreams, defined in 1911 in the Plan of Ayala, serve as a guide in the crafting of El Plan de Delano? In his commentary to the Plan, Valdez wrote: “It was decided by all involved that a statement of some kind was in order.” In tailoring Zapata’s ambitious “oratory” to the needs of Californias “humble” campesinos, Valdez described the adaptation of a revolutionary document with the goal of setting social justice ideals and demands down in words. Essentially serving as a human rights document, the Plan’s character was defined by a non-violent approach to redress the social suffering of farm workers in search of a broad coalition of support. Was the document subversive in its context or necessary for the rebalancing of labour asymmetries defined by racial oppression?

□ Turn of the Century Technê: Anarchist Newspapers as Social Media in Los Angeles, Borderlands, and Mexico in the Early Twentieth Century The vast cultural production of the transnational radical community in Los Angeles, California and the borderlands is exceeded by the overlapping network of individual and organizational connections that sustained it. By the early twentieth century the shrinking of space and time through technological change facilitated the ability of anarchists to turn a global movement into reality. Through personal networks, mass meetings, and print media anarchists in Los Angeles formulated the terms of their struggle, coordinated their actions, broadcasted their views to a broader tier of supporters and sympathizers, and attempted to spread their message to the public at large. Anarchist newspapers printed in Los Angeles, such as Regeneración, maintained a nearly-global readership as improvements in transportation technologies lowered the cost of international mail. During this period the leftist press as a whole fostered an imagined global community of radicals, which served as the base for expressions of solidarity. At moments of local crisis, usually during a strike or the imprisonment of participants, organizers sent letters and telegrams communicating appeals for financial contributions, and supporters responded.

Newspapers functioned as the most public manifestation of a layered radical community spanning individual association, organizational solidarities, and the printed page. In this sense, anarchist newspapers in the early twentieth century functioned as “social media” in a remarkably similar way as digital social media today. Further, the necessity of using mail delivery service to the transport newspapers and letters across distance became their biggest weakness. Most governments around the world—democratic or not—relied on their control of mail service to refuse delivery of publications deemed objectionable and to monitor personal communications. Governments controlled this crucial piece of the dissemination infrastructure. This paper will provide an examination of a movement that remained predominantly analogue in its communication technologies, but global in its reach.

□ Silence Sets the Agenda: LULAC's Inactivity during the 1946 Copper Strike and the CoalescenceThis study explores how the League of United Latin American Citizens earned a reputation for being an out-of-touch middle-class organization. Throughout the 1930s, local LULAC councils actively pursued community campaigns that matched the Mexican American civil rights organization’s larger agenda, especially against issues related to educational, social, and economic discrimination. World War II interrupted LULAC’s civil rights efforts both nationally and locally. After the war, the organization regrouped under a unified national agenda that made access to equal education LULAC’s most important priority. Therefore, as the El Paso copper workers struck for national wage standards, El Paso Local Council No. 132 had other civil rights concerns. Actions by leaders of El Paso’s Mexican American community indicated that they thought advancing LULAC’s national policy was more important than addressing immediate local issues. Mexican American workers, in turn, looked to the CIO and other unions for support. LULAC’s lack of response to the strike and the Mexican American workers in El Paso helps explain why many scholars have characterized LULAC as an out-of-touch middle class organization. Nevertheless, in 1946 LULAC did lead a successful campaign in the Mendez v. Westminster case against school segregation in California. Therefore, during the post-war period, LULAC continued to fight for civil rights through legal action and electoral politics. But the focus of these efforts had changed—and narrowed.
