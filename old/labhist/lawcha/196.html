Organizing the Academy: Strategies and Structures

Dan Jacoby1, William A. Herbert2, Lillian Taiz3, Naomi Williams4, Ken Mash5
1University of Washington, 2National Center for the Study of Collective Bargaining in Higher Education and the Professions, Hunter College, 3California Faculty Association, Past-President, 4The College at Brockport-SUNY, 5East Stroudsburg University

Abstract

□ Part-time Faculty and Collective Bargaining Outcomes Controversy swirls over the extent to which faculty unionization benefits part-time or contingent faculty. Many contingent faculty are dubious that bargaining units controlled by full time or tenure track faculty do or can represent their interests. Indeed, not all tenure track faculty embrace the idea of regularizing appointments for contingent faculty or improving their conditions lest that weaken the institution of tenure or the conditions tenured and full time faculty experience. However, very little data analysis has been presented in order to indicate contemporary outcomes related to contingent faculty. By merging several data bases including the National Center for the Study of Higher Education's Directory of Collective Bargaining we are able to begin assessing some outcomes including the extent to which results diverge where contingent faculty are a) not represented b) where they are including in general units with tenure track faculty, and c) where they create separate units. Where the data allows, this paper will focus on earning and upon the reliance of institutions upon part-time faculty.

□ The Winds of Changes Shift: An Analysis of Recent Growth in Bargaining Units and Representation Efforts in Higher Education This presentation will include an analysis of completed and pending questions of representation in 2016 involving faculty in higher education. The 2016 data demonstrates that the rate of newly certified units at private colleges and universities far outpaced new units in the public sector. The presentation will also examine the private and public sector legal principles underlying the composition of faculty bargaining units, i.e., whether tenured and tenure track faculty should be in the same or separate bargaining unit, and how the1980 Yeshiva University decision continues to impact faculty unionization and unit composition at private colleges and university.

□ Building Union Power: Contract Fights, Budget Battles, and the Future of the University This paper examines the California Faculty Association's transition to Social Justice unionism which fights for the rights of contingent labor as well as being advocates for our students, fighting for resources for our institutions, to resisting corporatization/privatization of our universities, and fighting for racial justice. In recent years we have fought back against student fee increases, we have successfully challenged legislative efforts to allow private vendors sell their online courses to the university system as for credit courses, we have joined the efforts of Ethnic Studies faculty to preserve and expand ethnic studies in our system, and we have negotiated a contract provided important economic and non-economic gains for both non-tenured and tenured faculty.
