Gender Politics and Labor Activism in Mexican American History

Tiffany J. González1, Joel Zapata2, Alejandra C Garza3, Katherine Bynum4, Michael Aguirre5, Cynthia T. Renteria6, Casandra Rincones7
1Texas A & M University, 2Southern Methodist University, 3University of Texas - Austin, 4Texas Christian University, 5University of Washington - Seattle, 6University of Texas - El Paso, 7Lone Star College-Kingwood

Abstract

This roundtable focuses on new histories that include gender politics and activist labor In Mexican American history from the 1940s to 1994. Alejandra Garza’s research examines the Texas vaquero through the lens of gender, race, and class, to explain the complexities of working class Mexicans in South Texas. She brings in works from Andrez Tijerina and Armando Alanzo to highlight the histories of ranchers and settlers, as well as the changes that occurred in ranching during the mid to late twentieth century. Katherine E. Bynum’s work analyzes the lived experiences of Elvira Portugal, a Mexican American businesswoman, who established the Dallas Tortilla and Tamale Factory in the heart of Little Mexico. Elvira’s story provides insight to women’s working commitments and how their dual duties to work and home helped sustain their families as well as the community. Michael D. Aguirre’s examines a pivotal movement that developed at the end of the Bracero Program in 1964. Through agriculture, transnational companies, and use of labor laws, Aguirre reveals the meanings of the borderlands working class, a transnational labor group that challenged nationalist notions of an American working class. Tiffany J. Gonzalez’s discussion focuses on the life of Maria Cardenas, the first Mexican American woman to serve on San Angelo’s city council. She will bring forth a nuanced discussion of labor and gender politics that improved the Mexican American community. Cynthia T. Renteria’s talk looks at La Mujer Obrera, an independent organization located in El Paso, Tex. The presentation considers the history of LMO beginning from organizing women in the garment industry, and the impact of the North American Free Trade Agreement. Lastly, Joel Zapata’s study discusses women’s transnational response to the North American Free Trade Agreement.
