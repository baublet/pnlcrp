Labor and Working-Class History in the North American West: The State of the Field

Stacey Smith1, Ryan Dearinger2, Laurie Mercier3, Matthew Basso4, James Naylor5
1Oregon State University, 2Eastern Oregon University, 3Washington State University Vancouver, 4University of Utah, 5Brandon University, Manitoba

Abstract

As LAWCHA comes to Seattle, the site of the historic 1919 general strike, we propose a roundtable that will assess the state of the field of labor and working-class history in the North American West. Labor and the struggles of working people have long been a focus of western history, from the 1960s gold rush studies of Rodman Paul to the birth of the “new western history” in the 1980s. In the last two decades, however, labor and working-class history has come to play an increasingly minor role in the broader field of western history. Western labor history panels are scarce at national conferences, fewer Ph.D. students tackle western labor history topics in their dissertations, and (despite some recent award-winning books) monographs on western labor history are few and far between. The panelists for this session, all established western labor historians, will address the decline of the field by suggesting new ways in which labor and working-class history can offer a critical framework for understanding the region's past. Many of the panelists focus on workers’ migration and mobility and they will explore how western historians can use the conceptual frameworks of borderlands and transnational history for analyzing the region’s labor history. Panelists will also explore how interjecting labor into western history can recast regional narratives of race and gender.
