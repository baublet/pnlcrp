Abstract

□ Interwoven Identities: Gender, Caste and Community in Mill Work in Colonial Madras This paper is about the processes of integration of women into the workforce of the cotton textile industry in colonial Madras. It examines the social perspectives and cultural framework that shaped life and work for the woman worker. Women played an active, though under-appreciated, role in unionization, lightning strikes and drawing workers into the freedom movement. Women waged workplace battles to challenge patriarchal attempts to subordinate them and helped frame legislative interventions and bring subaltern questions to the nationalist discourse. Life at the mill, influenced by ideas of equality of gender, common citizenship and contract but constrained by colonial subservience to authority, produced a caricatured modernity that strained against its conservative moorings in caste, community and gender. We trace the construction of notions of gender, community and class among the cotton mill labourers, and their interconnections. We examine perceptions of the state and its institutions, the unions and their practices.

□ The Watsonville Canning Strike, 1985-87 Thirty years ago, Watsonville California was the site of a successful 18 month strike by 1,000 frozen food workers, most of them Mexican immigrant women. They fought off an attempt to decertify their union, forced the plant owner into foreclosure, and settled with the new owner after a five-day wildcat. Besides revitalizing what had been a virtually moribund Teamster local, the strike was a serious bid for political power by Watsonville's emerging Mexican majority.

□ Rethinking Precarious Work and Care through Janitorial Workers’ Struggle

in South Korea Neoliberal restructuring after the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis in South Korea produced a sharp division between regular and contingent workers, creating a new legal category of worker pijŏnggyujik. Examining female janitorial workers’ struggle in the context of the new precarious class formation, this paper discusses the deepening gap between those who perform unpaid care work and those who are perceived as valuable subjects of care in the neoliberal labor regime. Weaving the narratives of female janitorial workers who perform various forms of unpaid domestic care work for their young and old family members, this paper delineates changing boundaries between the public and the private spheres. It reveals how aging female bodies are situated in the chain between the paid labor and the role of a caretaker at home, and how they contribute to the crisis-driven economy through double surplus value creation in both realms. In doing so, I argue that the new precarity is instituted and acted upon the vulnerable bodies through the old forms of regionally specific patriarchal and capitalist social relations.
