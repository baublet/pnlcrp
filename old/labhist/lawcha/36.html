Labor, Politics, and Social Movements: Chicanx Histories in the Northern Borderlands

Oscar Castañeda1, Norma Cárdenas2, Josué Q. Estrada3, Jerry Garcia4, Erasmo Gamboa1
1University of Washington, 2Eastern Washington University, 3Univerity of Washington, 4Northern Arizona University

Abstract

Historians, sociologists, demographers and scholars in other fields have taken greater notice of the rapidly increasing presence of Chicanx throughout the United States. Chicanx form a majority of the overall Latino population, which has become the largest under-represented group in the nation. Indeed, there are significant Chicanx communities in all fifty states. In addressing this phenomenon, this panel will address specifically the historical and current conditions of Chicanx beyond the well-studied Southwestern United States and focus on the Pacific Northwest. The papers have a multidisciplinary and interdisciplinary focus, addressing colonialism, gender, history, immigration, labor, literature, sociology, education, and religion. This panel builds on the historiography of the Chicanx presence in the Pacific Northwest and examines various forms of resistance, community mobilization, culture and how people negotiated their precarious position in regions that lacked a critical mass of Chicanax. More important, the panelists illuminate how Chicanx overcame their racialization, marginalization, and isolation in the Northern Borderlands by using their sense of identity and the search for social justice.
