Radical Religion: The Catholic Church and the War on Poverty in Brooklyn

Cassie Miller
Southern Poverty Law Center

Abstract

The Catholic Church was (and remains) defined by boundaries – between parishes, dioceses, and priests and parishioners. By the late 1960s, those boundaries were also racial and political. The majority of Catholic parishioners were white and conservative, but a small and active contingent of black Catholic churches emerged to challenge the politics of their coreligionists. This paper will examine the alliances that arose to overcome parish and racial boundaries in Brooklyn and, in particular, how a contingent of “radical” priests and nuns allied with liberal white Catholics, black and Puerto Rican Catholics, community activists, and like-minded organizations to spearhead a number of progressive programs – including non-profit housing corporations and welfare rights organizations – that were inspired by the Gospel but performed in the streets of the city. Significantly, the majority of these efforts were made possible through the Great Society’s Office of Economic Opportunity, as well as a “diocesan war on poverty.” This paper thus explores the combined efforts of the Church and the state to intervene in systems of poverty and discrimination, but also chronicles their ultimate failures. We are rich in histories of the Great Society from the perspective of politicians and bureaucrats, but this paper provides a grassroots perspective of the community institutions that carried out the OEO’s mission – from its well-funded initial outburst to its slow withdrawal that crippled progressive organizations. This paper will argue that the Catholic Church is an exemplary case study for examining the failures of the War on Poverty because it allows historians to view the program in microcosm, revealing how progressive, interracial efforts to alleviate poverty were abandoned to fund those that, as one Brooklyn Catholic argued, would not disrupt “the sanctity of the status quo.” The Church, like the state, failed to truly challenge the boundaries of inequality in American cities.
