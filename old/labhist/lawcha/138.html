Coal Miners and Class Identity in the Japanese Labor Movement: 1912-1928

Moshe Lakser
Seneca College, Toronto, ON

Abstract

The historiography of the Japanese labor movement in the early 20th century has typically adopted a simplistic narrative, pitting the burgeoning union movement against state and corporate efforts to ameliorate the so-called “labor problem” through appeals to industrial harmony. Within this narrative framework, the fate of an already-formed working class was determined through legislation, company reforms, and violent encounters. However, the mobilization of the working class by labor leaders, itself a contentious process demanding the articulation of a shared class identity, remains unexamined in these works.

My paper analyzes the challenges faced by local labor activists as they attempted to foster and articulate class identifications through an analysis of the works of two activists from the coal mining region of Chikuhō in southwest Japan: Mitsuyoshi Esshin and Hashimoto Eikichi.

Both Mitsuyoshi and Hashimoto are notable for being former coal miners who joined Japan’s national labor movement. Both were committed to mobilizing Chikuhō’s miners in the wake of increasingly violent unrest in the region. However, they faced a challenge in articulating a class identity for miners that both integrated the latter into the larger labor movement while appealing to existing regional and occupational identities. The resulting works betray an underlying tension between their affirmation of a class identity that crossed occupational boundaries and their tendency to ground their narrative of social revolution in the specificities of mining life. Consequently, in mobilizing their personal histories and intimate knowledge of mining work, both writers often reinforced claims that Japan’s miners possessed a unique occupational culture, which was simultaneously being used by industrialists to legitimize paternalistic management practices and challenge state or union intervention.

My paper thus explores two themes that permeated coalition building and class mobilization efforts in Japan. Firstly, it analyzes the tension between particularity and universality in class discourse. Secondly, it emphasizes the permeability of class identity itself and the indeterminacy of its articulation, especially in non-Western contexts.
