Abstract

The post-Stonewall gay rights movement of the 1970s formed successful communities of support and cultural liberation, especially in urban centers along the east and west coasts. But by 1977 a virulent national network of political backlash was on the move. Campaigns fueled by religion-based fear pressed to rescind all LGBT civil rights reforms and to support sanctions against employment of LGBT workers in education and social services. The religious right made gains in some communities and regions, but was not as strong in the San Francisco Bay Area, where resistance was vigorous. There, queer communities included revolutionary leftists whose organizations affirmed sexual self-determination. Independent issue-oriented lesbian groups organized their own community-based actions and educational programs as expressions of their socialist ethics. Starting with the Coors Beer Boycott in 1974, organized labor in the Bay Area made common cause with union-minded queer activists. LGBT labor coalitions grew in other regions, including the Pacific Northwest, where during the 1980s and 1990s queer unionists rallied with labor to defeat right-wing anti-gay electoral initiatives that would threaten the livelihoods and safety of LGBT union members.
