“Badly Need[ed]…Capital”: Organized Labor, Trade Policy, and Visions of Globalization at the end of Bretton Woods

Andrew Elrod
University of California, Santa Barbara

Abstract

Amid the historic fracturing of the American political system during Nixon's second term, it was business leaders and administration officials who embarked on an ambitious journey to open investment and trade markets abroad, including in the Communist countries. The Trade Act of 1974 was instrumental in this strategy. The attachment of American union leaders to an anti-Communist foreign policy kept them looking backwards, opposed to international collective bargaining in Europe, bereft of solutions to the problems of global economic integration. Their inability to bargain over employer investment decisions and the historic weakness of American full-employment policies left protectionism as one of the only tools to maintain domestic employment. For these reasons labor found itself opposed to détente, confronting an administration portraying investment regulations—capital controls—as not only illiberal diplomacy, but as restraints on domestic economic growth and full employment.In the minds of C. Fred Bergsten, Pete Peterson, and other cabinet officials including George Shultz, Herbert Stein, and William Simon, capital mobility, the destabilizing force of the Bretton Woods system, would become the new basis of world economic integration. The remarkable success of their program would have a lasting impact on the tone and inflection of American liberalism in the late twentieth century.
