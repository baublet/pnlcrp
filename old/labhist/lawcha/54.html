Spaces of Work and Struggle in the Southwest: From the New Deal to the Neoliberal State

Lori Flores1, Jonathan Cortez2, Maria Quintana3, Alina Mendez4, Jeannette Estruth5
1Stony Brook University, 2Brown University, 3Yale University, 4University of California San Diego, 5New York University

Abstract

This panel examines the interconnections between labor and racialized space in a larger context of capitalism and neoliberalism. Living in barrios, labor camps, or on the margins of the state, communities of color have experienced and endured the disciplining power of racialized space that often isolates and marginalizes them. Organizing within these spaces, working class communities have fought the socioeconomic and legal structures that force them into livelihoods characterized by low wages and poor living and working conditions. The social movements that have emerged from place-based struggles have operated at varying scales and with diverse definitions of community and belonging. Migrant communities, for instance, have drawn clear links between the conditions that push them out of their homes and the structures that keep them impoverished in the United States. Some Mexican Americans have drawn the boundaries of community around citizenship, making clear how legal status organizes social space. The papers in this session examine how working class groups have leveraged their social membership in the communities where they reside to fight for such things as better housing, higher wages, and environmental justice. Together they elucidate the changing relationship between workers and the state, pointing to the varying ways racialized space defines the terms of this relationship and what communities of color have done to remake theses spaces.
