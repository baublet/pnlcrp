Art and Work in the 1930s: Archives and Public Histories

Kate Sampsell-Willmann1, Conor M. Casey2, Patricia Raynor3, Gregg Narber4
1Tacoma Community College, 2University of Washington, 3National Postal Museum, Smithsonian Institution, 4Independent Scholar

Abstract

In What Michael Denning identifies as the beginning of the “era of the CIO,” roughly 1935 to 1955, the United States experienced the high water mark of governmental support for the arts. Notwithstanding LBJ’s Great Society, an extension of many New Deal inspired programs, the 1930s stand out for their cultural democracy, art of, by, and for the people. Of course, public history itself is another kind of democratic investment in the arts. In this panel, Patricia Raynor form the US Postal Museum will discuss and examine New Deal murals in the Pacific Northwest as public art aimed at putting artists to work but also for the purposes of making art available to all citizens, not just wealthy patrons in the cities. Conor Casey will discuss his role as the labor archivist for the University of Washington and recently digitized collections featuring arts and labor, especially the work of Richard V. (Dick) Correll (1904–1990) who was “one of the leading masters of printmaking in the West.” Digitizing makes these resources available to all. Kate Sampsell-Willmann will mine the archives at the Library of Congress Farm Security Administration photographs for a images that show “The Long Arm of the Job,” where less iconic but just as compelling images can reveal about work as a social value untethered from labor for wages.
