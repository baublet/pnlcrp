Cultural Work: Performance and Working Class Consciousness

Eric Kaufman1, Lisa Milner2, Cathy Brigden3, Daniel Gilbert4, Lisa Phillips5
1The Ohio State University, 2Southern Cross University, 3RMIT University, Melbourne, Australia, 4University of Illinois, 5Indiana State University

Abstract

□ There's Gotta Be Something Better Than This": Representation and Consciousness of Labor and the Working Class in American Musical Theatre, 1945-1975 As a relatively elite stage form, “proletarianized” through development of wide-release film versions, musical theatre occupies an odd space in the spectrum of artistic production, straddling the boundary between “high art” and “popular art.” Workers are sometimes colorful background or fetishized, exoticized and sentimentalized objects of bourgeois gaze; in other instances, they are more authentic reflections of working-class values. Popular forms of music, dance, fashion and language, often with working-class roots, have been integral to musical theatre form and structure. Being both a product of and a producer of popular culture, musical theatre offers a window into the role of working-class representations and culture in the broader artistic, commercial and political culture of our society.

If the images, sounds, forms and values of workers are to be represented in art, whether as specifically working-class expressions or as appropriation for a dominant-class audience, workers have a right of ownership to those representations and an interest—a class interest—in attempting to interpret and understand them. That understanding is dependent upon an aggressive insistence that artistic works that depict working people answer to a working-class critique.

□ Workers’ Struggles on Stage: Australian Radical Theatre This paper considers connections within international left cultural activity by introducing the New Theatre in Australia, which has seen very little academic attention. Taking inspiration from the New Theatre of the USA and the Unity Theatre in the UK, the New Theatre employed a novel mix of conventional theatre forms, experimental performative styles and communist theories of ‘art as a weapon’ in their presentation of left-wing theatre within a society that was largely resistant to their broader political ideals. Their work and organisation were avowedly working class, and support from the Communist Party of Australia and left-wing unions was vital. Drawing from primary research and oral history interviews, the paper highlights their struggle for industrial justice in a marriage of culture and union activism in Australia. To illustrate this intersection, a 1952 performance of The Candy Store, written by an American activist, Bernard Rubin, is discussed. What makes the performance of this play notable is the location and audience: 1500 feet down a mineshaft to coal miners staging a stay-in strike.

□ Representing Teachers: Labor and Liberalism in Bel Kaufman’s Up the Down Staircase: This paper situates the explosion of teachers' labor militancy within a broader cultural conversation about public educators’ role in building the so-called Great Society. The paper centers on the work of Bel Kaufman, who drew on her experience as a New York City teacher in her 1965 novel Up the Down Staircase, which formed the basis for the 1967 Hollywood film of the same name. Kaufman became a prominent ally of teachers unions around the country from the mid-1960s forward, and her book articulated experiences and perspectives that resonated with millions of other teachers. The paper draws on new archival research in Kaufman’s papers, exploring the ways in which her novel became a touchstone for broader public reckoning with the workplace politics of public education.
