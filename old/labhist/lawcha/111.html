Organising Community: Hotel and Restaurant Unionisation and Community-based Radicalism in 1930s Minneapolis

Emmet Gillespie
University of Sydney

Abstract

This paper examines intersections between community struggles and unionism by focusing on the attempts of the Hotel and Restaurant Employees Union (HERE) to organise in Minneapolis in the late 1930s and early 1940s. HERE Local 665 organised on an industrial basis the miscellaneous workers who held low-status jobs within the hotel and restaurant industry, such as maids, kitchen and laundry staff and cafeteria workers, positions that were mostly held by women and African-Americans in a famously white city. Unionists spread 665 both through tactics of salting within the workplace, and through a nexus of communities that had been built up by radicals and ethnic groups throughout the 1930s. 665 would then contribute back to these broader struggles, using its position on the Farmer-Labor Association and the AFL Central Labor Body of Minneapolis to press for left policy and support the nascent Civil Rights movement. In examining the history of 665, I will bring out the often complex relationships between race, gender, community and union organisation in 1930s Minneapolis.

This paper is part of a broader project focusing on the development of third-party labor politics in Minnesota. The Minnesota Farmer-Labor Party held office from 1930-38, and is thus widely regarded as the most successful third party in US history. My work seeks to reconceptualise the relationship between the party and broader radical movements in Minnesota at that time. I argue that it was this connection with outside radical communities that provided a base for a working-class political organisation. This chapter, on 665, will provide a case study of this relationship. By focusing on a service workers union, dominated by women and African-Americans, I seek to broaden the image of working-class people who participated in radical politics. While labor historians have lately put considerable focus on service work, the image of the Farmer-Labor Party remains resolutely traditional; the (white male) producerist manufacturing worker to complement the (white male) producerist farmer. This paper seeks to contribute both to a new conception of working-class politics, and to uncover potentialities for connections between local community activism, labor, and political organisation.
