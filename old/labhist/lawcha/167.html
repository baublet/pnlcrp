Intersecting Empires: Accumulation, Labor, and Possibilities of Solidarity in the Pacific World

Hiroaki Matsusaka1, Toulouse-Antonin Roy2, Maryam Aziz1, JoAnna Poblete3
1University of Michigan, 2UCLA, 3Claremont Graduate University

Abstract

Informed by the recent scholarship on transpacific history (Fujitani 2013; Lowe 2015), this panel examines allegiances across the colonial periphery of the twentieth-century Pacific world, with a focus on the intersections between United States and East Asian history, as well as labor history and the history of empire. With the industrialization of the Japanese and U.S. empires at the turn of the twentieth century, rural and urban populations experienced new, uneven forms of expropriation, to which they responded with innovative modes of solidarity and protest. Our papers will address the ways in which a variety of workers responded to the violent wave of enclosures and colonialism that swept up the populations living between the U.S and Japanese Empires. First, reexamining the Marxian concept, Toulouse-Antonin Roy discusses how the process of “primitive accumulation” by the Japanese empire smashed the indigenous populations and prevented a possible workers’ collaboration in colonial Taiwan. Second, Hiroaki Matsusaka analyzes how Japanese proletarian writer Maedakō Hiroichirō, a former migrant worker in the U.S., described race and gender relations of transpacific migrant workers in a ship headed from Yokohama to Seattle in the 1920s. Third, Maryam Aziz examines the labor of Black soldiers, veterans, and activists who were stationed in the Pacific or practiced East Asian martial arts between World War II and the Vietnam War. As such, we will attempt to clarify the “scales of struggles” in the Pacific world, exploring the diversity of working-class responses to imperial domination and capitalist accumulation.
