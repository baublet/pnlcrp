Tyler Babbie

Abstract

Mapping The Crisis: Du Bois and the Small Scale

Display/Screening

In its early days in the nineteen-teens, the NAACP’s monthly magazine, The Crisis, was a multimedia, polygeneric publication containing long-form reporting, editorials, photography, fiction, poetry, and other kinds of content. One component of W.E.B. Du Bois’ journal was a monthly column that aggregated news clippings and accounts of smaller-scale events. Titled first “Along the Color Line” and later “The Horizon,” the miscellany contains events under headings ranging from “Education” to “Crime,” from “The Ghetto” to “Social Uplift,” from “Music and Arts” to “The War.” Most entries are tagged with a location or several locations, and the dates of the issues tag them in time as well.

I propose displaying or screening (whichever applies best) a digital humanities project that I have developed to engage with the miscellany. Developed through a fellowship from the University of Washington’s Simpson Center for the Humanities, and in collaboration with UW’s Mapping Social Movements website, this project, titled “A Year on the Horizon,” took each of the hundreds of entries per month and projected them on a map. After creating a database of more than a thousand entries from “The Horizon,” I used mapping software to create an interactive visualization of each month’s entries covering a year from summer 1916 to summer 1917 (a range that will be expanded in further drafts).

“A Year on the Horizon” is a micro-scale, qualitative complement to the quantitative history of the NAACP that Mapping American Social Movements already holds. The grand scale of the rise of the NAACP described by maps and charts that count the rise in membership and the increase in the number of branches provide mutual context for one year of the everyday life of the community it represented. “The Horizon” vividly illustrates collective and individual triumphs and tragedies. Reading the entries feels like opening a window to another time—but the more one reads, the more it feels like a mirror, reflecting 21st Century social justice issues.

In the January 1916 issue of The Crisis, Du Bois explained his process for selecting cuttings for “The Horizon” and other sections of the paper: “To preserve that balance of judgment and foresight on the one hand which will make the magazine popular, and on the other hand make it a real force for education and uplift—this is no easy task and calls for unusual ability and clairvoyance if it is well done.” (135). He ends this editorial by reminding his readers “remember that this is not a mere magazine—it is a magazine with a purpose.” “A Year on the Horizon” attempts to honor the ability and clairvoyance of Du Bois and his collaborators by making it easier to access, read, and sort through the history contained in “The Horizon.”

The maps are not currently up for display, though we hope to have them go live on Mapping Social Movements soon.
