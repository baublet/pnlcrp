Rethinking Carceral Labor Struggles: Unions, Job Training, and Parole for Incarcerated Workers in the Twentieth Century United States

Melanie Newport1, Amanda Hughett2, Morgan Shahan3, Dan Berger4
1University of Connecticut-Hartford, 2American Bar Foundation, 3Johns Hopkins University, 4University of Washington

Abstract

While many studies have considered the carceral state as a job site for both inmates and corrections officers, this panel goes beyond conventional paradigms to consider the many ways in which struggles over access to work, identities as workers, and labor relations shaped prison labor programs and litigation in the twentieth century. This panel shows that labor was central to claims on prisoner rights and to workers’ return to society after incarceration. Amanda Hughett’s paper concerning the 1970s alliance between the American Civil Liberties Union National Prison Project and the North Carolina Prisoners Labor Union reveals the limits of litigation in addressing prison labor and prisoner rights’ issues writ large. In an examination of a worker retraining program in Cook County Jail, Melanie Newport addresses how vocational education offered misdemeanant offenders new lives as workers after their incarceration while rationalizing the increased incarceration of ever larger numbers of poor people in 1970s Chicago. Finally, Morgan Shahan explores the development of parole policy and practice in early 20th century Illinois, revealing how employers ensured a steady supply of cheap labor for bosses willing to commit to aiding parolees in their quest to “make good.” Together, these papers show the range of struggles incarcerated people engaged in while behind bars and upon their release.

Melanie Newport is an Assistant Professor of US history at the University of Connecticut—Hartford. She is currently working on a book, Cook County Jail: Racism, Violence and the Dangerous History of Jail Reform. Looking to one of America’s largest jails, she explores how contests over reform, human rights, and race came to constitute everyday experiences of state violence among marginalized people in American cities from the 1950s to the present. Newport is also interested in histories of money bail; the focus of her second research project is how criminal courts, reformers, and bail bondsmen developed legal technologies to set the price of freedom for people accused of crimes in the modern US. She holds a BA from Pacific Lutheran University, an MA from the University of Utah , and a PhD from Temple University. Newport’s research has been supported by the Black Metropolis Research Consortium, the University of Chicago, and the Center for the Humanities at Temple.

Morgan Shahan is a PhD candidate in 20th Century U.S. history at Johns Hopkins University. As a social and cultural historian, she is broadly interested in the intersection of social movements, race and racism, and the carceral state. Her dissertation, tentatively entitled “Managing Deviancy: Parole, Probation, and Carceral Development, 1895-1939,” concerns the development and evolution of probation and parole laws and policy from the Progressive Era to World War II. With this work, she hopes to trace the creation and illustrate the implications of a new legal, social, and political category that lay between institutionalization and the enjoyment of ex-prisoners’ ability (however circumscribed) to navigate free society unsupervised. Raised in the San Francisco Bay Area, she holds a B.A. in History from the University of California at Berkeley and an M.A. from Johns Hopkins. Her research has been supported by the Illinois State Historical Society and Johns Hopkins University.

Dan Berger is Assistant Professor of History at University of Washington-Bothell. He is an interdisciplinary historian focusing on critical race theory, twentieth century U.S. social movements, and critical prison studies. He is the author of Captive Nation: Black Prison Organizing in the Civil Rights Era, which won the 2015 James A. Rawley Prize from the Organization of American Historians, The Struggle Within: Prisons, Political Prisoners, and Mass Movements in the United States, and editor of The Hidden 1970s: Histories of Radicalism. Rutgers University Press, 2010.
