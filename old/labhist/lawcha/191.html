A View From the Top: International Capital and Organized Labor

Andrew Elrod1, Jeff Schuhrke2, Leon Fink3, Rebecca Fowler4
1University of California, Santa Barbara, 2University of Illinois at Chicago, 3University of Illinois-Chicago, 4Washington State University

Abstract

□ “Badly Need[ed]…Capital”: Organized Labor, Trade Policy, and Visions of Globalization at the end of Bretton Woods Amid the historic fracturing of the American political system during Nixon's second term, it was business leaders and administration officials who embarked on an ambitious journey to open investment and trade markets abroad, including in the Communist countries. The Trade Act of 1974 was instrumental in this strategy. The attachment of American union leaders to an anti-Communist foreign policy kept them looking backwards, opposed to international collective bargaining in Europe, bereft of solutions to the problems of global economic integration. Their inability to bargain over employer investment decisions and the historic weakness of American full-employment policies left protectionism as one of the only tools to maintain domestic employment. For these reasons labor found itself opposed to détente, confronting an administration portraying investment regulations—capital controls—as not only illiberal diplomacy, but as restraints on domestic economic growth and full employment.In the minds of C. Fred Bergsten, Pete Peterson, and other cabinet officials including George Shultz, Herbert Stein, and William Simon, capital mobility, the destabilizing force of the Bretton Woods system, would become the new basis of world economic integration. The remarkable success of their program would have a lasting impact on the tone and inflection of American liberalism in the late twentieth century.

□ U.S. Labor’s Modernizing Mission: Exporting Industrial Pluralism in the “Development Decade” This essay considers the relationship between U.S. labor and international development in the 1960s. It centers the AFL-CIO’s Cold War foreign activities on modernization theory and ties to the U.S. Agency for International Development, instead of on covert ops and ties to the CIA, as other histories have done. Labor and industrial relations were central components of America’s overseas modernization project in the 1960s. “Industrial pluralism”—the political and legal paradigm that defined U.S. labor relations from the end of World War II to the 1970s, which emphasized class collaboration, productivity, and limited state intervention—was one of the defining features of what industrial relations scholars, labor leaders, and liberal policymakers in the 1960s considered to be “modern.” They aimed to export this paradigm to the global South in the name of development and anti-Communism.

□ From Laborlore to the Labor Bureaucracy and an Ideology of Pragmatism The folklorist Archie Green has argued that studying laborlore--a broad range of culturally expressive practices in trade unions, work traditions, practices, could help create a more inclusive pedagogy and history, built from the bottom up, rooted in class and class conflict in the service of a broad democratic politics. One way laborlore can do this is to investigate the culture practices of labor officials. This presentation will explore songs, stories, and jokes to help understand how laborlore can prop up a union bureaucracy as well support as democratic politics.

□ Undocumented Capital: U.S. [Im]mobilization, Containment, and Exploitation of Migrant Labor: shows how U.S. policies of citizenship, immigrant policing, and detention and deportation rely on the spatial logistics of containment to render invisible undocumented persons while shoring up the global neoliberal capital project that relies on a tractable, disposable labor force.
