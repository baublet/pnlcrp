Historical Perspectives on the 2016 Election: Working-Class Political Culture in the U.S.

Robyn Muncy1, Matthew Garcia2, Sarah Coleman3, Tula Connell4, Nancy MacLean5
1University of Maryland, 2Arizona State University, 3Southern Methodist University, 4Independent Historian, 5Duke University

Abstract

The complicated and at times contradictory interplay of class, race and immigration in the 2016 U.S. elections and the accompanying permutation of both national political parties offers a unique opportunity for historical analysis. This session will contextualize these issues as they shaped the 2016 campaign, analyze the factors underlying their emergence and reassess our understanding of class, race and immigration in recent American history.

Racism and extremism were among the more visible manifestations of the 2016 elections. Kevin Boyle will explore the elements underlying support for Donald Trump and draw lessons on how his campaign may provide an opportunity to rethink presumptions of race, politics and public life. Robyn Muncy will analyze the use of the term “working class” by presidential candidates and mass media reporting on the presidential contest and will set current uses of the phrase “working class” in the context of the term in U.S. political culture since the 1930s.

While U.S. labor’s active role in the 2016 elections represented a continuation of its long involvement in national politics, Matthew Garcia will examine the cost and benefits of partisan political action in a labor movement context through a case study of the United Farmworkers of America (UFW) in the 1976 national elections. Garcia also will explore lessons learned by Mexican/Latino civil and labor rights groups from the UFW’s emergence as a national political force. Sarah Coleman furthers the discussion with a focus on the influence of anti-immigrant sentiment on liberal policy-making through her examination of the role California’s middle-class white voters played in pushing the Clinton administration toward an embrace of triangulation in immigration policy formation.

Comments will be offered by Tula Connell, labor writer and historian, author of Conservative Counterrevolution: Challenging Liberalism in 1950s Milwaukee.
