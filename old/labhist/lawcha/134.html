Obreros y Estudiantes, Unidos y Adelante! Local and Transnational Student-Worker Solidarities in Uruguay, 1929-1958

Megan Strom
UC San Diego

Abstract

During the Early Cold War years (broadly defined here as the decades of the 1930s, 40s, and 50s), Uruguayan university students were deeply engaged with the broader social, economic, and political concerns of domestic and international affairs. Students in the Federation of Uruguayan University Students (FEUU) at the University of the Republic were so deeply connected to these causes that they made the commitment to improving society part of the organization’s official platform. While initially imagining this process as an extension of the University into the community and the working class populations, the FEUU quickly realized that students also had much to learn from workers. This approach laid the groundwork for developing a strong relationship between students an workers that would be tested and contested by the state during the 1930s, 40s, and 50s. Throughout this era, in the years leading up to the more tumultuous 1960s, students from the FEUU attended labor union meetings, supported worker strikes, and participated in marches as outspoken advocates for unions and workers’ rights, both at home and abroad. Reciprocally, various union leaders became important allies in the FEUU’s campaign for University reforms, regularly attending student meetings and marches, while also offering strategic advice. This mutually beneficial relationship was often cited at demonstrations with banners that read, “Workers and Students, United and Forward.” As a result of these interactions, students drew connections between the poor labor conditions of their compatriots and the inadequacies of the capitalist system that were lobbied as critiques of Uruguay’s national politics and a growing resistance to the global Cold War binary. Thus, the relationship between workers and students in Uruguay pushed many university students to think beyond the local circumstances, situating themselves and the struggles of workers in a larger, interconnected transnational framework.
