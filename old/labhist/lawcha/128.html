The Fraternal Tradition and the Global History of the Knights of Labor

Steven Parfitt
University of Nottingham

Abstract

The history of international labour movements during the nineteenth century is generally told in a certain way. Socialists, anarchists and trade unionists from different countries came together in a variety of networks, movements and organisations to win strikes, regulate migration, and agitate for political change across national borders. The First and the Second Internationals were the largest and best-known of these movements.

Has this narrative obscured other trends and traditions in that story? This paper will draw on my research and work into the global history of the Knights of Labor, and on Robert Weirâ€™s groundbreaking work concerning the fraternal side of the Knights, to think about whether we as historians have missed or underplayed the importance of fraternal culture in our understanding of international labour history. Fraternal orders were, after all, among the largest organisations that catered to a working-class audience. The United Order of Oddfellows, the Ancient Order of Foresters and other orders recruited working-class members across the world and provided them with the social insurance that states did not yet provide. Movements like the Knights of Labor were also organised on fraternal lines, and secret, fraternal organisations were present at the formation of the First International and other major global movements. There is an entire tradition here that needs much greater examination.

This paper has contemporary relevance as well. Many commentators have pointed to a decline in solidarity at the workplace and in the community as both a cause and effect of the decline in trade union membership. Fraternal culture, with its encouragement of brotherhood (or sisterhood) through shared ritual, once fulfilled this function. While there is very little chance of a revival of fraternal culture right now, we should consider other ways of building solidarity that do not rely only on economic self-interest.
