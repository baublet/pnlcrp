Teaching Labor’s Story: Using Curated Documents to Infuse Labor and Working-Class History into US History Classrooms

Nikki Mandell1, Randi Storch2, Adam Mertz3, Bob Bussel4, Rachel Reinhard5
1University of Wisconsin-Whitewater, 2SUNY Cortland, 3University of Illinois at Chicago, 4University of Oregon, 5Director, History-Social Science Project, University of California, Berkeley

Abstract

An effort begun by LAWCHA’s Teacher/Public Sector Initiative, Teaching Labor’s Story aims to collect primary sources that capture the struggles and sentiments of workers in each era of US history to help infuse labor and working-class history into US history classrooms. LAWCHA members provide both excerpts of these primary sources along with guides that provide the necessary background information—such as historical context, “sourcing” the source, defining key terms, and even some potential discussion questions—that help teachers and students utilize these documents. This panel will explain the development of Teaching Labor’s Story, provide existing source excerpts and guides, and invite LAWCHA members to provide and solicit additional contributions. Further, the committee distributed existing primary sources and guides to both practicing and pre-service teachers, asking for feedback on the current form of Teaching Labor’s Story materials. The panel will share the findings of this feedback. The Teaching Labor’s Story initiative aspires to make labor and working-class history more accessible to students and teachers, demonstrating just how central it is to all eras of US history, because, like race and gender, class is everywhere.
