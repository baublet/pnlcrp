The Strange Career of Industrial Relations: Labor Research and Social Change Postwar America

Toby Higbie1, Ronald Schatz2, Grace Davie3
1University of California, Los Angeles, 2Wesleyan University, 3CUNY

Abstract

After World War II, research expertise on working conditions, collective bargaining, and labor markets became strategic assets for academics, governments, and social movements. Industrial Relations scholars consolidated their authority as impartial interpreters of class conflict through their institutional positions within major universities, and extended their influence to new areas of social change. At the same time, research expertise became more diffuse as access to higher education widened. Trade unions and community activists increasingly turned to systematic research to support their campaigns and generate popular support for their causes. Each paper on this panel provides a case study of this process.
