Fraser Ottanelli1, Rachel Batch2, Robert Zecker3, John Enyeart4
1University of South Florida, 2Widener University, 3Saint Francis Xavier University, 4Bucknell University

Abstract

“Forgetting Fascism in the 1940s: Class, Political Visions, and South Slavic Americans”

During the 1940s, the rise of anti-Communism caused the narrowing of political imaginations in the United States that, among other outcomes, rendered South Slavic American radicalism incomprehensible to most of the larger public. Our panel seeks to recover the dynamism of that radicalism, which most South Slavic Americans referred to as antifascism, by explaining why we forgot about it. Too often scholars of the 1930s and 1940s present antifascism as an epiphenomenon of Communist activism. But for South Slavic Americans antifascism became their politics as it linked their homelands to their host land. Throughout the Depression years being an antifascist meant championing immigrant and African American rights, industrial democracy, and supporting leftists abroad. By 1943, Yugoslav Communist Guerrilla leader Josip Broz Tito epitomized the Allied struggle against the Axis forces as his polyglot Partisan army repeatedly won skirmishes with the Nazis. Conservative South Slavs, whether recent arrivals from Croatia who had supported the fascists, aliens and ethnics who lamented the fall of the Yugoslav monarchy, or those who long cherished Catholic anti-Communism used the growing anti-Communist mood to claim their own Americanness and vilify their rivals within their communities. Instead of viewing leftist South Slav efforts as an attempt to, in activist Louis Adamic’s word, “operationalize” Franklin Roosevelt’s four freedoms, the larger American public considered them foreign agents carrying out a Soviet agenda. These papers proposed by Rachel Batch, Robert Zecker, and John Enyeart reveal a clearer view of how antifascism functioned, and how internal ethnic conflicts forged anticommunism. From Herbert Gutman to Lizabeth Cohen and Michael Denning we have been instructed that immigrants made and remade the working class and American identity. But sometimes waves of immigrants joined with their fellow ethnics to obscure the legacies of their political rivals and undermine their social democratic contributions. For conservative South Slavs forgetting fascism was a project aimed at destroying both the politics of their leftist countrymen and denying their identities. Batch and Enyeart both use the well publicized trial of Croatian Archbishop Aloysius Stepinac as one example of the anticommunists’ project to forget fascism. Stepinac supported the Croatian fascist regime that took power after Axis forces invaded Yugoslavia. After the war, Tito arrested Stepinac as a war criminal and American Catholics presented this as a clear case of Communist amorality.
Zecker and Batch further advance this point by illustrating how anti-Tito Croatian immigrants, who likely had fascist affiliations during the war, succeeded in Americanizing by presenting Tito’s Yugoslavia within established tropes of Communist totalitarianism.
