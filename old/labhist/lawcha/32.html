Creating a City to Resist the State: The Seattle General Strike of 1919

Kathy Ferguson
University of Hawai'i

Abstract

The Seattle general strike of 1919 was a remarkable moment in the process that Eugene Holland characterizes in Nomad Citizenship as a slow-motion general strike. The Seattle general strike rose out of the immanent historical practices of Seattle’s labor communities as they interrupted state and capitalist formations and instead organized to govern themselves. Defying the conservative national leadership of their own unions as well as Seattle’s bombastic governmental and economic elites, these workers organized themselves into a viable alternative structure for making and carrying out decisions necessary to running the city. The writings of the strike’s historian, Anna Louise Strong, and of the “two-card men” whose dual union membership combined the fight to improve existing conditions with the larger struggle for a better world, both reflect and create a constructive dissidence that still resonates today. The Seattle general strike ended on February 11. Yet perhaps it did not. I suggest that Seattle’19 can still be available to us as an example of Eugene Holland’s idea of affirmative nomadology, an opportunity to distill principles and practices for organizing ourselves outside the confines of capital and the state. Holland encourages us to identify assemblages of resonating processes that serve as relays for material and political operations. Seattle’19, I argue, is a resonating moment within Holland’s imagining of “a long-term process of becoming-general of the general strike.” (p. 156)
