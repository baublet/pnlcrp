Labour in the Global Carnival Against Capital

Verity Burgmann
Monash University

Abstract

Though not the first of such events, the ‘Battle of Seattle’ late in 1999 was the seen as the coming out party for the anti-corporate movement. Where was labour in this important new movement? Did unions participate in blockading the citadels of corporate global power? Evidence from case-studies of four of the ‘summit-hopping’ episodes (Seattle November-December 1999, Melbourne September 2000, Quebec City April 2001 and Genoa July 2001) suggests strong working-class involvement, especially of white-collar workers from the public sector, and important contributions from union activists and particular radical unions as organizations, white-collar and blue-collar, public and private. However, in some cases trade union officialdom at higher levels preferred union contingents keep a safe distance from centres of action. Such reticence did not present the union movement in the best light to anti-corporate activists; and it revealed significant tensions within unions between militant, class-conscious activists and more co-opted and conservative officials.
