The Second Line of Defense: American Women Workers and World War I

Lynn Dumenil
Occidental College

Abstract

“WOMEN OF NATION ARE READY TO DON OVERALLS AND REPLACE MEN IN VARIOUS OCCUPATIONS,” screamed a headline in the Grand Rapids Press (Michigan) on May 9, 1917. The enthusiastic reporter breathlessly proclaimed that as the men left for war “women will start work as elevator operators, elevated, subway and street car conductors, machine shop experts, ticket agents, motion picture mechanics and distributors, telegraphers, railway car cleaners, office clerks, bell ‘boys,’ chauffeurs, automobile mechanics, shoemakers, bank clerks, farm hands, railway checkers and callers, waiters in exclusive cafes, train dispatchers, gatemen and theater musicians.”
The Grand Rapid Press was right. World War I saw an unprecedented challenge to the sex-segregated labor market that so limited women's work options. The phenomenon was much commented upon. As women took on formerly men's jobs, newspapers and magazines avidly reported on their incursion, often printing photographs that featured women dressed in overalls or mannish uniforms that underscored the ways in which they were transgressing gender boundaries. Woman suffrage activists seized on the idea of these workers as "the second line of defense" to bolster their arguments about women's rights to full citizenship. Women labor leaders and reformers, especially those associated with the Woman's Trade Union League, used the breakdown of barriers in the workplace to call for equal pay for equal work and to articulate a demand for what we now call "comparable worth." Yet despite what seemed to be a dramatic transformation, World War I did not significantly undermine patterns of sex-segregated labor. Ironically, perhaps the most enduring result of the war was the expansion of feminized jobs in the professional and clerical field. That such “women’s work” marked the war’s impact suggests that there were built-in limits to possibilities for women to challenge sex segregation. Even as they broke barriers, traditional expectations on the part of male employers and workers, as well as women reformers and government administrators, who mobilized to protect women war workers, constrained opportunities both during and after the war. This paper explores both the promises that war seemingly offered women workers and the powerful limits to realizing substantive progress in the struggle for a more equal workplace.
