Evolution of Labor-Community Coalitions: the Institutionalization of the AFL-CIO’s role in Social Movement

Jon Hiatt1, Ana Avendaño2, Nik T Theodore3, Pablo Alvarado4
1AFL-CIO, 2United Way Worldwide, 3University of Illinois at Chicago, 4National Day Laborer Organizing Network

Abstract

From the 1955 merger of the AFL and the CIO, the development of mutually valuable, enduring partnerships between the country’s major labor federation and community allies has been elusive. Especially in recent decades, they have typically been charitable in nature – whereby labor mobilized support to local communities in the face of natural disasters (hurricanes, floods) or basic need shortages (food banks, book fairs); or where designed to provide support to the unions, they have been highly transactional – soliciting one-time assistance from the community in the context of a pending labor dispute (a strike, a city hall rally, legislative lobbying), generally with little effort to build or nurture an ongoing relationship that transcends the particular crisis in question. For the most part, these charitable and/or transactional relationships have failed to strengthen either partner: they have neither led to lasting gains for unions, nor have they sustained, much less added to the effectiveness of, movements seeking lasting social, racial, or economic justice.

Even the Federation’s occasional engagement in broad, issue-based coalitions (e.g. around trade, labor law reform) and the relationships it has maintained with other progressive national organizations (e.g. NAACP, LCCR), have too infrequently resulted in sustained alliances marshaling their combined power to influence societal change.

Over the past decade, beginning with the AFL-CIO’s worker center partnerships, continuing with its prominent role in the community-labor CLEAN car wash worker campaign in Los Angeles, and taking root most recently in its restructured, “impact”-based national partnership with United Way, the Federation has sought to transform its community partnerships to ones that more effectively blend its institutional assets with the social movements for lasting change that its community allies are promoting.

The moderator, who will lead the discussion, and the other panel members, each of whom has played an active role in the transformation of the AFL-CIO’s relationships with community allies, will address the nature and the significance of these changing trends, and the potential that this has to offer much greater strength and effectiveness to labor-community coalitions.
