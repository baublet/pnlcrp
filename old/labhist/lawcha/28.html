“Hard Times at the Cooker:” The Struggle to Save Chicago’s Public Hospital

Amy Zanoni
Rutgers University

Abstract

My paper explores the impact that disinvestment and privatization had on staff and patients at Cook County Hospital during the late 1970s and early 1980s, and the movement they formed in response. In the 1970s, Chicago’s economy, like that of the nation’s, had collapsed while medical costs skyrocketed. Facing pressure from the state, Cook County enacted a series of austerity policies. Officials laid off nurses’ aides and clerical workers and reduced the number of hospital beds. When the County contracted out the hospital’s management to Hyatt Medical Management Enterprises, Inc. in 1980, the subsidiary of the Hyatt Hotel Corporation sent collections agencies after patients. Deteriorating working conditions and health care delivery moved the hospital community to action. Medical professionals, hospital workers, and community members mobilized their unions and formed the Committee to Save Cook County Hospital to defend the public institution that was integral to the livelihoods and health of many.
